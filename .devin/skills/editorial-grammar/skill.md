# EDITORIAL GRAMMAR

## Cut Types and Their Meaning

Every cut is a statement about time, space, and relationship. Choosing a cut carelessly is choosing a meaning carelessly.

### Straight Cut
The most common cut. Shot A ends, Shot B begins immediately. No transition effect.
- **Same sequence, continuous time**: The default. Audience reads as: same time, same place (or adjacent).
- **Across space in same time**: Parallel editing. Two things happening simultaneously.
- **Time jump** (same location, changed detail): Audience reads a time passage if context supports it. Requires visual cue of change.

### Dissolve
The outgoing shot fades down while the incoming shot fades up, overlapping briefly.
- **Meaning**: Change in time, change in location, or both. "We are now somewhere/somewhen else."
- **Slow dissolve**: Large time jump, or dreamlike quality
- **Fast dissolve**: Smaller time jump, narrative connection between before and after

### Dip to Black (Fade to Black / Up from Black)
The image fades to black before the next shot fades up from black.
- **Meaning**: Hard separation. A chapter has ended. The next chapter begins. Equivalent to a section break in prose.
- **Between two shots with no rest**: Creates impact — "that happened, now this happens, and the distance between them is darkness."

### Match Cut
A straight cut where the outgoing and incoming shots share a compositional element — an action, a shape, a movement, a sound — that creates visual continuity across a spatial or temporal gap.
- **Match on action**: Character reaches for a door → cut → hand on a different door, different time/place
- **Shape match**: Round porthole → cut → round planet exterior → cut → round data display
- **In ON THE EDGE**: The star being unmade → cut → a character's eye → cut → data display. The Entity rhymes across scales.

### Jump Cut
A cut between two shots of the same subject from nearly the same angle, with a gap in time — creating a visible, jarring discontinuity.
- **Unintentional**: A mistake. Signals insufficient coverage.
- **Intentional**: Psychological fracture, instability, time going wrong. The Nouvelle Vague signature. In horror, it signals that time is no longer reliable.

### L-Cut and J-Cut
See @acoustic-design for full detail. Briefly:
- **L-Cut**: Audio from outgoing shot continues into incoming shot's video. Forward momentum, naturalism.
- **J-Cut**: Audio from incoming shot begins before its video arrives. Anticipation, dread.

## The Kuleshov Effect

Lev Kuleshov's foundational experiment (Soviet montage, 1910s): A neutral, expressionless close-up of a man's face was intercut with three different images — a bowl of soup, a child in a coffin, a woman on a divan. Audiences saw three different emotions in the man's face: hunger, grief, desire. The face was identical in all three. The meaning was entirely in the juxtaposition. `[Frierson:EditingTheory p.149]` (Soviet Montage chapter; Frierson invokes the term in cross-reference at `p.58`: "see the 'Kuleshov effect' in Chapter 5, p. 149")

**The principle**: A shot means what it means IN CONTEXT. The shot before it and the shot after it determine what the audience reads in a given image.

**The tool**: Place a neutral human face immediately after an image. The face "reacts" to whatever the previous image was, even if the face was shot with no expression and no context.

## Pacing: The Rhythm of Meaning

Pacing is not speed. It is contrast. A fast sequence means nothing without a slow one preceding it. A slow, deliberate shot is unbearable tension if the film has been fast.

### Shot Length and Psychological Time

**Long takes** (10+ seconds): The audience has time to think. Suspense builds because the audience is ahead of the character. Used for dread — the character hasn't noticed yet. The audience has.

**Short cuts** (1-3 seconds): The audience doesn't have time to think. Energy, chaos, fragmentation. Used for action and psychological breakdown. Paradoxically, fast cutting makes the audience feel LESS, not more — they can't hold any single image long enough to feel it.

**The horror rule**: Build slowly (long takes → mounting dread) → release with fast cutting (chaos, loss of control) → then force the audience to hold on a single long take in the aftermath (the weight of what happened). The long take after horror is where the feeling actually lands.

### Pacing Map for ON THE EDGE

| Stage | Average Shot Length | Cut Rhythm | Transition Type |
|-------|--------------------|-----------|--------------------|
| Stage One | 8-12 seconds | Even, deliberate | Straight cuts, occasional dissolve for time passage |
| Stage Two | 5-8 seconds | Slightly uneven — some shots longer than expected | Straight cuts with L-cuts/J-cuts creeping in |
| Stage Three | 1-4 seconds intercutting / 15-30 second holds alternating | Chaotic rhythm — no predictable pattern | Jump cuts at fracture points, dip to black at catastrophes |
| Climax | 1-2 seconds maximum | Rapid | Match cuts break down into jump cuts |
| Resolution/Aftermath | 30 seconds+ | Single long take | Fade to black |

## Three-Stage Edit Process

### Assemble Edit
Place all shots in program order with rough in/out points. Rough, unfinished. The purpose is to feel the pacing. Don't stop to perfect. Move through the whole film before fixing any single moment.

### Rough Cut
Fix pacing, cut timing, technical mistakes. Add elements that clarify story. Preview to director and trusted eyes. Not a finished product — a legible draft.

### Fine Cut
All problems solved. Transitions added. Music, graphics, SFX integrated. Sound mixed. Color balanced. This is the film.

## Transition Grammar Reference

| Transition | When to Use | When NOT to Use |
|------------|------------|-----------------|
| Straight cut | Same sequence; continuous action; parallel edit | Across large time gaps without context |
| Dissolve | Time passage; location change; memory; dream | Between shots that are continuous in time |
| Dip to black | Major chapter break; aftermath; emphasis | Between shots that should feel connected |
| Jump cut | Intentional time instability; psychological fracture | As cover for insufficient coverage |
| Match cut | Elegant time/space transitions; thematic rhyming | As a gimmick without thematic payoff |
| Digital wipes/spins/flips | Training videos; light comedy | Never in dramatic/horror work |

**The invisible-editor rule**: Gimmick transitions — digital flips, page turns, star wipes — announce the editor's presence. The editor's job is to be invisible. Straight cuts, dissolves, and dips to black are the grammar. Everything else is noise. `[general-knowledge]` (a near-universal practitioner consensus; the version of this principle anchored in our corpus is Murch's Criterion 1–2 from `[Frierson:EditingTheory p.112-113]`: emotion and story override technical flourish)

## Split Edit Strategy for ON THE EDGE

The film should use split edits (L-cuts and J-cuts) deliberately and architecturally:

**L-cuts**: Dialogue from one scene flows into the establishing shot of the next. Continuity of thought across space changes. The crew's conversation doesn't stop just because we've moved to another room.

**J-cuts**: Hearing a sound — the Entity's frequency, an alarm, a scream, an inexplicable mechanical sound — before cutting to the source. The lag between hearing and seeing is the grammar of dread.

**Stage One**: Predominantly L-cuts — the world flows naturally forward.
**Stage Two**: L-cuts and J-cuts mixed — the world is still flowing but sometimes the future bleeds into the present.
**Stage Three**: Predominantly J-cuts, increasing delay — the future arrives before the present has resolved. Time has broken its own rules.

## DECISION PROTOCOL

### When to Invoke This Skill

This skill is invoked after shot list and coverage decisions (`@staging-blocking`) are confirmed. In post-production, invoke before any cut is made. Editorial decisions are downstream of production — you cannot fix a coverage problem by editing.

### How to Decide Whether to Cut

Sources: `[Frierson:EditingTheory p.112-113]` (canonical body summary of Murch); `[external-source: Murch, In the Blink of an Eye 1995/2001]` (original; not in local corpus).

Apply Murch's Six Criteria as **ordered priorities**. A cut that satisfies criteria 1–2 is correct even if it violates 3–6. Frierson `p.113`: "don't give up eye-trace before planarity, and don't give up planarity before spatial continuity."

```
1. EMOTION — Is this cut true to the emotion of the moment?
     → NO: Do not cut. Full stop. No other criterion overrides this.

2. STORY — Does this cut advance the story?
     → NO: Delay is not neutral. A cut that delays story without emotional payoff is wrong.

3. RHYTHM — Is the cut rhythmically right?
     → Rhythm serves emotion; it never overrides it.

4. EYE-TRACE — Does the cut acknowledge where the audience is already looking?

5. PLANARITY (2D continuity / 180° line) — Does screen direction and the stage line hold?
     → Lowest substantive technical criterion. Violate if 1 demands it.

6. SPATIAL CONTINUITY (3D space) — Does the geography hold?
     → Always the last priority. Can be broken intentionally.
```

`[Frierson:EditingTheory p.112-113]`. Frierson explicitly notes that Murch's hierarchy aligns with Dmytryk's Rule 6: "Cut for Proper Values Rather Than for Proper 'Matches.'" `[Frierson:EditingTheory p.112]`

**On the often-quoted percentage weighting** ("Emotion 51% / Story 23% / Rhythm 10% / Eye-trace 7% / 2D 5% / 3D 4%"): this attribution is widely circulated online and in lectures but does **not** appear in the locally-extracted corpus. Treat as `[external-source: Murch, In the Blink of an Eye — not in local corpus]` until verified directly against the original. The ordered list above is the in-corpus canonical form.

### How to Choose Cut Type

Sources: `[Frierson:EditingTheory p.86-92]` (Hollywood Theorists chapter; canonical body summary of Dmytryk's Seven Rules); `[external-source: Dmytryk, On Film Editing 1984]` — flagged `[pdf-needs-ocr]` because the local PDF is 100% scanned image-only.

```
→ Movement carries the story forward → CUT ON ACTION
  (cut during the motion, not before or after; the eye follows movement, not the frame change)

→ Visual or thematic similarity bridges two moments → MATCH CUT

→ Intentional disorientation, psychological fracture → JUMP CUT
  (always motivated; never used to cover insufficient coverage)

→ Two simultaneous actions → CROSS-CUT (parallel editing)

→ Audience needs space, context, or a beat → CUTAWAY / HOLD
  (the long-take-as-duration argument is `[external-source: Tarkovsky, Sculpting in Time 1986]`; not in local corpus, but the underlying idea — that holding a shot creates emotional weight — maps to Murch's Criterion 1 emotion-over-rhythm at `[Frierson:EditingTheory p.112]`)
```

### How to Choose Transition

See Transition Grammar Reference table above. Abbreviated decision rule:

```
→ Default (95% of all edits) → STRAIGHT CUT
→ Chapter/act break → DIP TO BLACK
→ Time/space passage with emotional continuity → DISSOLVE
→ Audio leads or trails the picture → L-CUT or J-CUT
→ Everything else → do not use
```
