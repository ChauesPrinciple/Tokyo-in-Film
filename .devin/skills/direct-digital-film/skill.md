## PART I: VISUAL GRAMMAR — THE SHOT VOCABULARY

### Shot Size Hierarchy (Camera-to-Subject Distance)

**Extreme Long Shot (ELS) / Establishing Shot**
- Function: Scale, isolation, geography, world-building
- Subject occupies <10% of frame height
- Reads as: insignificance, vulnerability, cosmic indifference
- Use: Opening images, act transitions, moments of realization about scale
- On the Edge application: Ship against the anomaly; stars reversing in deep field

**Long Shot (LS) / Wide Shot (WS)**
- Function: Character in environment, spatial relationships, blocking visibility
- Subject occupies 30-50% of frame height (full body visible, head to toe)
- Reads as: context, agency within constraints, physical capability
- Use: Action sequences, entrances/exits, group dynamics
- On the Edge application: Crew on bridge together; Marshall's isolation orders creating physical distance

**Medium Shot (MS)**
- Function: Dialogue, relationship, social interaction
- Subject occupies 50-70% of frame height (waist up)
- Reads as: conversation, negotiation, interpersonal tension
- Use: Two-shots, over-the-shoulder dialogue, collaborative work
- On the Edge application: Reyes and Marshall's final conversation; Costa and Tanaka's confrontation

**Close-Up (CU)**
- Function: Emotion, reaction, psychological state
- Subject occupies 70-90% of frame height (shoulders up, full face visible)
- Reads as: interiority, decision-making, emotional processing
- Use: Reaction shots, moments of realization, emotional peaks
- On the Edge application: Reyes closing the files; Marshall reading his timeline

**Extreme Close-Up (ECU)**
- Function: Fragmentation, psychological breakdown, obsessive focus
- Subject occupies >90% of frame height (eyes only, hands only, mouth only)
- Reads as: dissociation, fixation, loss of wholeness
- Use: Horror beats, moments of consumption, loss of self
- On the Edge application: Tanaka's hand writing HELP; Carter's destroyed eyes; Costa's mouth forming predictions

### Compositional Grammar

**The Cut-Off Rule (Preventing Compositional Violence)**
- Never cut at joints: no cuts at neck, waist, knees, ankles, wrists, elbows
- Safe cuts: mid-thigh, mid-torso, mid-forearm, mid-shin
- Violation reads as: amputation, violence, carelessness
- Exception: Intentional fragmentation (ECU of hands = acceptable because hands are the subject)

**Lead Room (Space in Direction of Motion/Gaze)**
- Subject looking/moving right → more space on right side of frame
- Subject looking/moving left → more space on left side of frame
- Ratio: 60/40 or 70/30 (more space in direction of attention)
- Violation reads as: claustrophobia, no future, trapped
- On the Edge application: As crew is consumed, reduce lead room progressively — they have no forward motion left

**Headroom (Space Above Subject's Head)**
- Standard: 10% of frame height above head in MS/CU
- Too much headroom: subject diminished, "floating head," compositional error
- Too little headroom: claustrophobic, oppressive
- Violation for effect: Reduce headroom as psychological pressure increases

**The Rule of Thirds**
- Divide frame into 9 equal sections (3×3 grid)
- Place subject's eyes on upper third line
- Place horizon on upper or lower third line (never center unless symmetry is the point)
- Intersection points = power positions for subject placement
- Centered composition reads as: formal, symmetrical, artificial, confrontational

## PART II: OPTICS AS PSYCHOLOGY

### Focal Length on 1/3" CCD Sensor (Standard DV/HDV Camera)

**Wide Angle (4mm–6.5mm equivalent)**
- Effect: Exaggerates depth, distorts edges, expands space
- Subject in context: environment dominates, subject is part of world
- Psychological read: Vulnerability, exposure, distortion under pressure
- Distortion increases closer to lens — faces warp, spaces stretch
- Use: Establishing shots, moments of disorientation, environmental dominance
- On the Edge application: The bridge in early scenes — functional, expansive, under control

**Normal (6.5mm–13mm equivalent, roughly 35mm–50mm in 35mm film terms)**
- Effect: Approximates human eye perspective, minimal distortion
- Balanced relationship between subject and environment
- Psychological read: Neutral, observational, documentary realism
- Use: Dialogue, standard coverage, moments of stability
- On the Edge application: Stage One — the crew is still operating normally

**Telephoto (13mm+ equivalent, 85mm+ in 35mm film terms)**
- Effect: Compresses distance, flattens depth, isolates subject from background
- Background goes soft (shallow depth of field at wider apertures)
- Psychological read: Surveillance, isolation, intimacy through distance, alien observation
- Use: CU that feels watched, moments of paranoia, the Entity's POV
- On the Edge application: The Entity should only exist in telephoto — compressed, distant, flattened, wrong

### Depth of Field (DOF) as Narrative Tool

**Shallow DOF (wide aperture, f/1.4–f/2.8)**
- Subject sharp, background/foreground soft
- Isolates subject from environment
- Reads as: subjective POV, focus, interiority, dream state
- Use: Moments of obsession, tunnel vision, psychological narrowing
- On the Edge application: Reyes staring at her equations — background crew goes soft

**Deep DOF (narrow aperture, f/8–f/16)**
- Everything in focus, foreground to background
- Subject exists in legible spatial relationship to world
- Reads as: objective POV, surveillance, everything visible, no escape
- Use: Establishing shots, moments of clarity, the Entity observing
- On the Edge application: The bridge in Stage Three — everything visible, nothing hidden, no safety in blur

## PART III: LIGHT AS EMOTIONAL ARCHITECTURE

### Three-Point Lighting (The Baseline System)

`[DigitalFilmmaking p.73]` (Hughes — verbatim: "Back light (2) (Example: 300 foot candles) Fill light (1) (Example: 150 foot candles) Key light (2) (Example: 300 foot candles)"; "The intensity of the lights is a 2 to 1 ratio"). See also `@lighting-architecture` for full verification chain.

**Key Light (Primary Source)**
- Intensity: 300 foot-candles (baseline for proper exposure) `[DigitalFilmmaking p.73]`
- Position: 30–45° off camera axis, 30–45° above subject
- Function: Defines form, creates shadows, establishes mood
- Hard key (undiffused) = dramatic, high-contrast
- Soft key (diffused through silk/softbox) = naturalistic, gentle

**Fill Light (Shadow Control)**
- Intensity: 150 foot-candles (half of key, baseline)
- Position: Opposite side of camera from key, at camera height
- Function: Softens shadows created by key, controls contrast ratio
- Diffused always — never hard fill

**Back Light / Rim Light (Separation)**
- Intensity: Equal to key light (300 foot-candles)
- Position: Behind subject, 45° above, aimed at hair/shoulders
- Function: Separates subject from background, creates depth, adds dimension
- Creates rim of light on edges — "halo effect"

### Key-to-Fill Ratio (The Emotional Dial)

**2:1 Ratio (Key at 300 fc, Fill at 150 fc)**
- Reads as: Natural, daytime, neutral, broadcast standard
- Soft shadows, gentle contrast
- Use: Ordinary world, stability, normalcy
- On the Edge application: Stage One — the mission is routine

**4:1 Ratio (Key at 300 fc, Fill at 75 fc)**
- Reads as: Dramatic, cinematic, emotional weight
- Defined shadows, clear contrast between light/dark sides of face
- Use: Conflict, tension, decision-making
- On the Edge application: Stage Two — amplification begins

**8:1 Ratio or Higher (Key at 300 fc, Fill at 37.5 fc or lower)**
- Reads as: Horror, noir, psychological breakdown
- Deep shadows, half the face lost to darkness
- Use: Moments of consumption, terror, loss of self
- On the Edge application: Tanaka outputting equations — one side of his face in shadow

**No Fill (Key Only)**
- Reads as: Silhouette, mystery, the unknowable
- Subject backlit, face in shadow, outline visible only
- Use: The Entity, moments of total otherness
- On the Edge application: Kuchisake-onna in KURASAWA; the anomaly through the viewport in ON THE EDGE

### Practical Lighting Techniques

**Motivated Lighting (Light Sources Justified by Environment)**
- Ship interior = overhead fluorescents, console glow, viewport starlight
- Every light in frame should have a visible or implied source
- Unmotivated light reads as artificial, theatrical — breaks immersion unless intentional

**Color Temperature & Gels**
- Tungsten (3200K) = warm, orange, intimate, analog
- Daylight (5600K) = cool, blue, clinical, digital
- Fluorescent (4100K) = green-tinged, institutional, sterile
- Gels shift temperature: CTO (Color Temperature Orange) warms, CTB (Color Temperature Blue) cools
- On the Edge application: Ship interior = cool fluorescent (institutional); anomaly glow = no color temperature (it's not light, it's wrongness)

**Practical Lights in Frame**
- Desk lamps, console screens, overhead fixtures visible in shot
- These become part of the composition, not just illumination
- Can serve as key, fill, or back depending on placement
- On the Edge application: Console glow as key light on faces in darkened bridge

## PART IV: ACOUSTIC ARCHITECTURE

### Microphone Types & Polar Patterns

**Shotgun Microphone (Hypercardioid Pattern)**
- Picks up sound in narrow cone in front, rejects off-axis sound
- Mounted on boom pole, aimed at subject from above or below frame
- Use: Controlled environments, dialogue, ADR reference
- Weakness: Requires skilled operator, picks up handling noise if not isolated
- On the Edge application: All dialogue recording — ship interior is controlled environment

**Lavalier Microphone (Omnidirectional Pattern)**
- Small clip-on mic, hidden on talent (under collar, in hair)
- Picks up sound equally from all directions (subject's voice + environment)
- Use: Backup for boom, moving subjects, wide shots where boom can't reach
- Weakness: Clothing rustle, less directional control, visible if not hidden well
- On the Edge application: Backup only — ship environment is quiet enough for boom to dominate

**Boundary Microphone (PZM - Pressure Zone Microphone)**
- Flat mic placed on hard surface (table, wall, floor)
- Picks up sound reflecting off surface — creates "zone" of coverage
- Use: Room tone, ambient sound, group scenes around table
- On the Edge application: Mess hall scene — captures group dynamic without visible boom

### Boom Placement Strategy

**Above Frame (Preferred)**
- Boom enters from top of frame, mic aimed down at 45° angle
- Keeps mic out of shot, natural sound perspective (we hear from above, like we see)
- Operator holds boom pole overhead, aims at speaking subject's mouth
- Requires 10–12 foot ceilings for comfort

**Below Frame (Tight Shots)**
- Boom enters from bottom of frame, mic aimed up at subject
- Use when headroom is tight (CU, ECU) or ceiling is low
- Sound perspective slightly unnatural (we don't hear from below) but acceptable
- On the Edge application: CU of Reyes at console — boom from below to stay out of shot

**Boom Discipline**
- Operator wears headphones, monitors levels in real-time
- Follows speaking subject, anticipates who speaks next
- Stays just outside frame line — if you see the mic, the shot is ruined
- Marks floor positions for repeatable placement across takes

### Room Tone (The Acoustic Fingerprint)

`[FilmCheckingContent p.92-93]` (Sharman: "Hold for room tone!" — 60-second protocol); `[DigitalFilmmaking p.99]` (Hughes: "before leaving a setting... the sound technician should request a room tone recording"). See also `@acoustic-design` for full verification chain.

**What It Is**
- 30–60 seconds of "silence" recorded at each location
- Captures ambient sound: HVAC hum, electrical buzz, distant traffic, air pressure
- Every location has a unique acoustic signature — room tone is that signature

**Why It's Mandatory**
- Post-production filler: between dialogue edits, between ADR lines, under music
- Without room tone: unnatural silence, "dead air," audible cuts between takes
- With room tone: seamless acoustic space, living environment

**How to Record**
- After final take at each location, call "Room tone, rolling"
- Everyone freezes, no movement, no talking, 60 seconds
- Record at same levels as dialogue
- Label file clearly: "Bridge_RoomTone_Take1"

**On the Edge Application**
- Bridge room tone: console hum, air recycler whir, distant mechanical sounds
- Mess hall room tone: quieter, less mechanical, more "dead" (smaller space)
- Medical bay room tone: sterile, fluorescent buzz, no mechanical hum
- Each location = distinct acoustic world

### Editorial Audio Grammar

**L-Cut (Audio Leads Video)**
- Outgoing scene's audio continues into incoming scene's video
- We hear the next scene before we see it
- Reads as: anticipation, inevitability, the world continuing
- Use: Transitions where the next scene is already happening (we just arrive late)
- On the Edge application: Hear Tanaka's voice outputting equations before we cut to him in the signal array room

**J-Cut (Video Leads Audio)**
- Incoming scene's audio bleeds into outgoing scene's video
- We hear what's coming before it arrives
- Reads as: dread, foreshadowing, the future intruding on the present
- Use: Horror transitions, moments of premonition
- On the Edge application: Hear the static from the anomaly under the final shot of Stage One, before we cut to Stage Two

**Sound Bridge**
- Audio element (music, ambient sound, specific noise) carries across multiple shots
- Creates continuity, unifies disparate images
- Use: Montages, time passage, thematic connection
- On the Edge application: The hum of the ship as constant under all interior scenes — when it cuts out, we know something is wrong

## PART V: STAGING & BLOCKING — SPATIAL GRAMMAR

### The 180° Line (The Axis of Action)

**What It Is**
- Imaginary line between two subjects in a scene
- Camera must stay on one side of the line for entire scene
- Crossing the line reverses screen direction, breaks spatial continuity

**Why It Matters**
- Maintains consistent screen direction: Subject A always on left, Subject B always on right
- Audience builds mental map of space — crossing the line destroys that map
- Violation reads as: disorientation, spatial confusion, "something is wrong"

**Legal Ways to Cross the Line**
1. **Dolly move during shot**: Camera physically crosses line while rolling — audience sees the crossing happen
2. **Cutaway to neutral shot**: Cut to object/environment with no clear screen direction, then re-establish on other side of line
3. **Subject crosses line**: Character walks across line, camera follows — new line established

**Intentional Violation (Controlled Disorientation)**
- Cross the line invisibly (hard cut) to create spatial wrongness
- Audience feels disoriented without knowing why
- Use: Psychological breakdown, reality fracturing, loss of spatial coherence
- On the Edge application: As Marshall's control deteriorates, begin crossing the line in his scenes — space stops making sense

### Coverage Strategy (Shooting for the Edit)

**The Master Shot**
- Wide shot covering entire scene from one angle
- Runs from "Action" to "Cut" — full scene, no breaks
- Function: Safety, spatial reference, pacing option in edit
- Always shoot the master first — it's your insurance policy

**Story Shots (The Core Coverage)**
- MS two-shots: both subjects in frame, relationship visible
- OTS (Over-the-Shoulder): Subject A in foreground (back of head/shoulder), Subject B in background (face visible)
- Reverse OTS: Same scene, opposite angle — now Subject B in foreground, Subject A in background
- Singles: CU of each subject, isolated
- Function: Dialogue, reaction, emotional beats

**Cut-Ins (Detail Shots)**
- CU or ECU of object, hands, props, specific detail
- Same axis as master — maintains screen direction
- Function: Emphasis, information, pacing control
- On the Edge application: CU of Tanaka's hand writing HELP; ECU of console screen showing equations

**Cut-Aways (Reaction Shots, Environment)**
- Shot of something outside the main action — other character's reaction, environment detail, object
- Different axis from master — doesn't need to match screen direction
- Function: Time compression, parallel action, editorial flexibility
- On the Edge application: Cut away to viewport showing anomaly while dialogue continues

**Re-Establishing Shot**
- Return to wide shot after sequence of CUs
- Re-grounds audience in space
- Function: Spatial reset, act break, transition
- On the Edge application: After intense CU sequence of Reyes at console, re-establish bridge to show Marshall watching her

### Blocking Notation (Spike Marks & T-Marks)

**Spike Marks (Actor Positions)**
- Small tape marks on floor showing where actor should stand/stop
- Color-coded by character (red for Character A, blue for Character B)
- Allows repeatable performance across multiple takes
- Invisible to camera (placed just outside frame line or under furniture)

**T-Marks (Camera Positions)**
- T-shaped tape mark showing where camera tripod legs should be placed
- Ensures consistent framing across takes
- Labeled (T1, T2, T3) corresponding to shot list

**On the Edge Application**
- Bridge has fixed console positions — actors return to same marks for spatial continuity
- As crew deteriorates, actors drift off marks — spatial breakdown visible in frame

## PART VI: EDITORIAL GRAMMAR — THE CUT VOCABULARY

### Cut Types & Their Meanings

**Straight Cut (Hard Cut)**
- Instant transition from Shot A to Shot B
- Same sequence, same time, continuous action
- Reads as: normal progression, real-time, causality
- Use: 95% of all cuts in narrative film

**Dissolve (Cross-Fade)**
- Shot A fades out as Shot B fades in, brief overlap
- Reads as: time passage, location change, dream state, memory
- Duration: 24–48 frames (1–2 seconds)
- Use: Act breaks, time jumps, subjective transitions
- On the Edge application: Dissolve from Stage One to Stage Two — time has passed, the infection has begun

**Dip to Black (Fade Out/Fade In)**
- Shot A fades to black, hold black, Shot B fades in from black
- Reads as: hard separation, chapter break, significant time passage
- Duration: 48–96 frames (2–4 seconds)
- Use: Act breaks, major time jumps, "the end"
- On the Edge application: End of Stage Two — dip to black before Stage Three begins

**Wipe**
- Shot B "wipes" across frame, replacing Shot A
- Reads as: stylized, artificial, genre-specific (Star Wars)
- Use: Rarely — only if style supports it
- On the Edge application: Do not use — breaks realism

**Match Cut (Graphic Match)**
- Cut from object in Shot A to similar object in Shot B
- Shape/position matches across cut — eye follows continuity
- Reads as: connection, parallel, thematic link
- Use: Transitions with meaning, visual rhyme
- On the Edge application: Cut from Reyes's coffee cup (empty) to Marshall's coffee cup (also empty) — shared exhaustion

**Jump Cut**
- Cut within same shot, same angle, subject in same position — time jumps forward
- Reads as: time compression, disorientation, editorial aggression
- Use: Montages, moments of dissociation, stylistic choice
- On the Edge application: Tanaka writing — jump cut through pages filling — time compressing as he loses himself

### The Kuleshov Effect (Meaning Through Juxtaposition)

**The Principle**
- Shot A (face) + Shot B (object) = audience infers emotion based on object
- Same face + bowl of soup = hunger
- Same face + dead body = grief
- Same face + attractive person = desire
- The cut creates the meaning — the face is neutral

**Application**
- Reaction shots gain meaning from what precedes them
- CU of character's face after seeing the anomaly = fear (even if actor's face is neutral)
- CU of character's face after seeing equations = obsession
- On the Edge application: Reyes's face is neutral — cut from equations to her face = obsession; cut from Marshall to her face = concern

## PART VII: PRODUCTION HIERARCHY & SET PROTOCOL

### The Command Chain (Set Rhythm)

**Pre-Roll Sequence**
1. **1st AD**: "Lock it up!" — set goes silent, no movement
2. **1st AD**: "Quiet on set!" — reinforcement, final warning
3. **1st AD**: "Camera ready?" — DOP confirms camera is framed and ready
4. **DOP**: "Camera ready."
5. **1st AD**: "Sound ready?" — Sound mixer confirms levels are good
6. **Sound Mixer**: "Sound ready." or "Speed." (tape is rolling)
7. **1st AD**: "Roll camera."
8. **Camera Operator**: "Rolling." (camera is recording)
9. **2nd AC**: Slate — claps sticks, states scene/take number
10. **DOP**: "Framed." (slate is out of frame, shot is composed)
11. **Director**: "Action."

**Post-Roll Sequence**
1. **Director**: "Cut."
2. **1st AD**: "Cut!" (reinforces, ensures everyone heard)
3. **Director**: "Check the gate." (film term, now means "check the take")
4. **DOP**: Reviews take, confirms it's good
5. **1st AD**: "Moving on." or "Going again."

**Why This Exists**
- Controlled chaos — 20+ people, expensive equipment, limited time
- The hierarchy is the only thing preventing disaster
- Everyone knows their role, their cue, their responsibility

### Key Roles & Responsibilities

**Director**
- Creative vision, performance, story
- Calls "Action" and "Cut"
- Final say on take quality
- Works with actors on performance

**Director of Photography (DOP / Cinematographer)**
- Visual design: shot composition, lighting design, camera movement
- Does not physically place lights — designs the lighting, gaffer executes
- Works with camera operator (or operates camera themselves on small productions)

**1st Assistant Director (1st AD)**
- Director's operational voice
- Runs the set, manages schedule, keeps production on time
- Calls the command chain (everything except "Action" and "Cut")
- Enforces safety, manages crowd control

**Gaffer (Chief Lighting Technician)**
- Executes DOP's lighting design
- Manages electrical distribution, places lights, sets intensity/color
- Commands the lighting crew (electrics)

**Key Grip**
- Manages all non-electrical equipment: flags, diffusion, camera support (dolly, crane, steadicam)
- Works with gaffer to shape light (flags, nets, silks)
- Commands the grip crew

**Script Supervisor (Continuity)**
- Film's spatial and temporal memory
- Tracks what was shot, what's left, continuity errors (props, costumes, positions)
- Notes screen direction, eyelines, actor positions for matching across takes

**Sound Mixer**
- Records production sound, monitors levels, manages boom operator
- Responsible for clean dialogue, room tone, ambient sound
- Calls "Sound ready" or "Speed"

**2nd Assistant Camera (2nd AC)**
- Operates the slate (clapperboard)
- Loads film (on film productions) or manages media cards (digital)
- Keeps camera reports (metadata for each take)

## PART VIII: ON THE EDGE — PRODUCTION NEURAL NETWORK

**Dr. David Carter (The Observer)**

- Consumption: ECU of destroyed eyes, 8:1 key-to-fill, hard key — self-inflicted

### The Entity — Visual Protocol

**Never in Close-Up**
- Exists only in background, at edge of telephoto frames
- Never fills the frame
- Never receives focus — always soft, always distant
- Silhouetted against viewport, backlit, no detail visible

**Lighting the Entity**
- No key light — it is not a subject
- Rim light only, if anything — outline against stars
- The anomaly glows, but the glow has no color temperature — it's not light, it's wrongness

**Sound Design for the Entity**
- No sound of its own — it uses existing sounds (Hashimoto's voice, static, the ship's hum)
- When it's present, room tone shifts — frequencies drop out, the ship sounds wrong
- The Entity is heard in what's missing, not what's added

## PART IX: NEURAL NETWORK CONSTRUCTION — SELF-TRAINING PROTOCOL

### Knowledge Domains (The Clusters)

**Domain 1: Visual Grammar**
- Input: Shot size, composition, framing rules
- Output: Psychological read, narrative function, application strategy
- Training: Analyze 50 films across genres, catalog every shot size choice and its effect

**Domain 2: Optics**
- Input: Focal length, depth of field, lens distortion
- Output: Mood, perspective, subject-environment relationship
- Training: Shoot same scene at 5 different focal lengths, document psychological shift

**Domain 3: Lighting**
- Input: Key-to-fill ratio, color temperature, motivated vs. unmotivated sources
- Output: Emotional tone, genre signaling, spatial depth
- Training: Light same subject at 2:1, 4:1, 8:1 ratios, document emotional read

**Domain 4: Sound**
- Input: Mic type, placement, room tone, editorial grammar (L-cut, J-cut)

### Training Exercises (Building the Network)

**Exercise 1: The Shot Size Ladder**
- Shoot same subject at ELS, LS, MS, CU, ECU
- Same lighting, same focal length, only distance changes
- Document psychological shift at each size
- Repeat with different subjects (person, object, environment)

**Exercise 2: The Focal Length Test**
- Shoot same MS at 6mm, 10mm, 16mm, 25mm, 50mm equivalent
- Maintain same framing (subject occupies same % of frame)
- Document distortion, depth compression, background relationship
- Identify psychological read at each focal length

**Exercise 3: The Lighting Ratio Progression**
- Light same subject at 1:1, 2:1, 4:1, 8:1, 16:1 key-to-fill ratios
- Same shot size, same focal length, only lighting changes
- Document emotional tone at each ratio
- Identify genre associations (1:1 = sitcom, 8:1 = horror)

**Exercise 4: The 180° Line Violation**
- Shoot simple dialogue scene maintaining 180° line
- Shoot same scene crossing line with dolly move (legal)
- Shoot same scene crossing line with hard cut (illegal)
- Show all three versions to test audience, document disorientation

**Exercise 5: The Kuleshov Test**
- Shoot neutral face (actor instructed to show no emotion)
- Shoot 5 objects (bowl of soup, dead animal, attractive person, weapon, child's toy)
- Edit face + object in sequence, test audience
- Document inferred emotions — prove the cut creates meaning

**Exercise 6: The Room Tone Necessity**
- Record dialogue scene without room tone
- Record same scene with room tone
- Edit both versions, attempt seamless cuts
- Version without room tone will have audible gaps — document the failure

**Exercise 7: The Coverage Strategy**
- Shoot scene with full coverage (master, MS two-shot, OTS both ways, singles, cut-ins, cut-aways)
- Edit scene using only master (no cuts)
- Edit scene using full coverage
- Document editorial flexibility gained by coverage
