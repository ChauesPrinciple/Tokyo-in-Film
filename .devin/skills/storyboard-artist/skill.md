# STORYBOARD ARTIST

Sources: `[Filmmaking:MakingAFilm p.145-151]` — Chapter 9 "Storyboarding Your Movie" (basics/benefits `p.146`; script breakdown `p.148`; framing panels and angles `p.149-151`); `[JCam:InstructorGuide p.17]` (glossary: "A visual outline of a film or animation"); `[JCam:InstructorGuide p.47-51]` (Cameron / Terminator storyboard workflow).

## The Storyboard's Purpose

A storyboard is not art. It is a communication tool. It tells the entire production team — director, DOP, gaffer, grips, actors, editors — what each shot looks like before a single light is placed or camera rolled. Time spent storyboarding saves three times that time on set.

A storyboard answers: What is in the frame? How is the camera positioned? What movement happens? In what order do shots occur?

## Script Breakdown (Beat Identification)

Source: `[Filmmaking:MakingAFilm p.148]` ("Breaking down your script" — TOC at `p.11`; body treatment at `p.148`). The step-by-step beat-numbering protocol below is `[agent-synthesis]` extending that foundational step.

Before drawing a single frame, break the script into beats.

**A beat** = a single unit of story action. Not a scene — scenes contain multiple beats. Not a line — a beat may span several lines, or occur with no dialogue at all.

A beat is: a moment that changes something. The character's emotional state changes. The power dynamic shifts. New information arrives. A decision is made or avoided.

### Beat Identification Process
1. Read the scene without stopping
2. Re-read and mark each moment where something changes — with a vertical line in the margin
3. Each marked section = one beat
4. Each beat = potentially one or more shots
5. Group beats by geography (same camera setup can serve multiple beats)

**Story point focus**: Every beat must serve the narrative. If you cannot explain in one sentence what the beat accomplishes for the story, the beat (and its shots) may be expendable.

## The Six Core Shots

Sources: `[Filmmaking:MakingAFilm p.149-151]` ("Deciding What to Include in Each Panel"; shot sizes and angles); `[DigitalFilmmaking p.134]` (Hughes simple sequence: establishing → story shots → cut-ins). Individual shot-size names (ES/WS/LS/MS/CU/ECU) are `[general-knowledge]` standard cinematography vocabulary; the six-category taxonomy is `[agent-synthesis]`.

Every storyboard draws from six fundamental shot types, applied according to narrative function:

| Shot | Frame Content | Narrative Function |
|------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Establishing** | Sweeping wide, entire setting visible | "Here is where we are." Opens scenes, establishes geography, shows scale |
| **Wide** | Less sweeping than establishing, characters visible at distance | Re-orients viewer mid-scene, shows spatial relationship of characters |
| **Long** | Character head-to-toe | Shows character in space, physical action, entrance/exit |
| **Medium** | Waist to head | Conversation, emotional legibility, hands + face visible |
| **Close-up** | Head and shoulders | Primary identification shot, empathy, emotion |
| **Extreme close-up** | Single feature | Detail, tension, obsession, revelation — and horror |

Every storyboard sequence begins with an establishing or wide shot to orient the audience, then moves into the story shots (long, medium) and climbs to close-up or ECU for the emotional peak.

## Camera Angles

Angle communicates power and psychological state:

| Angle | Description | Effect |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| **Eye level** | Camera at subject's eye height | Neutral, equal, standard — the audience is with the character |
| **High angle** | Camera above, looking down | Subject appears smaller, vulnerable, surveilled, diminished |
| **Low angle** | Camera below, looking up | Subject appears larger, powerful, threatening, monumental |
| **Dutch angle (canted)** | Camera tilted on the roll axis | Unease, psychological instability, wrongness, disorientation |

**In ON THE EDGE**:
- Eye level: Stage One default
- High angle: Characters under the weight of what they're facing (Stage Two)
- Low angle: The Entity, when we must show it — the only thing that gets a low angle
- Dutch angle: Stage Three — every scene, increasingly severe tilt

## Rule of Thirds as Narrative Tool

**Centered composition**: Stability, symmetry, equal power, natural order. Used at the opening to show a crew that functions as a unit. Also used for oppressive confinement: a character TRAPPED at center of frame, no place to go.

**Off-center composition (rule of thirds)**: Dynamic, unresolved, in motion, in conflict. Power imbalance between subjects. Used throughout Stage Two — no conversation is balanced anymore.

**The principle**: Position characters in the frame to underscore their relationship subconsciously. Two characters at equal power = one at left third, one at right third. Character losing power = pushed toward edge of frame, less space. Character gaining dominance = centered or moving toward center.

## Hero Identification Shots

The audience bonds with a character through specific shots. These must appear early and be maintained:

**Close-up**: Creates intimacy and empathy. The audience is inside this character's experience. The first CU of each crew member in ON THE EDGE establishes who the audience is watching. `[Filmmaking:MakingAFilm p.149-151]` (CU as identification/empathy shot).

**Point of View (POV)**: The audience sees exactly what the character sees. `[Tomlinson:PlotBasics text00008]` (subjective POV principle: "stories must be told subjectively, from the point of view of the hero, so that we can identify with them and have access to their emotions"). Camera vocabulary for executing POV is `[general-knowledge]`. Two types:
- **Direct POV**: Camera placed exactly at character's eye level and position. The frame IS the character's eyes.
- **Over-the-shoulder (OTS)**: Character at edge of frame, occupying approximately 1/3 of the frame, with the subject of their attention in the remaining 2/3.

**POV Motivation Rule (critical)**: ALWAYS show the character looking — toward something, at something, off-screen — BEFORE cutting to the POV shot. The motivation shot (character looking) → POV shot (what they see) → reaction shot (character's response). Without the motivation shot, the POV is spatial confusion. With it, the audience is inside the character's experience.

Sequence: [Character looks off-screen right] → CUT → [What they see, from their position] → CUT → [Character reacts]

## 180° Rule Application in Storyboards

The 180° line must be drawn on the floor plan BEFORE thumbnailing begins. Every panel must be consistent with which side of the line the camera is on.

**Marking the line on thumbnails**: In the thumbnail, draw a small arrow indicating the camera's facing direction relative to the 180° line. Panels on the same side are consistent. Any panel that requires a line crossing must be flagged and accompanied by a note explaining the legal crossing method.

**Intentional breaking in storyboards**: Mark intentional line-break panels with a red border or a notation "INTENTIONAL 180° VIOLATION — spatial breakdown". This ensures the decision is intentional and communicates to the director and editor what the sequence is doing.

## Thumbnail Process

Thumbnails are rough, fast sketches on simple rectangular panels. They are NOT finished storyboard frames. They are thinking-on-paper.

**Materials**: Simple panel template (rectangles in rows), pencil or marker, no eraser — mistakes are part of the process.

**Drawing rule**: Capture pose, angle, and composition. Use stick figures if necessary. The COMPOSITION matters. The art does not.

**Copy-paste technique**: For sequences where the camera stays on the same subject across multiple beats (e.g., a character walking down a corridor), draw the first frame, then duplicate it and change only what changes. Storyboards are not animation — held camera positions can reuse the same base drawing.

## Camera Move Notation

| Move | Definition | Thumbnail Notation |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Pan** | Camera body static, rotates horizontally | Horizontal arrows sweeping across panel from start to end position |
| **Tilt** | Camera body static, rotates vertically | Vertical arrows showing up or down direction |
| **Zoom in/out** | Lens changes focal length, camera static | Arrows pointing inward (zoom in) or outward (zoom out) with start/end frame marks |
| **Track/Truck** | Camera physically moves horizontally | Arrows beneath panel indicating left/right movement |
| **Dolly** | Camera physically moves toward/away from subject | Arrows beneath panel indicating push-in or pull-out |
| **Boom/Jib** | Camera physically moves vertically | Arrows indicating up/down crane movement |

For ON THE EDGE: Most camera moves in Stage One should be static or gentle pan/tilt. The ship is stable; the camera is stable. In Stage Three, introduce unmotivated camera movement — slight handheld shake beginning in Stage Two that increases to disorienting in Stage Three. A stable world has a stable camera; a breaking world has a breaking camera.

## Animatic Workflow

The animatic transforms storyboard panels into a timed, sequential video draft of the film before a single frame is shot.

**Process**:
1. Storyboard panels are finalized and locked
2. Panels are scanned or photographed and imported into editing software
3. Director times each panel — "this shot is 4 seconds, this one is 8" — matching to audio
4. Dialogue/scratch audio track is added if available
5. The animatic is played back: this IS the film in rough visual form
6. Adjust timing based on what works
7. Lock the animatic as the production reference

**Distribution**: The locked animatic goes to every department head. Animators (if any) match their work to animatic timing. Background artists build environments that match animatic frames. The editor uses the animatic as the guide for final assembly.

**For ON THE EDGE**: An animatic, even a rough one using sketched thumbnails, would allow the director to time every scene, identify pacing problems, and discover coverage gaps before shooting day — when they are cheap to fix, not after.
