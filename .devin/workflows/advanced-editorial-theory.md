# ADVANCED EDITORIAL THEORY WORKFLOW

## Academic Frameworks for Editing as Meaning-Making

Based on *Film and Video Editing Theory: How Editing Creates Meaning* by Michael Frierson. This workflow applies formal editing theory to practical scene analysis and construction.

---

## PHASE 1: ZETTL'S SCREEN SPACE & VECTORS (Herbert Zettl)

### Graphic Vectors and Index Vectors

**Graphic Vectors**: Lines or directions established by visual elements within the frame
- **Application**: Composition creates implicit lines that guide eye movement
- **Continuity**: Matching graphic vectors between shots creates smooth flow
- **Discontinuity**: Clashing graphic vectors create tension or confusion

**Index Vectors**: Lines created by subject gaze or movement
- **Gaze Vector**: Where a character is looking (off-screen space)
- **Motion Vector**: Direction of subject movement
- **Application**: Cut on matching vectors = continuity; cut on clashing vectors = disjunction

**Z-Axis Vectors** (Depth)
- Movement toward/away from camera (z-axis motion)
- Can create continuity even when crossing 180° line
- Special case: Character moves toward camera = acceptable line crossing

### The 180° Rule at Production Stage

**Zettl's Contribution**: The 180° rule is not arbitrary — it maintains consistent screen direction for:
- **Eye trace**: Where audience looks between shots
- **Stage line**: Spatial coherence for action
- **Vector continuity**: Matching motion directions

**Production Application**:
- Establish line before action begins
- Consider vector directions when placing camera
- Use z-axis movement to legally cross line

## PHASE 2: MURCH'S SIX CRITERIA FOR THE IDEAL CUT (Walter Murch)

### The Hierarchy of Editing Decisions

When deciding where to cut, prioritize in this order:

**1. Emotion (51%)**
- How does the cut make the audience feel?
- Does it enhance or diminish the emotional impact?
- **Rule**: Never sacrifice emotion for other criteria

**2. Story (23%)**
- Does the cut advance the narrative?
- Does it clarify or confuse story progression?
- **Application**: Cut must serve the story beat

**3. Rhythm (10%)**
- Does the cut fit the rhythmic pattern?
- Does it feel too long or too short?
- **Application**: Cut on the "beat" of the scene

**4. Eye-trace (7%)**
- Where is the audience looking?
- Does the cut respect eye movement?
- **Application**: Cut so eye doesn't have to jump across frame

**5. Two-dimensional plane of screen (5%)**
- Does the cut maintain screen direction?
- Does it follow the 180° rule?
- **Application**: Technical continuity

**6. Three-dimensional space of action (4%)**
- Does the cut maintain spatial continuity?
- Do characters stay in consistent positions?
- **Application**: Geographical coherence

### Blink Theory

**Concept**: People blink when thoughts change
- **Application**: Cut on a blink = invisible edit
- **Rhythm**: Blinks provide natural cutting rhythm
- **Emotion**: Suppressed blinks = tension; frequent blinks = release

## PHASE 3: BURCH'S MATRIX (Noël Burch)

### Institutional Mode of Representation (IMR) vs Primitive Mode of Representation (PMR)

**IMR** (Hollywood Continuity):
- Invisible cuts
- Spatial/temporal coherence
- Audience forgets they're watching constructed reality
- **Application**: Narrative fiction, mainstream film

**PMR** (Early Cinema, Experimental):
- Visible cuts
- Discontinuous space/time
- Audience aware of construction
- **Application**: Experimental, political, avant-garde

### The 5 x 3 Matrix

Five spatial/temporal relations between Shot A (outgoing) and Shot B (incoming):

| Temporal Relation | Graphic Relation | Index Relation |
|-------------------|----------------|----------------|
| Continuity | Match | Match |
| Ellipsis | Change | Change |
| Reverse | Reverse | Reverse |

**Application**: Each combination creates different effect:
- Continuity + Match + Match = Seamless flow
- Ellipsis + Change + Change = Time jump, new location
- Reverse + Reverse + Reverse = Flashback or disorientation

### Temporal Relations

**Temporal Continuity**: Shot B begins where Shot A ends (real-time)
**Temporal Ellipsis**: Shot B begins after Shot A ends (time compressed)
**Temporal Reversal**: Shot B shows earlier time (flashback)

## PHASE 4: BORDWELL'S NARRATIVE FUNCTIONS (David Bordwell)

### Temporal Order Manipulation

**Case A**: Simultaneous events, simultaneous presentation (Cross-cutting/Parallel action)
- Two actions happening at same time
- Cut between them
- **Function**: Create tension, compare/contrast

**Case B**: Successive events, simultaneous presentation (Montage/Summary)
- Events happen one after another
- Presented simultaneously (compressed)
- **Function**: Time compression, summary

**Case C**: Simultaneous events, successive presentation (Sequential revelation)
- Events happen at same time
- Shown one after another
- **Function**: Multiple perspectives on same event

**Case D**: Successive events, successive presentation (Standard continuity)
- Events happen one after another
- Shown in that order
- **Function**: Standard narrative progression

### Temporal Duration Manipulation

**Equivalence**: Fabula Duration = Syuzhet Duration = Screen Duration
- Real-time presentation
- **Example**: Rope, Russian Ark

**Ellipsis**: FD > SYD (Story time longer than plot time)
- Time skipped between shots
- **Function**: Move through time efficiently

**Compression**: FD = SYD but both longer than Screen Duration
- Fast motion
- **Function**: Show process in shortened time

**Insertion**: FD < SYD (Story time shorter than plot time)
- Expansion of moment
- **Function**: Emphasize, dramatize moment

### Intensified Continuity

**Characteristics**:
- Shorter Average Shot Length (ASL)
- More close-ups
- Shot/reverse shot dominance
- Faster cutting in dialogue
- **Application**: Modern Hollywood style (post-1960s)

## PHASE 5: RHYTHMIC EDITING THEORY

### Internal Rhythm vs External Rhythm

**Internal Rhythm**: Rhythm created within the shot
- Tempo of action
- Pattern of movement
- Lens usage (depth cues)
- Camera placement
- Scope of shot
- Camera movement
- Color
- Repeated forms

**External Rhythm**: Rhythm created by cutting
- Average Shot Length (ASL)
- Decrease in ASL over film history
- Cutting to music (match vs counterpoint)

### Factors Controlling Internal Rhythm

**1. Tempo of Action**
- Fast movement = fast rhythm
- Slow movement = slow rhythm
- **Application**: Match cutting rhythm to action tempo

**2. Pattern of Movement**
- Repetitive motion creates rhythmic expectation
- Interrupted pattern creates surprise
- **Application**: Cut on repetitive motion for flow

**3. Lens Usage**
- Wide lens = more depth cues = more visual information
- Telephoto = flat = less information
- **Application**: Wide shots hold longer (more to see)

**4. Camera Placement**
- Close camera = intimate = hold longer or cut faster depending on intent
- Distant camera = observational = typically hold longer

**5. Scope of Shot**
- Wide scope = more elements = longer hold time needed
- Narrow scope (CU) = less elements = can cut faster

**6. Camera Movement**
- Moving camera creates its own rhythm
- Cut during movement = continuous flow
- Cut after movement stops = punctuation

**7. Color**
- Saturated colors = more visual energy
- Desaturated = less energy
- **Application**: Color intensity affects perceived rhythm

**8. Repeated Forms**
- Visual motifs create rhythmic pattern
- Breaking pattern creates emphasis
- **Application**: Use repeated forms for rhythmic coherence

### External Rhythm: Transitions as Modifiers

**Dissolves**:
- Slower transition
- Indicates time passage
- Softens harsh cuts
- **Rhythmic function**: Slows pace momentarily

**Fades**:
- Black or white fade
- Indicates major time/space shift
- **Rhythmic function**: Full stop, then restart

**Wipe**:
- Graphic transition
- Visible, artificial
- **Rhythmic function**: Energetic, disruptive

**Iris**:
- Circular fade
- Archaic/vintage feel
- **Rhythmic function**: Isolates subject, ends scene

## PHASE 6: BAZIN AND THE LONG TAKE (André Bazin)

### Realism and the Ontology of the Image

**Bazin's Position**:
- Editing is analysis (breaking reality into pieces)
- Long take is synthesis (preserving reality's wholeness)
- Deep focus allows viewer to choose what to look at (democratic)

**Deep Focus Strategies**:
- Everything in frame is in focus
- Viewer actively scans image
- No need for cutting to guide attention
- **Application**: Staging in depth, theatrical blocking

**When to Use Long Takes**:
- When wholeness of space is important
- When you want democratic viewing (audience chooses focus)
- When cutting would destroy reality effect
- **Examples**: Welles, Renoir, Tarkovsky

---

## PHASE 7: TARKOVSKY AND TIME-PRESSURE (Andrei Tarkovsky)

### Sculpting in Time

**Time-Pressure**: The feeling of time passing within a shot
- Created by duration, not action
- **Application**: Hold on static image to create time-pressure

**Characteristics**:
- Long takes as meditation
- Time is the medium, not space or action
- **Application**: Use when time itself is the subject

**Versus Montage**:
- Tarkovsky rejects collision montage (Eisenstein)
- Prefers time-pressure created by duration
- **Application**: Long takes for contemplative states

---

## PHASE 8: DREAM AND RITUAL EDITING (Maya Deren)

### Poetic/Psychological Editing

**Maya Deren's Approach**:
- Editing follows dream logic, not waking logic
- Spatial/temporal discontinuity is expressive
- **Application**: Psychological states, dreams, hallucinations

**Meshes of the Afternoon**:
- Circular narrative structure
- Repeated actions with variations
- **Application**: Obsession, compulsion, psychological loops

## PRACTICAL APPLICATION WORKFLOW

### Step-by-Step Analysis

**Given a scene or cut sequence:**

**Step 1: Murch's Criteria Check**
- Rate each cut on 6 criteria (51/23/10/7/5/4)
- Priority: Emotion > Story > Rhythm > Eye-trace > 2D > 3D
- Note any sacrifices made and justify

**Step 2: Zettl's Vector Analysis**
- Map graphic vectors in each shot
- Map index vectors (gaze, motion)
- Check vector continuity or intentional discontinuity

**Step 3: Burch's Matrix Classification**
- Identify temporal relation (continuity/ellipsis/reverse)
- Identify graphic relation (match/change/reverse)
- Identify index relation (match/change/reverse)
- Classify combination effect

**Step 4: Bordwell's Temporal Function**
- Identify which case (A/B/C/D)
- Identify duration manipulation (equivalence/ellipsis/compression/insertion)
- Note if intensified continuity techniques used

**Step 5: Rhythmic Analysis**
- Measure internal rhythm factors (action, movement, lens, etc.)
- Measure external rhythm (ASL, cutting pattern)
- Check transition modifiers

**Step 6: Realism Assessment**
- Bazin test: Does this need long take/deep focus?
- Tarkovsky test: Is time-pressure relevant?
- Deren test: Is dream/psychological logic needed?

## CASE STUDIES

### Case: Tension Building (Cameron-style)
- Murch: Emotion priority (51%) — cut for anxiety
- Rhythm: Decrease ASL as tension builds
- Bordwell: Case A (cross-cutting for simultaneous tension)

### Case: Flashback Sequence
- Burch: Temporal reversal + graphic change + index change
- Bazin: Long take at start to establish "reality" of memory
- Tarkovsky: Time-pressure if memory is contemplative

### Case: Dream/Hallucination
- Deren: Discontinuous space/time
- Burch: PMR (visible construction)
- Rhythm: Irregular pattern (no predictability)
