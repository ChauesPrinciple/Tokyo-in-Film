1.3

The Script and Genre

Over the past century, cinema has evolved into an incredibly complex medium involving the art and science of capturing the moving image, the equally important and co-expressive craft of sound design, not to mention new innovations in virtual reality and immersive technologies that will push the boundaries of what is possible in the years to come.

But one thing has not changed: the importance of a good story.

No matter how innovative the visual delights, how creative the soundscape, or how many millions are spent on the production design and celebrity talent, if it isn't all in service of a compelling narrative we'll walk away unmoved and unsatisfied. Good storytelling has been around at least as long as humans have been able to put together complete sentences — probably longer.

In this chapter we'll examine what makes cinematic storytelling unique, how narrative structure shapes our experience of the moving image, how compelling characters move that narrative forward, how theme and narrative intent inform everything from the mise-en-scène to the cinematography, music, sound design, and editing, and how all of this morphs into different narrative forms — or genres — in cinema.

But before we explore the technique of crafting a compelling narrative, let's take a look at the essential tool in that process: the screenplay.

1.3.1 The Screenplay

Before a single frame is shot, the film exists on paper. The screenplay is the blueprint for the entire production. It is a technical document, not a novel. It instructs the cast and crew on exactly what can be seen and heard.

Screenplays follow a rigid format for a practical reason: One page equals roughly one minute of screen time. This allows producers to look at a 120-page script and know "Okay, this is a two-hour movie." It allows them to budget the film and schedule the shoot.

EXT. SHIBUYA CROSSING - NIGHT

Rain hammers the asphalt. Neon lights reject the gloom. KENJI (20s, disheveled) pushes through the crowd of umbrellas. He stops. He sees HER.

KENJI

(shouting)

I know you are in here!

Passersby glance at him, then look away. A heavy silence amidst the noise.

OFFICER (O.S.)

Hey! You!

Kenji bolts.

Notice the economy of language. The writer does not tell us Kenji is "thinking about his regrets." We cannot photograph thoughts. We can only photograph action. Every element of the script page is there for a reason: the scene heading tells the crew whether the scene is interior or exterior (INT/EXT), where they are, and what time of day — information that affects every member of the team from the producer scheduling the shoot to the gaffer lighting the scene.

But notice too how economical the writing must be. There is no room to probe the inner life of characters or spin off into detailed descriptions. As a general rule of thumb, one page of screenplay equals approximately one minute of screen time. That doesn't always work out exactly, but tends to average out over the length of the script. So there simply isn't time to include anything but the essentials — allowing the other creative collaborators the freedom to interpret the rest.

And that is the key point: cinema is a collaborative medium. There's always a give and take between the script and the finished film, just as there is between the director and screenwriter, cinematographer, production designer, sound designer, actors, and editor. What the camera does is the cinematographer's job, not the screenwriter's. Wardrobe is the production designer's concern. Once you start building the film, you must confront and overcome hundreds of variables you could not anticipate — a neighbor's dog wanders into a shot, the weather turns on the last day of filming, the editor discovers a line plays better over the following scene. As much as a screenplay can and should be a great read, it is, ultimately, a blueprint for something exponentially more complex.

The Logline

A logline is a single sentence that captures the protagonist, goal, conflict, and stakes. This concise statement forms the "hook" for the story.

Example Loglines

Spirited Away

A timid girl must navigate a mysterious spirit world to rescue her parents and reclaim her identity, or risk being forgotten forever.

13 Assassins

Thirteen warriors must assassinate a sadistic lord protected by the state, knowing their mission could spark a civil war, but also restore justice to a corrupt empire.

Your Name

When a city boy and a rural girl begin switching bodies, they must find a way to meet before time and memory erase their connection forever.

1.3.2 Narrative Structure

Almost every story ever told can be boiled down to three elements:

One Protagonist. One Goal. A whole bunch of Obstacles.

While experimental films exist, the vast majority of cinema follows the Three-Act Structure. This is not a rule enforced by some Hollywood police force. It is simply a pattern that works for human psychology: we like setups, confrontations, and resolutions. Over more than a century of narrative filmmaking, and in Hollywood in particular, this structure has been refined into what is arguably cinema's most significant contribution to the global storytelling tradition — as formal and as generative as the haiku.

Act One (~25–30 min)

Introduces the protagonist, establishes their world, and defines the goal they will pursue for the rest of the story. By the end of Act One, we know who we're rooting for, what they want, and what's in the way. The protagonist is launched into Act Two — sometimes against their will.

Act Two (~55–60 min)

All about obstacles. With every confrontation, the stakes get higher and the protagonist must risk more. Around the midpoint (~minute 55–60), the protagonist faces a choice: turn back or double down. They double down. By the end of Act Two, it seems all hope is lost. The biggest obstacle yet has sealed their fate.

Act Three (~25–30 min)

The protagonist rallies. They overcome the final obstacle in a climactic confrontation and reach a resolution. Usually they achieve the goal defined in Act One — though sometimes the journey reveals that a different goal was the one that mattered all along.

To be clear, three-act structure is not an explicit rule to which screenwriters must conform. It is less a writing technique than an analytic tool — a way of breaking down cinematic stories for analysis. Unlike stage plays, there are no explicit act breaks in the script itself. Some writers actively work against the structure. But even Tarantino, who regularly "breaks the rules," understands that the journey of a protagonist toward their goal follows an arc toward resolution. More often than not, the exceptions prove how effective the structure has become — not just for screenwriters, but because audiences have internalized it as part of our shared cinematic language.

The Story Circle

Dan Harmon's Story Circle is a tool for mapping a character's journey through change. Whether in the violent chaos of Kubi or the quiet coming-of-age in Initial D, this structure reveals how characters evolve.

1. You, Establishing the Ordinary World
Kubi: The film opens in a volatile yet eerily ordered court. Nobunaga sits atop a delicate power structure held together by fear.
Initial D: Takumi lives in quiet monotony, delivering tofu before dawn. His world is constrained, familiar, and disconnected.

2. Need, Highlighting the Desire or Problem
Kubi: Nobunaga's unchecked ambition has created enemies on all sides.
Initial D: Takumi's need is buried under passivity. The challenge of racing awakens something dormant.

3. Go, Crossing into the Unfamiliar
Initial D: Takumi agrees to his first official race. This moment thrusts him into a subculture he never sought.

4. Search, Adapting to the New Situation
Kubi: Everyone starts maneuvering. Mitsuhide weighs loyalty against survival. Hideyoshi quietly calculates his opening. Nobody is where they were in scene one.
Initial D: Takumi learns what his car can actually do, and what he can do in it. The deliveries, the girlfriend, and the tofu shop all start competing for the same 24 hours.

5. Find, Getting What They Wanted
Kubi: The strike at Honno-ji lands. Nobunaga falls, and for a moment the top of the mountain is empty.
Initial D: Takumi wins the race he was not supposed to win and becomes the fastest driver on the mountain he has delivered tofu on his entire life.

6. Take, Paying a Heavy Price
Kubi: The victory is poisoned almost immediately. Mitsuhide rules for days before Hideyoshi cuts him down. Every winner just becomes the next target.
Initial D: The win costs Takumi his relationship with Natsuki and forces him to face the truth about who his father was before he was the tofu-shop dad.

7. Return, Heading Back to the Familiar
Kubi: The surviving players walk back into the same court, now hollowed out. The cycle of severed heads and new ambitions just resets for the next round.
Initial D: Takumi is back on the same mountain roads. Same corners, same curves, but the pre-dawn tofu run is no longer the whole point of his life.

8. Change, Having Been Transformed by the Journey
Kubi: The court endures and that is exactly the problem. Ambition, the film quietly argues, is a closed circle that only ends in more severed heads.
Initial D: Takumi is a driver now. The quiet monotony he started in has been permanently overwritten.

Structural Beats

Screenwriter and theorist Syd Field identified four load-bearing moments that recur across virtually every commercial screenplay. These are established terms in professional screenwriting practice. Check the timecode of any major studio film and you will find them landing at nearly identical minute marks.

Inciting Incident (~10–15 min)

The event that permanently disrupts the protagonist's ordinary world and sets the story in motion. In Spirited Away, Chihiro's parents are turned into pigs — she cannot return to her previous life. The story's central question is now locked in.

Act One Break (~25–30 min)

Field's Plot Point I: a turning point that spins the story in a new direction and propels the protagonist into Act Two. Chihiro signs Yubaba's contract — she commits to surviving in the spirit world on its own terms.

Midpoint (~60 min)

A major shift at the structural center of the film. The protagonist moves from reactive to active — from being acted upon to driving the story forward. In thrillers, this is often where the hunted becomes the hunter.

Act Two Break (~85–90 min)

Field's Plot Point II: the protagonist's plan collapses. Everything they have built in Act Two fails. This low point forces a final, irreversible commitment before the climax.

1.3.3 Compelling Characters

Narrative structure may be a critical component of cinematic language, but ultimately, structure is another word for plot — and we don't go to the movies to root for plots. We root for people. If there isn't a compelling character at the center of the story, all the plot points and special effects in the world won't hold our attention.

Round Characters

Complex, conflicted, and capable of change. They have a deep internal life and contradictions. Most protagonists are, or should be, round characters.

They want two mutually exclusive things (e.g., Safety vs. Freedom).

Flat Characters

Lack internal complexity and do not change over the course of the story. Usually present only to serve the round characters on their journey.

(The Bully, The Wise Mentor, The Henchman)

A protagonist should at the very least be interesting — and that does not mean they must be inherently good. Often the most compelling protagonists are flawed in some fundamental way, and part of the drama is watching them struggle with that flaw. That's one reason Superman is such a difficult character to pull off on screen. He's just so… good. He doesn't change very much. But Batman? That character is dark, conflicted, and morally compromised. And that's what makes him so much more interesting to watch — and arguably so much more successful at the box office.

Protagonist vs Goal

Your protagonist is the person whose journey defines the story. Their goal is the driving force behind the plot.

Spirited Away

Chihiro (Protagonist) must navigate a spirit world to rescue her parents (Goal).

Tokyo Drift

Sean (Protagonist) must adapt to a subculture built on speed and loyalty (Goal: Survival/Belonging).

Adrift in Tokyo

Fumiya (Protagonist) uses his unexpected walk across Tokyo as a slow, spiritual reawakening (Goal: Acceptance).

The Anti-Hero

Sometimes the most compelling protagonist is not a "good guy." An anti-hero is an unsympathetic hero pursuing an immoral goal — and somehow we end up rooting for them anyway. Think of virtually every heist film, every vigilante action movie, or any Tarantino picture. The main characters are essentially criminals. And we can't wait to see how they pull it off.

Think of Light Yagami in Death Note. He is a mass murderer with a god complex. Yet he is the protagonist. We are glued to the screen not because we agree with him, but because we want to see what he does next.

To be clear, an anti-hero is not the same as an antagonist. The antagonist's role is to stop the protagonist from reaching their goal. In The Dark Knight (2008), Batman is the protagonist and the Joker is the antagonist. But in Joker (2019), the Joker is the protagonist — an anti-hero — and the police, ostensibly the "good guys," are the antagonists. Whether protagonist or anti-hero, the central character should always drive the story forward. We are on their journey, and it's their actions that move us through the plot.

1.3.4 Narration and Point of View

Who is telling the story? In a novel, there is a narrator — a voice, sometimes a character, sometimes an authorial abstraction. In cinema, the primary narrator is always the camera. The camera dictates where we look and when. It provides all the information we need to construct the narrative unspooling at 24 frames per second. We are, all of us, voyeurs — peering into other people's lives through the frame.

We can distinguish between two broad modes of cinematic narration:

Restricted Narration

The camera never leaves the protagonist. We only know what the hero knows. Suspense is built through limited information. If the hero doesn't see the killer behind the door, neither do we.

Omniscient Narration

The camera can follow any character — protagonist, antagonist, minor figure — wherever the story requires. We may know things the hero does not, creating dramatic irony and tension from superior information.

The Unreliable Narrator

Normally we assume the camera tells the truth. If we see a character walk into a room, they walked into a room. But what if the narrator is lying?

Watch on YouTube

The classic example is Akira Kurosawa's Rashomon (1950). A samurai is murdered. Four witnesses testify: the bandit, the wife, the woodcutter, and even the ghost of the samurai himself. Each story contradicts the others. Each narrator paints themselves in a better light. We never learn the "objective" truth. We only learn that truth is subjective.

A modern Tokyo equivalent is Satoshi Kon's Perfect Blue (1997), where the protagonist's fracturing sanity makes the camera itself unreliable. Are those fans really there? Is she seeing a ghost? Or is she just losing her mind?

1.3.5 Theme and Narrative Intent

A clear narrative structure and compelling characters are crucial. But once we understand how a screenplay tells a story, we can ask the deeper question: what is it actually saying?

It's important to distinguish between plot — what happens in a film — and theme — what the film is really about.

Star Wars (1977) is about a farm boy saving a princess and destroying a planet-sized weapon. That's the plot. But it's really about believing in oneself and the difference one brave person can make in the face of overwhelming evil. That is its narrative intent. It's that underlying idea that activates the plot, defines the characters, and leads to a satisfying resolution. In the climactic Death Star sequence, Luke becomes the last pilot — one tiny fighter against a planet-sized weapon. And to defeat it, he must draw upon skills he learned back on the farm. The spectacle serves the theme.

Compare that to the action set piece at the center of G.I. Joe: Rise of Cobra (2009), where a missile destroys the Eiffel Tower in a blaze of CGI. It looks spectacular. But it is not connected to a clear theme, because there is no clear theme — just a plot, a sequence of events where things happen. One is left with the impression that the sequence exists only because someone thought it would look cool. And that's often why cinematic spectacle can leave us flat: it has no unifying narrative intent.

Theme doesn't mean "message" in the after-school-special sense. It means that great cinema is organized around an idea. Take Pixar's Toy Story (1995). The plot is simple: a child's favorite toy is threatened by a new arrival, jealousy leads to both becoming lost, and they must work together to return home. But the film is really about friendship and self-sacrifice — and every obstacle encountered dramatizes that theme, either as counterpoint or confirmation. That's what makes it a classic, and not just another cartoon.

A clear and well-planned theme can serve as a unifying principle, informing not just plot and character, but mise-en-scène, cinematography, sound design, and editing as well. Theme is the North Star of the screenplay.

1.3.6 Time: Story vs. Plot

It is crucial to distinguish between two concepts that are often used interchangeably:

  • Story: The chronological sequence of events. (A → B → C → D)
  • Plot: The order in which the audience sees them. (3 → 1 → 2 → 4)

A film like Pulp Fiction has a simple story but a complex, scrambled plot. In Tokyo cinema, The Tatami Galaxy (2010) takes this to the extreme. The protagonist relives his college years over and over, trying to find the "rose-colored" campus life. The Story events (meeting Ozu, joining a club) repeat, but the Plot is a spiral, showing us variations until the character finally breaks the loop.

1.3.7 Genre

Genre is a way to categorize types of cinematic narrative — westerns, romantic comedies, horror, superhero films — based on narrative conventions: the shared characters, settings, themes, and even musical scores that audiences recognize as belonging to a type. Genre is useful shorthand for both the people who consume cinema and the people who produce it. Cinema is an incredibly capital-intensive medium, and the more targeted the content, the more likely filmmakers will see a return on that investment.

But sometimes, genre's apparent rigidity is exactly what we want. When we go to see a romantic comedy, we know two people will meet, spend 90 minutes overcoming obstacles, suffer some terrible misunderstanding late in Act Two, and then someone will run through an airport to profess their true feelings. We know all of this before the opening credits. That's the point. We want to see how this particular filmmaker gets them there. But they better get there — that's why we bought the ticket.

Just like three-act structure, genre is ultimately more an analytic technique than a writing tool. The narrative conventions we associate with certain types of films help us analyze how a particular filmmaker approaches the fundamental questions in any story: Who is the hero? What do they want? How are they going to get it?

Story (Action) + Plot + Character + Setting = Genre

If you have a Sheriff (Character) in a dusty town (Setting) facing a duel (Action) to restore order (Plot), you have a Western.

If you have a Detective (Character) in a rainy city (Setting) solving a murder (Action) where everyone is corrupt (Plot), you have Film Noir.

Japanese Genre Spotlights

Jidai-geki

Period Drama

Historical films, usually set in the Edo period (1603–1868). Think Shogun or traditional samurai films.

Chambara

Swordplay Action

A sub-genre of Jidai-geki focused on action and sword fighting. The Eastern equivalent of the Western. Seven Samurai is a Chambara film.

Kaiju

Monster

Giant monsters attacking cities. Originally a metaphor for nuclear destruction (Godzilla), now often a distinct action sub-genre.

Understanding these conventions is what allows filmmakers to subvert them. Structure doesn't dictate predictability — it can just as easily inspire creativity. You cannot break the rules until you know them. And you can't know them until you've watched, read, and internalized what cinema has been building for more than a century.

One Protagonist. One Goal. A whole bunch of Obstacles.
