1.3

The Script and Genre

Over the past century, cinema has evolved into an incredibly complex medium. We have massive IMAX screens, Dolby Atmos sound, and VR headsets. But one thing hasn't changed: the importance of a good story.

No matter how innovative the visuals or how expensive the production design, if the specialized techniques of cinema aren't serving a compelling narrative, the audience will check out. Good storytelling has been around since we huddled around fires in caves. Cinema just gives us a new way to do it.

1.3.1 The Screenplay

Before a single frame is shot, the film exists on paper. The screenplay is the blueprint for the entire production. It is technical document, not a novel. It instructs the cast and crew on exactly what can be seen and heard.

Screenplays follow a rigid format for a practical reason: One page equals roughly one minute of screen time. This allows producers to look at a 120-page script and know "Okay, this is a two-hour movie." It allows them to budget the film and schedule the shoot.

The Logline

A logline is a single sentence that captures the protagonist, goal, conflict, and stakes. This concise statement forms the “hook” for the story.

Example Loglines

Spirited Away

A timid girl must navigate a mysterious spirit world to rescue her parents and reclaim her identity—or risk being forgotten forever.

13 Assassins

Thirteen warriors must assassinate a sadistic lord protected by the state, knowing their mission could spark a civil war—but also restore justice to a corrupt empire.

Your Name

When a city boy and a rural girl begin switching bodies, they must find a way to meet before time—and memory—erases their connection forever.

1.3.2 Narrative Structure

Almost every story ever told can be boiled down to three elements:

1 Protagonist. 1 Goal. A whole bunch of Obstacles.

While experimental films exist, the vast majority of cinema follows the Three-Act Structure. This isn't a "rule" enforced by some Hollywood police force. It's simply a pattern that works for human psychology. We like setups, confrontations, and resolutions.

The Story Circle

Dan Harmon's Story Circle is a tool for mapping a character's journey through change. Whether in the violent chaos of Kubi or the quiet coming-of-age in Initial D, this structure reveals how characters evolve.

1. You — Establishing the Ordinary World
Kubi: The film opens in a volatile yet eerily ordered court. Nobunaga sits atop a delicate power structure held together by fear.
Initial D: Takumi lives in quiet monotony, delivering tofu before dawn. His world is constrained, familiar, and disconnected.

2. Need — Highlighting the Desire or Problem
Kubi: Nobunaga's unchecked ambition has created enemies on all sides.
Initial D: Takumi's need is buried under passivity. The challenge of racing awakens something dormant.

3. Go — Crossing into the Unfamiliar
Initial D: Takumi agrees to his first official race. This moment thrusts him into a subculture he never sought.

The Plot Points

To keep a story moving, screenwriters use specific structural beats. If you look at the timecode of your favorite blockbuster, you will likely find these moments hitting at the exact same minute marks.

  • The Inciting Incident (10-15m): The "Call to Action." The hero’s normal life is disrupted. In *Spirited Away*, this is when Chihiro's parents turn into pigs. She can't go back to normal.
  • Plot Point I (25-30m): The hero commits to the journey. They leave the ordinary world. Chihiro signs the contract with Yubaba. She is in the spirit world now.
  • The Midpoint (60m): The stakes are raised. The hero moves from passive to active. In a thriller, this is often where the hunter becomes the hunted.
  • Plot Point II (85-90m): The "All Hope is Lost" moment. The plan fails. The hero is defeated. Now they must rise from the ashes for the finale.

1.3.3 Compelling Characters

Structure is useless without someone to root for. But "rooting for" doesn't mean "liking."

  • Round Characters: Complex, conflicted, and capable of change. They have contradictions. They want two mutually exclusive things (e.g., Safety vs. Freedom).
  • Flat Characters: Predictable. They serve a single function in the plot (The Bully, The Wise Mentor).

Protagonist vs Goal

Your protagonist is the person whose journey defines the story. Their goal is the driving force behind the plot.

Spirited Away

Chihiro (Protagonist) must navigate a spirit world to rescue her parents (Goal).

Tokyo Drift

Sean (Protagonist) must adapt to a subculture built on speed and loyalty (Goal: Survival/Belonging).

Adrift in Tokyo

Fumiya (Protagonist) uses his unexpected walk across Tokyo as a slow, spiritual reawakening (Goal: Acceptance).

The Anti-Hero

Sometimes the most compelling protagonist isn't a "good guy." An Anti-Hero lacks traditional heroic qualities like idealism or courage. They might be selfish, cynical, or even criminal.

Think of Light Yagami in Death Note. He is a mass murderer. He has a god complex. Yet he is the protagonist. We are glued to the screen not because we agree with him, but because we want to see what he does next.

1.3.4 Narration and Point of View

Who is telling the story?

In literature, you have "First Person" (I went to the store) or "Third Person" (He went to the store). In film, the primary narrator is always the Camera.

The camera decides what we see. And more importantly, what we don't see.

The Unreliable Narrator

Normally we assume the camera tells the truth. If we see a character walk into a room, they walked into a room. But what if the narrator is lying?

The classic example is Akira Kurosawa's Rashomon (1950). A samurai is murdered. Four witnesses testify: the bandit, the wife, the woodcutter, and even the ghost of the samurai himself. Each story contradicts the others. Each narrator paints themselves in a better light. We never learn the "objective" truth. We only learn that truth is subjective.

A modern Tokyo equivalent is Satoshi Kon's Perfect Blue (1997), where the protagonist's fracturing sanity makes the camera itself unreliable. Are those fans really there? Is she seeing a ghost? Or is she just losing her mind?

1.3.5 Time: Story vs. Plot

It is crucial to distinguish between two concepts that are often used interchangeably:

  • Story: The chronological sequence of events. (A -> B -> C -> D)
  • Plot: The order in which the audience sees them. (C -> A -> B -> D)

A film like Pulp Fiction has a simple story but a complex, scrambled plot. In Tokyo cinema, The Tatami Galaxy (2010) takes this to the extreme. The protagonist relives his college years over and over, trying to find the "rose-colored" campus life. The Story events (meeting Ozu, joining a club) repeat, but the Plot is a spiral, showing us variations until the character finally breaks the loop.

1.3.6 Genre

Genre is simply a way to categorize stories based on shared elements. It helps audiences know what they are buying a ticket for, and it helps producers sell those tickets.

Story (Action) + Plot + Character + Setting = Genre

If you have a Sheriff (Character) in a dusty town (Setting) facing a duel (Action) to restore order (Plot), you have a Western.

If you have a Detective (Character) in a rainy city (Setting) solving a murder (Action) where everyone is corrupt (Plot), you have Film Noir.

Japanese Genre Spotlights

  • Jidai-geki (Period Drama): Historical films set usually in the Edo period (1603-1868). Think Shogun or traditional samurai films.
  • Chambara (Swordplay): A sub-genre of Jidai-geki focused on action and sword fighting. The Eastern equivalent of the Western. *Seven Samurai* is a Chambara film.
  • Kaiju (Monster): Giant monsters attacking cities. Originally a metaphor for nuclear destruction (*Godzilla*), now often a distinct action sub-genre.

Understanding these conventions allows filmmakers to subvert them. You can't break the rules until you know them.
