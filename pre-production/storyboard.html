Storyboard: Visualizing a Movement-Driven Scene

Pre-Production Assignments

Script Storyboard Animatic Shot List Pilgrimage Shots

This assignment challenges you to develop a cinematic visual blueprint for one of your film's key sequences. Using storyboarding techniques, you will translate your scene into a series of visual beats, emphasizing framing, character motion, and camera movement. The goal is to pre-visualize how the story unfolds on screen, beat by beat, before the camera ever rolls.

Whether you are staging a moment of urgency, hesitation, confrontation, or revelation, this task demands clarity of vision and precision of execution. In animation, storyboards provide the skeleton. In live-action, they serve as a visual contract between director, DP, and crew. Your frames are not sketches for sketching's sake; they are instructions, decisions, and cinematic intentions.

Task

Select a 20 to 30 second movement-based scene from your own short film project. This may be a chase, a procession, an emotionally loaded walk, or a character's physical reaction to space or another person. You will break the scene into 6 to 8 storyboard frames, clearly visualizing how the camera frames the action and why the movement matters.

You are not illustrating the script; you are designing the film.

Steps

1

Choose Your Scene

Pick a 20 to 30 second section of your film that revolves around motion or physical behavior. Avoid dialogue-heavy static scenes. Prioritize sequences where blocking, pacing, and framing carry meaning.

Examples from course films:

  • A character moves through a crowded market, present but invisible to those around them (Shoplifters)
  • A confrontation escalates without words (13 Assassins)
  • A character throws open a door and runs into the night (Your Name)
2

Break Down the Sequence

Identify key action beats and camera perspectives. Ask:

  • Where does the character start and end?
  • What does the camera need to see, and when?
  • Where should we cut, or hold?

Focus on intentional framing: when do we go wide to show space? When do we go tight to show emotion?

3

Create Your Storyboard

Produce 6 to 8 sequential frames, clearly drawn and annotated with:

  • Shot type and camera angle
  • Camera movement (dolly, pan, static)
  • Subject movement
  • Environmental or lighting cues (if relevant)
  • One action note per frame

Deliverables

  • PDF or scanned storyboard images with annotations (6 to 8 frames)
  • A short director's note (100 to 150 words) explaining why this scene was chosen and what visual and emotional rhythm you are aiming for

Upload to: Storyboard_[ProjectTitle]_[YourName]

Purpose

This is not concept art. It is a directorial pre-visualization tool.

  • Storyboarding teaches clarity of visual intention
  • Frames make your film communicable to collaborators and executable on set
  • Visual planning separates reactive shooting from deliberate directing

A film that exists only in your head is not yet a film.

Motion is only cinematic if it is seen with intention. A glance. A step. A slide. A pause.

Frame it. Anchor it. Decide it.
