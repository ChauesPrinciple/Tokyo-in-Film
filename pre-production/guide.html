Pre-Production Stage

This stage is all about planning, brainstorming, and developing pre-production materials for both your chosen assignment and your own film. By the end of this stage, you will have created storyboards, shot lists, scripts, and a clear vision for shooting the scenes in production. Choose one assignment to complete and apply the techniques you learn to a corresponding scene in your own film.

Writing the Cinematic Beat

This assignment challenges you to script a short, movement-based moment that conveys emotion or escalation through precise action, physical rhythm, and camera-conscious direction. You are not writing a play. You are writing for the screen, where motion, silence, and framing matter as much as words, often more.

Whether it is a glance held too long, a character pacing before a confession, or the slow draw of a weapon, your script must guide the reader to see the film in their head. This is about controlling time, space, and tone on the page, before you touch a storyboard or camera.

Your Goal

Write a brief, formatted screenplay (30 to 45 seconds) for a pivotal visual moment in your project. The scene should revolve around movement, blocking, and tension-building, not extended dialogue. Think:

  • A confrontation where no one says what they mean
  • A private breakdown in a public space
  • A chase that reveals character, not just momentum

Your writing must be readable as cinema, not as prose, not as exposition. This is a director's draft, not a novelist's.

Step-by-Step Instructions

1

Choose a Visual Moment

Select a 30 to 45 second moment from your project that contains a physical action or behavioral shift, minimal or purposeful dialogue, and an emotional or narrative turning point. Example beats:

  • A character hesitates before walking through a door
  • Two people almost speak, but one leaves first
  • Someone prepares a space for something that never happens
2

Write the Scene in Screenplay Format

Use standard screenplay formatting. Tools like Celtx, Arc Studio, or WriterDuet (free versions) will help. Include: sluglines (INT./EXT., time of day), action lines (short, visual, cinematic), minimal dialogue only when essential, character behavior (physical choices, posture, glance, pacing), and sound cues when relevant (e.g., distant train horn, flickering lights).

Weak: “She is nervous and sad.”

Strong: “She adjusts her jacket again. A third time. Does not move.”

Your job is to suggest the film, not describe it.

Deliverables

  • PDF or formatted script, one scene (30 to 45 seconds)
  • Optional: 100-word reflection on how movement and silence carry the storytelling

Upload to: Script_[SceneTitle]_[YourName]

Why This Matters

Before you shoot a scene, you must write it clearly enough that others can see it and feel it before it is real. This assignment builds spatial writing discipline, cinematic tempo, and visual emotional control. If your script can be sketched, scored, or cut, it is working.

A great scene does not begin with dialogue. It begins with a choice, a movement, a pause. Film it first in the reader's mind. That is the script's job.

Visualizing a Movement-Driven Scene

This assignment challenges you to develop a cinematic visual blueprint for one of your film's key sequences. Using storyboarding techniques, you will translate your scene into a series of visual beats, emphasizing framing, character motion, and camera movement. The goal is to pre-visualize how the story unfolds on screen, beat by beat, before the camera ever rolls.

In animation, storyboards provide the skeleton. In live-action, they serve as a visual contract between director, DP, and crew. Your frames are not sketches for sketching's sake; they are instructions, decisions, and cinematic intentions.

Your Goal

Select a 20 to 30 second movement-based scene from your short film project. Break it into 6 to 8 storyboard frames, clearly visualizing how the camera frames the action and why the movement matters. You are not illustrating the script; you are designing the film.

Step-by-Step Instructions

1

Choose Your Scene

Pick a 20 to 30 second section that revolves around motion or physical behavior. Avoid dialogue-heavy static scenes. Prioritize sequences where blocking, pacing, and framing carry meaning. Examples from course films:

  • A character moves through a crowded market, present but invisible to those around them (Shoplifters)
  • A confrontation escalates without words (13 Assassins)
  • A character throws open a door and runs into the night (Your Name)
2

Break Down the Sequence

Identify key action beats and camera perspectives. Ask: Where does the character start and end? What does the camera need to see, and when? Where should we cut, or hold? When do we go wide to show space? When do we go tight to show emotion?

3

Create Your Storyboard

Produce 6 to 8 sequential frames with the following annotated for each:

  • Shot type and camera angle
  • Camera movement (dolly, pan, static)
  • Subject movement
  • Environmental or lighting cues (if relevant)
  • One action note per frame

Deliverables

  • PDF or scanned storyboard images with annotations (6 to 8 frames)
  • Director's note (100 to 150 words): why this scene, and what visual and emotional rhythm you are aiming for

Upload to: Storyboard_[ProjectTitle]_[YourName]

Why This Matters

Storyboarding teaches clarity of visual intention. Frames make your film communicable to collaborators and executable on set. Visual planning separates reactive shooting from deliberate directing. A film that exists only in your head is not yet a film.

Motion is only cinematic if it is seen with intention. A glance. A step. A slide. A pause. Frame it. Anchor it. Decide it.

Structuring a Scene Through Visual Logic

A film is not made in the moment of inspiration; it is built, brick by brick, shot by shot. This assignment challenges you to create a detailed shot list for one of your scene's key moments, transforming your ideas into an executable visual sequence.

A great shot list is more than a checklist; it is a structural score. It defines rhythm, frames emotional tone, and ensures that your coverage is purposeful, not reactive. Every shot is a decision about what the audience sees, when, and how.

Your Goal

Select a 20 to 30 second segment where visual coverage shapes mood or momentum. Build a professional shot list that breaks this moment into discrete, carefully chosen camera setups accounting for: framing and angle, camera movement (or stillness), duration and pacing, and action and performance cues. Make the scene cuttable before it is shot.

Step-by-Step Instructions

1

Choose Your Sequence

Select a scene with meaningful movement, gesture, or emotional progression. Options:

  • A walk through an emotionally charged space (Shoplifters, Tsukigakirei)
  • A tense face-off (Kubi, 13 Assassins)
  • A moment of urban disorientation (Spirited Away, Tokyo Ghoul)

Avoid scenes that rely only on static two-shots or dialogue coverage.

2

Build Your Shot List Table

3

Consider Spatial and Emotional Progression

What begins wide and tightens? When does the camera mirror or oppose the character's psychology? Are transitions clean, or deliberately jarring?

Why This Matters

A well-structured shot list directs the energy. It defines whether a moment stutters or flows, isolates or entangles. If your film cannot be broken down into shots, it cannot yet be shot.

Every shot is a verb. It asks: What do we show? Who sees it? When do we cut, and why? If you can answer that, you are thinking like a director.

Timing the Visual Beat (Advanced Option)

An animatic is the next step beyond a storyboard. Where a storyboard defines what the camera sees, an animatic defines when it sees it, and for how long. You are building a rough, timed version of your film before a single frame is shot. Used in animation, commercials, and complex live-action pre-production, the animatic trains you to make decisions about pacing, rhythm, and image duration before the edit room. If a storyboard is a score, the animatic is the first rehearsal.

Your Goal

Select a 20 to 30 second movement-based scene. Using your storyboard frames (or new rough images), assemble a timed sequence that communicates how the scene will feel in motion. The question is not just what the audience sees; it is how long they live inside each image, and what that duration does to the emotion of the scene.

Step-by-Step Instructions

1

Gather or Create Your Frames

Use your storyboard panels or create new rough images. Scan or photograph them if working on paper. Minimum 6 frames for a 20 to 30 second sequence. Each frame should clearly indicate shot type, subject position, movement direction, and approximate screen duration.

2

Assemble the Timed Sequence

Import your frames into a video editing tool (DaVinci Resolve, iMovie, CapCut, or similar) and set the duration of each frame to match your intended screen time. Ask: Does this shot breathe, or does it rush? Where does a longer hold create tension? Where does a quick cut create urgency?

A 4-second hold on a character's face before they speak changes the meaning of their words. A half-second cut between two locations suggests panic. Decide every duration on purpose.
3

Add Temporary Audio (Optional but Recommended)

Even rough audio helps enormously. Consider: voiceover narration for dialogue or internal thought, temporary music or ambient sound to establish tone, and sound effect placeholders (e.g., a clap to mark a door slam). This does not need to be your final audio; it is a scaffold to test whether the scene's rhythm holds when image and sound combine.

4

Review and Refine

Watch your animatic at least three times. Ask: Does the scene communicate its emotional intention without spoken explanation? Are there moments that drag, or cut too soon? Does the pacing fit the genre and tone? Adjust durations, reorder frames if needed.

Deliverables

  • Exported video file or shareable link (10 to 30 seconds)
  • Director's note (100 to 150 words) on pacing choices and emotional rhythm
  • Optional: frame-by-frame breakdown listing each shot's duration and intended effect

Upload to: Animatic_[ProjectTitle]_[YourName]

Why This Matters

Animatics train temporal thinking: how motion and image create rhythm. They reveal pacing problems before the edit room, when changes are cheap, and make your creative vision communicable to a crew. A film that exists only in your head is not yet a film. An animatic is your first proof that the film is real.
