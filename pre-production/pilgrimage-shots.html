Pilgrimage Shots: Locating the Frame

In Your Name, Tokyo Ghoul, and Beyond

Pre-Production Assignments

Script Storyboard Animatic Shot List Pilgrimage Shots

This assignment challenges you to step into the director's shoes, literally. You will visit a real Tokyo location featured in Your Name, Tokyo Ghoul, or another course film that transforms real space into cinematic meaning. These include Tsukigakirei, which frames Kawagoe's riverside and school grounds with a still, observational lens to mirror adolescent hesitation; Initial D, where Tokyo's outer mountain roads become liminal speed zones charged with nocturnal motion and solitude; Godzilla Minus One, which renders Ginza and Shimbashi as national trauma sites through axial composition and architectural rupture; Kubi, where Nikko's temples and Yabusame grounds stage ritualized absurdity through symmetrical wide shots; and The Seven Samurai, which uses village topography and seasonal texture to choreograph tension, community, and defense.

This is not about fan tourism. It is about understanding how space, architecture, and natural light construct emotional and thematic weight. In anime and film, cityscapes and rural structures are not just backgrounds; they are emotional scaffolding. A staircase becomes destiny. An alley becomes violence. A shrine becomes judgment. A parade ground becomes the geometry of war. Your task is to return to these locations with a camera and a filmmaker's eye, to uncover what these frames teach us about the language of cinema.

Task

Select one specific shot from Your Name or Tokyo Ghoul (or another approved course film) that takes place in a real-world Tokyo location. Then recreate that image on location using one of two visual strategies:

  • A side-by-side comparison: the original film frame alongside your live photo
  • A postcard overlay: holding the film frame physically (or digitally) in front of the real location and aligning it to match

After capturing the shot, you will write a 300 to 400 word location plan explaining how the scene uses the setting to shape tone or emotion, what changed when you saw it in person, and how this space could function in your own future film work.

Nikko: For Kubi, The Seven Samurai, and 13 Assassins

Shrines (Toshogu, Futarasan)

Use the symmetry of temple approaches and gate-lined paths to mirror Kubi's stylized compositions and ironic serenity before eruptions of violence. These spaces offer strong axial framing and depth of field opportunities rarely available in urban Tokyo.

Yabusame Tournament Grounds and Stables (Nikko Tamozawa Imperial Villa area)

Ideal for capturing mounted processionals and martial ritual. Recreate the geometric rhythms seen in The Seven Samurai's horse drills and 13 Assassins's combat rehearsal sequences. Focus on the relationship between figure, ground, and movement within a defined corridor.

Forest Pathways (between Toshogu and Kanmangafuchi Abyss)

These winding, elevated tracks match the ambush staging in 13 Assassins. Use low or obscured angles to evoke suspense and spatial uncertainty. The canopy and dappled light create natural contrast that mirrors the film's tonal shifts between ceremony and chaos.

Kawagoe: For Kubi, The Seven Samurai, and 13 Assassins

Taisho Roman Street and Kurazukuri Zone

These preserved Edo-era townscapes support wide-lens shots of group movement, parades, and civilian presence, reminiscent of pre-battle tension in The Seven Samurai and 13 Assassins. The continuous street facades create natural leading lines and depth that reward careful framing.

Warehouse District Alleyways

Use for tighter framing and shadowed tension, well-suited to covert movement, betrayal, or duels. The compressed sightlines and irregular geometry create natural obstacles that force compositional decisions. Work with what the space denies you, not just what it offers.

Festival Routes (near Kawagoe Hikawa Shrine)

Can be staged to evoke civic order before disruption, as seen in the lead-up to violent turns in Kubi. Wide shots of procession routes and shrine approaches establish the formal geometry that makes their eventual collapse emotionally legible.

Use reference images from the film or published anime pilgrimage guides to prepare your frame match before arriving on location.

2

Recreate the Shot

Travel to the location and capture your version of the film frame using one of these two approaches:

  • Side-by-side format: line up your live-action photo with the original frame and present them together
  • Postcard overlay: hold the film still (printed or on a phone screen) in front of the real setting and photograph them together, aligning the composition as closely as possible

Your frame does not need to be perfect. This is about angle, composition, and the translation of animated or filmed emotion into real space. The gap between the original and yours is part of the learning.

3

Write Your Location Plan (300 to 400 words)

Address all four of the following in your written response:

  • Why did you choose this frame?
  • What role does the location play in the original scene's tone, pacing, or symbolism?
  • What was different or surprising about seeing the space in person?
  • Could this location work in your own project? What kind of story would unfold here?

What to look for on location: The original composition in Your Name uses the Suga Shrine stairs as a visual threshold, a border between worlds, ordinary life below and something unknown above. When you stand at the bottom of those stairs, ask not what they look like, but what they feel like to ascend. That feeling is the film's argument. Your job is to find it again through your own lens.

Deliverables

  • One photographic match (side-by-side or overlay format)
  • One location plan document (300 to 400 words) addressing all four required questions
  • Optional: GPS pin or Google Maps link to your exact shooting location

Upload to: LocationPilgrimage_[Film]_[YourName]

Purpose

Directing does not start on set; it starts in space. This assignment trains you to:

  • Think like a location scout with a director's instinct
  • Understand how real-world geometry supports animated and filmed rhythm
  • Use framing and architecture as emotional tools before a camera is ever raised

This is cinematic thinking applied to real-world geography.

The stairs in Your Name are not magic because they are animated.

They are magic because they are composed, grounded, and felt.

This assignment asks you to find that feeling again, through your lens.
