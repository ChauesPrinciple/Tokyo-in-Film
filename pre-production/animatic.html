Animatic: Timing the Visual Beat Advanced

Pre-Production Assignments

Script Storyboard Animatic Shot List Pilgrimage Shots

An animatic is the next step beyond a storyboard. Where a storyboard defines what the camera sees, an animatic defines when it sees it, and for how long. You are building a rough, timed version of your film before a single frame is shot. This is where visual intention meets temporal thinking.

Used in animation, commercials, and complex live-action pre-production, the animatic trains you to make decisions about pacing, rhythm, and image duration before the edit room. If a storyboard is a score, the animatic is the first rehearsal.

Task

Select a 20 to 30 second movement-based scene from your own short film project. Using your storyboard frames (or new rough images), assemble a timed sequence that communicates how the scene will feel in motion. The animatic should include basic pacing, rhythm, and, where applicable, temporary audio to suggest timing.

The question is not just what the audience sees; it is how long they live inside each image, and what that duration does to the emotion of the scene.

Steps

1

Gather or Create Your Frames

Use your storyboard panels or create new rough images (hand-drawn or digital). Scan or photograph them if working on paper. You do not need polished art; clarity of composition is all that matters.

  • Minimum of 6 frames for a 20 to 30 second sequence
  • Each frame should clearly indicate shot type, subject position, and any movement direction
  • Label each frame with its approximate screen duration
2

Assemble the Timed Sequence

Import your frames into a video editing tool (DaVinci Resolve, iMovie, CapCut, or similar) and set the duration of each frame to match your intended screen time. Ask yourself:

  • Does this shot breathe, or does it rush?
  • Where does a longer hold create tension?
  • Where does a quick cut create urgency or disorientation?

Timing as storytelling: A 4-second hold on a character's face before they speak changes the meaning of their words. A half-second cut between two locations suggests panic. Decide every duration on purpose.

3

Add Temporary Audio (Optional but Recommended)

Animatics gain enormously from even rough audio. Consider adding:

  • Voiceover narration to indicate dialogue or internal thought
  • Temporary music or ambient sound to establish tone
  • Sound effect placeholders (e.g., a clap to mark a door slam or footstep rhythm)

This does not need to be your final audio. It is a scaffold to test whether the scene's emotional rhythm holds when image and sound are combined.

4

Review and Refine

Watch your animatic at least three times before submitting. Ask:

  • Does the scene communicate its emotional intention without spoken explanation?
  • Are there moments that drag, or moments that feel cut too soon?
  • Does the pacing feel appropriate to the genre and tone of your project?

Adjust durations, reorder frames if needed, and refine until the sequence feels like it is moving with purpose.

Deliverables

  • Exported video file or shareable link (10 to 30 seconds)
  • A short director's note (100 to 150 words) explaining the pacing choices and what emotional rhythm you aimed for
  • Optional: include a brief frame-by-frame breakdown listing each shot's duration and intended effect

Purpose

An animatic is not just a pre-production formality; it is a directorial argument. It says: this is how time moves in my film. This is the rhythm I am building toward. This is what I want the audience to feel, and for how long.

  • Animatics train temporal thinking: how motion and image create rhythm
  • They reveal pacing problems before the edit room, when changes are cheap
  • They make your creative vision communicable to a crew

A film that exists only in your head is not yet a film. An animatic is your first proof that the film is real.
