Base Recipes

Fake Blood

Objective: Create blood for spraying, smearing, or pooling effects.

Materials:

  • 12 cups light corn syrup (3 x 32 oz bottles)

  • 8 cups chocolate syrup

  • 6 cups strawberry syrup

  • 2 cups water

  • 1½ cups creamy peanut butter

  • 5-second smooth pour of liquid red food coloring

  • 20 drops liquid blue food coloring

  • 3 cups non-toxic washable children’s poster paint (classic red)

Steps:

  1. In a large mixing bowl, combine the corn syrup, chocolate syrup, strawberry syrup, and water.

  2. Add the peanut butter and mix until smooth.

  3. Gradually stir in red food coloring, blue food coloring, and poster paint.

  4. Adjust the ratios to achieve the desired viscosity and color.

Filming Tips:

  • Use a syringe or spray bottle for blood spurts.

  • Capture in slow motion to emphasize texture and motion.

  • Add lighting to highlight the shine and depth of the blood.

Open Wounds

Objective: Create realistic open wounds for close-up shots.

Materials:

  • Glue (non-toxic school glue works well)

  • Toilet paper

  • Standard makeup items (foundation, red lipstick, black eyeshadow)

  • Fake blood (from above recipe)

Steps:

  1. Apply a thin layer of glue to the skin where the wound will go.

  2. Place a small piece of toilet paper on top and let it dry partially.

  3. Apply additional glue on top, shaping the toilet paper to create raised edges or tears.

  4. Blend foundation or skin-colored makeup over the dried glue and paper to match the skin tone.

  5. Add red lipstick or red makeup inside the wound to mimic fresh tissue.

  6. Dab black eyeshadow around the edges for depth.

  7. Finish with fake blood for realism.

Filming Tips:

  • Use close-up shots to emphasize the wound detail.

Gore Realism Enhancements

  • Textures:

    • Mix sand or fine gravel into fake blood for gritty, coagulated effects.

    • Use oatmeal soaked in blood for chunky or clotted appearances.

  • Layering:

    • Apply thin layers of gelatin over fake blood to create "skin" peeling effects.

    • Add translucent silicone sheets to simulate deeper layers of flesh.

  • Temperature Effects:

    • Warm fake blood for realistic fluidity.

    • Chill gelatin for stiffer, aged wounds.

      • Add subtle lighting from the side to highlight textures.

DIY Creating Smoke

  1. Dry Ice Smoke (Classic Fog)

    • Materials:

      • Dry ice (available at grocery or specialty stores)

      • Warm water (not boiling, around 60°C/140°F)

      • Insulated gloves or tongs (to handle dry ice safely)

      • Plastic or metal container

      • Optional: Fans or tubing for control

    • Steps:

      • Fill the container with warm water (approximately halfway full).

      • Using gloves or tongs, carefully add a small piece of dry ice into the water.

      • The dry ice will sublimate (turn directly into gas), producing dense, low-lying fog.

      • To sustain the effect, add more warm water as it cools or periodically refresh the water and dry ice.

      • For directed smoke, attach a flexible tube to the container and guide the flow.

    • Practical Tips:

      • Use shallow containers for ground-level effects and tall containers for columns.

      • For outdoor use, ensure minimal wind for better smoke control.

  2. Smoke from Incense or Smoke Pellets

    • Materials:

      • Incense sticks or cones

      • Smoke pellets (available from theatrical or hobby stores)

      • Metal tray or fire-safe surface

    • Steps:

      • Place the incense or smoke pellet on a fire-safe surface.

      • Light the incense or pellet and allow it to smolder, producing a steady stream of smoke.

      • Adjust placement or combine multiple sources for thicker smoke effects.

      • Use a handheld fan to guide the smoke in desired directions or create motion.

    • Practical Tips:

      • Ideal for smaller, controlled smoke sources like steaming pipes or mystical effects.

      • Incense offers various scents, which can help mitigate discomfort for actors.

  3. Paper and Sawdust Smoke (Old-School Fire Simulation)

    • Materials:

      • Torn paper or cardboard

      • Fine sawdust

      • Metal tray or fire-safe container

      • Matches or a lighter

    • Steps:

      • Place the paper and sawdust into the metal tray.

      • Light the edge of the paper and allow it to burn briefly before extinguishing it.

      • The smoldering materials will release thick, dark smoke.

      • Add more sawdust to sustain the effect as needed.

    • Practical Tips:

      • Works well for simulating burning debris or aftermath of a fire.

      • Use outdoors or in well-ventilated areas due to strong odors.

  4. Haze in a Can (Commercial Smoke Spray)

    • Materials:

      • Haze in a Can (available at theatrical supply stores or online)

    • Steps:

      • Shake the can thoroughly before use.

      • Spray in short bursts in the desired area, allowing the haze to settle naturally.

      • Combine with fans or light airflow to create movement.

    • Practical Tips:

      • Best for subtle atmospheric effects or confined indoor spaces.

      • Completely portable and easy to clean up compared to other methods.

  5. Cotton Wick Smoke (Handheld or Small Area Effects)

    • Materials:

      • Cotton rope or thick string

      • Small fireproof dish

      • Vegetable oil or candle wax

    • Steps:

      • Soak the cotton rope in oil or melted candle wax.

      • Allow it to dry partially until it is tacky.

      • Light one end of the rope and blow out the flame to produce smoke.

      • Move the smoldering rope around the scene for localized smoke effects.

    • Practical Tips:

      • Works well for mystical or ritualistic settings.

      • Use in a fire-safe environment and extinguish thoroughly after use.

    • Safety and General Tips for All Methods

      • Always work in a well-ventilated area to prevent inhaling excessive smoke.

      • Keep fire extinguishers or buckets of water nearby when using heat or flames.

      • Test smoke effects in advance to ensure compatibility with your scene’s requirements.

      • Use strong directional lighting to maximize the visibility and drama of the smoke.

Enhancements for All Effects

  1. Lighting:

    • Use gels to add thematic colors to any effect.

    • Side-lighting enhances texture in smoke and water.

  2. Multi-Element Integration:

    • Combine water ripple effects with smoke for ethereal visuals.

    • Pair firebursts with wind for explosive, dynamic shots.

  3. Camera Work:

    • Slow-motion enhances every effect by revealing intricate details.

    • Handheld shots can add chaos to storm and wind scenes.

Exercise: Creating a Realistic Prosthetic Hand

Objective: Make a life-like severed hand for horror scenes.

Materials:

  • Alginate molding powder

  • Water

  • Two mixing buckets

  • Mixing stick (or paint mixer drill attachment)

  • Silicone rubber (trial-size kit)

  • Silicone paint (with flesh tones and tints)

Steps:

  1. Prepare the Mold:

    • Mix alginate molding powder and water in a 1:1 ratio in a bucket.

    • Stir quickly (it hardens in 5 minutes) until the consistency is smooth.

    • Submerge your hand into the mixture in the desired position.

    • Wait 10-15 minutes for the mold to harden. Wiggle your hand free carefully.

  2. Pour the Silicone:

    • Combine the two parts of silicone rubber according to the kit instructions.

    • Pour the mixture into the hand mold, ensuring it fills every crevice.

    • Let the silicone set for 5-6 hours.

  3. Paint and Detail:

    • Remove the prosthetic from the mold.

    • Use silicone paint to match a realistic skin tone.

    • Add details like veins, bruises, or blood using red, white, and blue tones.

Filming:

  • Frame the hand in close-ups to highlight realistic textures.

  • Position it on a surface with fake blood for an added gore effect.

Exercise: Realistic Guts

Objective: Make convincing intestines for gut-spilling scenes.

Materials:

  • Pantyhose (nude or light color)

  • Paper towels

  • Fake blood (from Exercise 2)

Steps:

  1. Cut pantyhose into long, tubular sections.

  2. Stuff each section with crumpled paper towels.

  3. Twist and tie the ends to mimic intestinal shapes.

  4. Soak the “guts” in your fake blood mixture until saturated.

  5. Twist and fold the guts for added realism.

Filming:

  • Lay the guts on a surface or spill them from a container for maximum effect.

  • Combine with close-up shots of blood dripping to enhance the grotesque visuals.

Exercise: Simulated Blood Spray

Objective: Create dynamic blood sprays for action or horror scenes.

Materials:

  • Small water gun or bulb syringe

  • Fake blood (from Exercise 2)

  • Plastic sheeting (for easy cleanup)

Steps:

  1. Load the water gun or syringe with the fake blood mixture.

  2. Practice spraying the blood at different angles to find the right pressure.

  3. For a finer mist, introduce air into the syringe or use a spray bottle.

  4. Protect your filming area with plastic sheeting.

Filming:

  • Capture the blood spray in slow motion for a dramatic effect.

  • Use side lighting to highlight droplets in motion.

Exercise: Melting Flesh Effect

Objective: Create a melting or decaying look for body horror scenes.

Materials:

  • Gelatin (plain, unflavored)

  • Hot water

  • Flesh-colored food coloring

  • Heat gun or hair dryer

Steps:

  1. Mix gelatin with hot water to create a thick paste.

  2. Add flesh-colored food coloring for a realistic base.

  3. Apply the gelatin paste onto a surface (e.g., a prosthetic or object).

  4. Use a heat gun or hair dryer to gently melt the gelatin, creating the appearance of dripping or decaying flesh.

Filming:

  • Film close-ups of the gelatin melting and dripping off the object.

  • Use soft lighting to accentuate the textures and movement.

Exercise: Severed Limbs

Objective: Create a realistic severed hand or foot.

Materials:

  • Alginate molding powder

  • Water

  • Mixing bucket

  • Silicone rubber kit

  • Silicone paint (flesh tone and red)

  • Utility knife

Steps:

  1. Mix alginate molding powder and water in a 1:1 ratio in a bucket.

  2. Submerge your hand or foot in the alginate, keeping still while it hardens (10-15 minutes).

  3. Carefully remove your hand or foot to leave the mold intact.

  4. Mix the silicone rubber components and pour into the mold.

  5. Allow the silicone to set (5-6 hours) before removing.

  6. Paint the silicone with flesh tones and add red or black paint at the severed ends for gore.

Filming Tips:

  • Frame the limb with blood pooling around it.

  • Use a tripod for steady close-up shots.

Exercise: Exploding Heads

Objective: Simulate a head explosion for a dramatic effect.

Materials:

  • Water balloon

  • Fake blood (from above recipe)

  • Green screen

  • Adobe After Effects (or similar software)

Steps:

  1. Fill a water balloon with fake blood and seal tightly.

  2. Place the balloon on a mannequin head or dummy, aligned with the actor’s position in the green screen shot.

  3. Film the balloon bursting (use a sharp object to pop it).

  4. In post-production, replace the mannequin head with footage of the actor’s head, using keying and masking techniques.

Filming Tips:

  • Use slow motion to capture the blood splatter.

Ensure proper alignment in post-production for realism.

Exercise: Severed Limb with Bone Exposed

Objective: Create a severed limb effect with visible bone.

Materials:

  • PVC pipe or white plastic (for the bone)

  • Foam or cotton batting

  • Silicone rubber or gelatin (for flesh)

  • Fake blood (from Exercise 2)

Steps:

  1. Cut the PVC pipe to the desired “bone” length and roughen the ends.

  2. Wrap foam or batting around one end of the PVC pipe to create flesh.

  3. Coat the flesh area with silicone rubber or gelatin for a realistic texture.

  4. Paint the exposed bone with white and red details (e.g., blood stains).

  5. Add fake blood to the flesh and bone for a freshly severed look.

Filming:

  • Frame the severed limb lying on the ground with blood pooling around it.

  • Use camera movements like slow zooms or pans to build tension.

Exercise: Gunshots with Squibs

Objective: Simulate gunshot wounds with blood spray.

Materials:

  • DIY squib (condom, small firecracker, fishing line)

  • Fake blood

  • Small pump or air compressor

Steps:

  1. Fill a small balloon or condom with fake blood.

  2. Attach the balloon to the actor’s clothing where the "bullet wound" will be.

  3. Run fishing line or air tubing to the balloon.

  4. Trigger the squib remotely to burst the balloon and spray blood.

Filming Tips:

  • Film from multiple angles to capture the impact and spray.

  • Use close-ups to highlight the wound opening.

Exercise: Splattering Glass with Fake Blood

Objective: Create dramatic blood splatters on a glass surface for horror or action scenes.

Materials:

  • Clear glass pane

  • Fake blood (from your recipe)

  • Plastic sheet or tarp (for cleanup)

  • Paintbrush or toothbrush

  • Compressed air can (optional)

Steps:

  1. Lay the glass pane horizontally on a protected surface.

  2. Dip the toothbrush or paintbrush into the fake blood mixture.

  3. Flick the bristles toward the glass for controlled splatters, or blow compressed air across the bristles for finer droplets.

  4. Film close-ups of blood slowly dripping down the glass for added tension.

Filming Tips:

  • Backlight the glass to highlight the dripping patterns.

  • Film in slow motion to capture the droplets mid-air.

Exercise: Dynamic Spurt with Compression

Objective: Create high-pressure blood sprays for violent impact effects.

Materials:

  • Large syringe or air pump

  • Flexible tubing

  • Fake blood

  • Tarp for cleanup

Steps:

  1. Fill the syringe or air pump with fake blood.

  2. Attach flexible tubing to the nozzle of the pump.

  3. Place the other end of the tubing near the desired impact location.

  4. Trigger the pump to release a high-pressure spray.

Filming Tips:

  • Use high-speed cameras to capture the spray in stunning detail.

  • Film against a light or white background to emphasize the blood.

Exercise: Simulated Rain

Objective: Create cinematic rain for dramatic or atmospheric scenes.

Materials:

  • Garden hose with a spray nozzle or sprinkler system

  • Large, clean tarp (to control water flow if indoors)

  • Backlighting source (e.g., a strong LED or floodlight)

Steps:

  1. Attach the spray nozzle to the garden hose and set it to a fine mist or rain-like pattern.

  2. Position the hose or sprinkler system at a height that allows water to fall naturally.

  3. Backlight the rain with a bright light source to make each droplet visible on camera.

  4. Film with a slightly slow frame rate (e.g., 60 fps) to capture the rain's movement dramatically.

Filming Tips:

  • Use shallow depth of field to create a soft, dreamy rain effect.

  • Adjust water pressure for varying intensities, from a light drizzle to a downpour.

Exercise: Water Ripple Effects

Objective: Simulate reflective or rippling water for abstract or surreal visuals.

Materials:

  • Shallow glass container or aquarium

  • Black or white backdrop

  • Small pebbles or droppers for controlled ripple effects

Steps:

  1. Fill the container with clean water and place it on a flat, well-lit surface.

  2. Drop pebbles into the water to create natural ripples.

  3. Use a dropper to add small, controlled ripples or patterns.

  4. Film from above or at an angle to capture the ripple motion and reflections.

Filming Tips:

  • Backlight the container to create high-contrast visuals.

  • Combine with colored lights or gels to add an artistic touch.

Exercise: Controlled Fireburst

Objective: Create a safe, small-scale fire effect for explosive or dramatic visuals.

Materials:

  • Steel wool

  • Wire hanger or metal stick

  • Lighter

  • Bucket of sand (for extinguishing)

Steps:

  1. Stretch the steel wool onto the wire hanger or metal stick.

  2. Ignite the steel wool with a lighter, allowing it to burn slowly.

  3. Swing the wire hanger in circular motions to create a trail of sparks.

  4. Capture the sparks in low-light settings for dynamic, fiery visuals.

Safety Tips:

  • Perform this exercise in an open, outdoor area free of flammable materials.

  • Always keep a bucket of sand or a fire extinguisher nearby.

Exercise: Fake Flames

Objective: Simulate flames without using real fire for safety.

Materials:

  • Silk fabric (orange, red, and yellow)

  • Small fan

  • LED lights (orange and red tones)

Steps:

  1. Attach strips of silk fabric to the base of the fan.

  2. Position the LED lights beneath the fabric to mimic a flame's glow.

  3. Turn on the fan to make the fabric "dance" like fire.

  4. Adjust lighting and fabric lengths for different flame sizes.

Filming Tips:

  • Use close-ups and dynamic camera angles to heighten the effect.

  • Combine with a fog machine to simulate smoke.

Exercise: Low-Lying Fog

Film Example: The cold breath scenes in The Exorcist use condensation effects that mimic low-lying fog and chilling atmospheres.

Objective: Recreate eerie ground fog that clings to the surface, ideal for horror or dream-like sequences.

Materials:

  • Dry ice

  • Shallow container or tray

  • Warm water

  • Tubing (optional for targeted fog placement)

  • Black or neutral backdrop

Steps:

  1. Place a shallow container or tray on the ground and fill it with warm water.

  2. Add small chunks of dry ice to the water to generate dense fog.

  3. Use tubing to direct the fog to specific areas or props for precision.

  4. Refresh with additional warm water as needed to maintain fog density.

Filming Tips:

  • Light the fog from the side or below to accentuate its movement.

  • Shoot at ground level to emphasize the fog’s interaction with surfaces.

Exercise: Smoke Filling a Room

Film Example: The atmospheric smoke-filled interiors in Blade Runner 2049 establish a mysterious and dystopian tone.

Objective: Create thick, diffused smoke to fill a confined space for moody, atmospheric shots.

Materials:

  • Smoke machine (or fog machine)

  • Haze in a Can (alternative for smaller spaces)

  • LED lights with gels for color effects

  • Blackout curtains or boards to limit external light

Steps:

  1. Place the smoke emission (machine or otherwise) in a corner of the room and let it run intermittently.

  2. Use a fan to distribute the smoke evenly throughout the space.

  3. Add lighting with colored gels to create a soft, diffused glow through the smoke.

  4. Adjust the density of the smoke by controlling the machine output.

Filming Tips:

  • Use slow pans or tracking shots to reveal details emerging from the smoke.

  • Experiment with underexposed settings to create silhouettes against backlit smoke.

Exercise: Controlled Steam Effect

Film Example: Alien uses steam and smoke in confined environments to heighten tension and create a sense of claustrophobia.

Objective: Simulate venting steam for industrial or sci-fi settings.

Materials:

  • Kettle or small steam generator

  • Flexible tubing

  • LED lights with cool tones

Steps:

  1. Boil water in a kettle or steam generator to produce steam.

  2. Attach tubing to the steam source to direct it into the frame.

  3. Use fans to spread the steam for a diffused effect or leave it concentrated for venting visuals.

  4. Highlight the steam with LED lights for a futuristic feel.

Filming Tips:

  • Shoot at close range to capture the steam’s interaction with surfaces.

  • Use minimal background elements to keep the focus on the steam.
