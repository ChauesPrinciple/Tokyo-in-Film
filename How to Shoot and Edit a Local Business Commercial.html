How to Shoot and Edit a Local Business Commercial

Core Goal

You’re not making a pretty montage.
You’re making a 60-second ad that:

  1. Hooks the viewer in the first 3–5 seconds.

  2. Clearly shows what the service/experience feels like.

  3. Ends with a specific call to action that can actually generate sales.

Everything below exists to serve those three things.

Pre-Production (Weeks Before the Shoot)

Talk to the client

Before you touch a camera:

  • Clarify:

    • What are we selling? (exact service/offer)

    • Who is it for?

    • What should viewers do after watching? (click, book, call, visit)

  • Ask where the ad will live:

    • Website (horizontal 16:9)

    • Social media (vertical 9:16)

    • Paid ads (both)

Write a simple script

Aim for ~60 seconds. Structure:

  1. Hook (first line):

    • Directly hit a problem or desire.

    • Example: “If you’re feeling tense, stressed, and overwhelmed, it’s time to reset.”

  2. Experience / solution:

    • Walk the viewer through the experience step by step.

    • Focus on sensory detail and transformation:

      • What they see, hear, feel.

  3. Offer + call to action:

    • Spell out the offer (time, price, discount, location).

    • Tell them exactly what to do:

      • “Click below to book,”

      • “Call now,”

      • “Book your first session today.”

Turn the script into a shot list

For each beat of the script, define:

  • What we see while that line is heard:

    • Wide: establish space and vibe.

    • Medium: show the process.

    • Close-up: show details and emotion.

    • Extreme close-up: hands, feet, tools, textures.

Aim for:

  • A mix of wides, mediums, tights, and extreme close-ups.

  • Enough coverage that you’re never stuck on one shot for too long.

On-Set Workflow (Order of Operations)

On shoot day, the basic order is:

  1. Shoot the hook.

  2. Shoot the outro / call to action.

  3. Record the entire script as audio (like a voiceover) while talent is still mic’d.

  4. Shoot B-roll to cover the whole script.

  5. Finish with extra detail shots to give yourself options in the edit.

You’re building the spine first (hook + CTA + full script), then filling it with visuals.

General Shooting Practices (Camera-Agnostic)

Frame rate and motion

  • Use 24 fps (or 25 if you’re in a PAL region) for the main edit:

    • Hook

    • Most talking moments

    • Outros / CTA

  • Use a slightly higher frame rate (e.g., 30 fps or 50/60 fps) for B-roll when:

    • You want a subtle slow-motion feel.

    • You’re capturing gentle, slow movement (hands, water, fabrics, etc.).

Rule of thumb:

  • Main narrative: 24/25 fps.

  • Soft slow-mo B-roll: slightly higher frame rate, slowed down in post.

Shutter and exposure (generic)

  • Shutter:

    • Use roughly double the frame rate (180° shutter equivalent).

    • Example:

      • 24 fps → ~1/50

      • 30 fps → ~1/60

  • Exposure:

    • Use your camera’s exposure tools:

      • Histogram, zebras, false color (if available).

    • Skin tones should sit comfortably above mid-grey without clipping.

  • Avoid “monster lighting”:

    • Don’t light from directly below the chin.

    • Look for a soft shadow under the chin and one side of the face slightly darker.

Lighting basics

  • Key light:

    • Place the key light so the subject faces slightly toward it and toward camera.

    • Soften it (softbox, bounce, diffusion) to match the mood of the business (spa = soft, relaxing).

  • Back/hair light:

    • Add a small light behind or above to separate talent from the background.

  • Ambient mood:

    • Lift exposure enough so it’s readable.

    • Do not flatten or over-brighten a space that is supposed to feel cozy or calm.

Audio

  • Use lav mics for spoken lines if possible.

  • Monitor levels:

    • Avoid clipping.

    • Capture at a healthy level with headroom.

  • Bonus: if you have timecode or any sync tool, use it; if not:

    • Clap for sync.

    • Keep good notes on which audio track goes with which talent.

Backup recording

  • If your camera supports dual recording (two cards), use it.

  • If not:

    • Keep extra cards.

    • Don’t fill any card to 100%.

    • Back up as soon as you’re done.

Directing and Capturing Performance

The hook

Direct the hook like a mini-scene:

  • Give your subject a simple action:

    • Coming out of a relaxed state in a chair.

    • Walking into frame.

  • Have them deliver one strong line aimed at:

    • The viewer’s problem.

    • The name of the service.

    • The feeling they’ll get.

Get multiple takes:

  • Slightly different wording.

  • Different energy levels (more calm, more direct, more “urgent but warm”).

The outro / call to action

While they’re still comfortable and mic’d:

  • Record the CTA:

    • “Click below to book your first experience.”

    • “Book now to get your first session at [price].”

  • Get multiple versions:

    • Short, punchy version.

    • Slightly longer version that includes the offer.

You might not end up using a talking-head outro, but having it gives you options.

Recording the full script as audio (voiceover model)

Before you remove mics:

  1. Have them read the entire script like a voiceover.

  2. They don’t need to be on camera. This audio will be covered by B-roll.

  3. Direct them to:

    • Read naturally.

    • Pause between sentences.

    • If they mess up, restart from that sentence only.

Result: You now have a clean, complete narration track that describes the full experience and offer.

B-Roll Strategy (Visualizing the Script)

Start with movement shots

If you’re using a gimbal, slider, or any stabilizer:

  • Use it while it’s already set up.

  • Get your wider and medium moving shots first:

    • Establish the space.

    • Show the environment, décor, and overall vibe.

    • Track the customer entering, sitting, relaxing.

Cover the whole experience

Match B-roll to the script beats:

  1. Arrival / context

    • Exterior of the business.

    • Door, sign, logo.

    • Client walking in.

  2. Settle in

    • Chair reclining.

    • Weighted blanket, warm towels.

    • Close-ups of hands placing the blanket, adjusting the chair.

  3. Sensory elements

    • Essential oils, candles.

    • Steam or warm towels.

    • Close-ups of textures (fabrics, tools, bottles).

  4. Service in action

    • Hands working on feet, hands, neck, shoulders, head.

    • Different angles:

      • Over-the-shoulder.

      • Side profile.

      • Tight detail shots.

  5. Afterglow / exit

    • Client relaxed, smiling softly.

    • Standing up, stretching.

    • Walking out of the room or building.

Variety of shots

For each moment, aim for:

  • Wide shot – show the space and context.

  • Medium shot – show the interaction.

  • Close-up – show emotion and details.

  • Extreme close-up – tools, skin, water, oils, contact.

Get more than you think you need:

  • You want options to maintain pacing at 2–3 seconds per shot.

Handheld detail shots

If you’re going handheld:

  • Make the camera slightly heavier with a handle, cage, or monitor:

    • More mass = smoother motion.

  • Use three points of contact:

    • Camera/rig against your chest or shoulder.

    • Right hand on grip.

    • Left hand under lens/rig.

Direct your talent:

  • Move slower than feels natural.

  • Hold positions for longer.

  • Repeat the same action if needed so you can get multiple angles.

Editing the Commercial

Build the structure

  1. Drop your voiceover/full script audio into the timeline.

  2. Place your best hook take at the very beginning.

  3. Lay B-roll over the script so the visuals:

    • Follow the experience chronologically.

    • Show the steps as they’re being described.

Pacing

  • Aim for 2–3 seconds per shot on average.

  • Shorten shots to:

    • Keep energy up.

    • Avoid repetitive visuals.

If the ad feels slow:

  • Cut earlier.

  • Use tighter shots.

  • Drop redundant angles.

Choosing the ending

You have two main options:

  1. Talking-head CTA on screen:

    • Talent speaks directly to camera.

    • Text or logo appears beside/over them.

  2. Experience ending:

    • Show the client leaving relaxed.

    • Cut to logo and text with a CTA (no talking head).

Choose whichever feels more confident and on-brand for the business.

Delivering Both Horizontal and Vertical Versions

Shoot with both formats in mind

When framing on set:

  • Keep main action centered or slightly toward the middle vertically.

  • Avoid putting key information at the extreme edges:

    • It may get cropped out in vertical.

If your camera allows higher resolution or taller aspect capture (e.g., full sensor rather than strict 16:9):

  • Use that to give yourself extra room to reframe in post.

Edit workflow

  1. Primary edit in horizontal (16:9):

    • Build your main cut for web and YouTube.

    • Lock pacing and story here first.

  2. Duplicate for vertical (9:16):

    • Copy the full timeline.

    • Paste into a 9:16 sequence.

    • Reframe each shot:

      • Slide the frame up or down to keep faces and action centered vertically.

    • Avoid cropping off hands, faces, or key elements.

Captions and on-screen text

Especially for vertical (where people often watch muted):

  • Add captions for key spoken lines.

  • Use a clear, bold font when possible.

  • If the brand’s font is thin:

    • Add a soft black gradient bar behind the text at the bottom to improve legibility.

Keep text:

  • Short

  • High contrast

  • Easy to read instantly

Color and Finishing (Camera-Neutral Log Workflow)

If you shot in a log profile:

  1. Technical conversion

    • Apply an appropriate log-to-Rec.709 transform or LUT.

    • This gets you into a normal contrast and color space.

  2. Basic correction

    • Adjust exposure until skin tones look natural.

    • Fix color temperature (too warm / too cool).

    • Adjust tint (remove green/magenta shifts).

  3. Creative look

    • Optionally add a gentle creative LUT or grade:

      • Slight warmth for a spa.

      • A bit more contrast, but not harsh.

    • Dial back intensity so it supports, not dominates.

  4. Optional bloom / softness

    • Add a subtle glow/bloom on highlights if you want a more “filmic,” gentle feel.

    • Do not overdo it; the image still needs clarity.

Final check:

  • Does it still feel like a cozy, calm, relaxing space?

  • Can you clearly see the essential actions and expressions?

Business Outcome: Making the Ad Work

The end goal:

  • Viewers:

    • Understand the service.

    • Feel what it would be like to experience it.

    • Know exactly what to do next.

The client cares about:

  • Clicks

  • Bookings

  • Revenue

So make sure the ad:

  • Clearly states the offer.

  • Clearly states the action (“Click below,” “Book now,” etc.).

  • Feels trustworthy and aligned with the brand.
