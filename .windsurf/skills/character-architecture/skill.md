# CHARACTER ARCHITECTURE

Characters are not people. They are machines built to prove or disprove a thesis. Every design decision — flaw, arc, telling detail, speech pattern, relationship — flows from the story's Controlling Idea. Build backward from the thesis. Build forward only once the architecture is sound.

**Sources:** McKee, *Story*; Walley, *Turn & Burn*; Garland/UCA character engineering; Trottier, *The Screenwriter's Bible*.

---

## The Design Order (Non-Negotiable)

```
STEP 1: STATE THE CONTROLLING IDEA
         (VALUE + CAUSE — McKee)
              ↓
STEP 2: BUILD THE PROTAGONIST'S FLAW AS ITS ANTITHESIS
         (Walley — flaw is the misconception the story destroys)
              ↓
STEP 3: MAP THE ARC ENDPOINT
         (positive / tragic / irony arc)
              ↓
STEP 4: POPULATE THE CAST AS A POLARIZATION NETWORK
         (McKee — each role must contrast at least one other)
              ↓
STEP 5: ASSIGN TELLING DETAILS
         (Garland — visual shorthand that replaces exposition)
              ↓
STEP 6: BUILD THE WORLD OF THE STORY QUESTIONS
         (McKee — character only makes sense inside their world)
```

Never reverse this order. A character without a flaw is an action figure. A cast without polarization is a focus group.

---

## Step 1 — Controlling Idea (Review)

State it before touching character design: *"VALUE is achieved/lost through CAUSE."*

This sentence tells you what the protagonist must learn (the value), how (the cause), and therefore what their flaw must be (the inverse of the cause). Every character in the cast is positioned relative to this sentence.

---

## Step 2 — The Flaw

### Definition
The flaw is not a character weakness (shy, clumsy, impatient). It is a **misconception about life** — a false belief the protagonist holds that is the antithesis of the story's thesis. The story is a machine that tests this belief under maximum pressure until it breaks or destroys the protagonist.

| Arc Type | What Happens to the Flaw |
|----------|-------------------------|
| **Positive arc** | Protagonist recognizes the flaw and corrects it. The story proves the Controlling Idea. |
| **Tragic arc** | Protagonist never corrects the flaw, or corrects it too late. The story proves the Controlling Idea by negative example. |
| **Irony/Flat arc** | Protagonist's flaw is correct — the world is wrong. The protagonist changes the world, not themselves. (Rare. Requires genre awareness.) |

### Flaw Construction Method (Walley)
1. State the Controlling Idea as a thesis: *"Survival is achieved through refusing the question that erases you."*
2. Build the flaw as its antithesis: *"The protagonist believes documentation is the same as presence — that witnessing something through a lens counts as being there."*
3. The story must test this belief in the most difficult possible situation. Whatever you choose as the story's pressure — that is what cracks the flaw open.

### Walley's Five-Stage Arc
Map the protagonist's movement from flaw through to resolution:

| Stage | Function | Story Question |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| **Yearn** | Stasis + desire. The protagonist is comfortable but incomplete. The flaw is invisible to them. | What do they want? What do they not yet know they need? |
| **Turn** | The antagonistic force activates. The tipping point. Comfortable stasis ends. | What forces them to move? |
| **Burn** | Escalation. The protagonist adapts, fails, adapts again. Each failure harder. Point of no return crossed. | What is the worst moment before the revelation? |
| **Learn** | The revelation. The flaw is revealed — either to the protagonist, to the audience, or both. | What does the protagonist finally understand? |
| **Earn** | The confrontation with truth. The protagonist acts from their new understanding. The Controlling Idea is proved or disproved. | What do they do with what they've learned? |

---

## Step 3 — Arc Endpoint

Before writing a single scene, know which type of arc you are writing. The arc type determines:
- Which scenes are necessary (and which can be cut)
- Where the revelation lands in the sequence map
- Whether the final image is hope, tragedy, or irony

**Positive arc final image:** The protagonist in a world remade by their corrected understanding. They are different. The image reflects this.

**Tragic arc final image:** The protagonist destroyed by the flaw they refused to correct — or the world destroyed around them. The image reflects the cost.

**Flat arc final image:** The world changed. The protagonist, unbroken, moves forward into a world now shaped by their truth.

## Step 4 — Polarization: Cast as Network

### The McKee Principle
"The first principle of cast design is polarization." Between roles, devise a network of contrasting or contradictory attitudes, values, and approaches. Characters who agree cannot generate drama. They can debate, but they cannot conflict.

### The Polarization Map
For every script, build this map before assigning scenes:

| Character | Position on Central Conflict | What They Believe About the Controlling Idea's Value | What They Fear |
|-----------|-----------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|----------------|
| Protagonist | | | |
| Antagonist | | | |
| Supporting 1 | | | |
| Supporting 2 | | | |

Every character must occupy a **different position** on the conflict axis. If two characters share a position, ask: which one is doing the work the other could also do? The answer is the character to cut or redesign.

### Cast Failure Modes
- **Exposition-only characters:** Exist to tell the audience something. Cut them. Move the information to the environment, a prop, or an action.
- **Agreement pairs:** Two characters who share a worldview with no meaningful distinction. Merge them or polarize them.
- **Undefined antagonism:** The antagonist has no coherent worldview. An antagonist who is simply evil is not an antagonist — they are a plot device. The antagonist must be *right from their own perspective.*
- **Satellite characters:** Support the protagonist without any independent desire of their own. Every character must want something, even if it's only a glass of water. (Mamet)

Each crew member falls to their own primary instrument. The entity does not attack — it amplifies. What you use to feel safe becomes the weapon. The two who resist (Reyes, Marshall) don't survive. They just close the door.

## Step 5 — Telling Details

### The Principle (Garland/UCA)
Visual shorthand that replaces exposition. A telling detail is a physical behavior, object, or habit that tells the audience everything they need to know about a character without a word of explanation.

**Rule:** Three telling details per character in the first appearance. After that, the audience fills in the rest.

### Categories of Telling Details

| Category | What It Reveals | Example |
|----------|----------------|---------|
| **Object relationship** | What the character values, fears, or hides | Keiko adjusts the camera frame before adjusting her smile |
| **Spatial behavior** | How they occupy space — confident, contracted, aggressive, withdrawn | Aya presses the notebook to her chest. Facing the village. Eyes open. Nothing moves in them. |
| **Habitual action** | What they do automatically, without thinking | Aya reaches for the railing with her left hand before she needs it, steps over the third step without looking down |
| **Voice quality** | Not content — pitch, weight, rhythm, hesitation | Aya's voice: same pitch, different weight. Older than her. |
| **Relational behavior** | How they handle the gap between what they feel and what they show | Keiko forces a laugh for the camera. Smile a fraction less certain. |

### What Telling Details Are NOT
- Backstory delivered verbally
- Personality adjectives in action lines ("nervously," "awkwardly," "sadly")
- Props that exist only once and are never paid off
- Physical descriptions that tell us how the character looks but not who they are

## Step 6 — World of the Story (McKee, Ch. 8)

Characters cannot be designed in isolation. They are products of their world. Before finalizing any character, answer these questions about the world they inhabit:

**Period:** When does the story take place? What does this period mean for how people move, speak, think?

**Duration:** How much time passes? What is the rate of change?

**Location:** Where precisely — country, city, district, building? What are the physical rules of this space?

**Conflict level:** Where does this story sit on McKee's five levels?
1. Inner conflict (psychomachia — the conflict within)
2. Personal conflict (between individuals, intimate)
3. Social conflict (between individual and institution)
4. Environmental conflict (between character and physical world)
5. Cosmic conflict (between character and supernatural/metaphysical forces)

*KURASAWA operates at levels 1, 2, and 5 simultaneously. Keiko's inner conflict (documentation vs. presence) is the level-1 engine; her relationship with Aya is level 2; the supernatural force of Tanaka and the village is level 5. This stacking is what makes the horror psychological rather than atmospheric.*

**Moral field:** What is legal and what is illegal in this world? What is moral and what is immoral? How do these overlap or diverge? Characters who break the moral field of their world are the most interesting to watch.

**Genre:** What does this genre promise the audience? What emotions did they come for? What are the genre's mandatory conventions (what must be present) and its forbidden elements (what would betray the audience)?

## Character Diagnosis Protocol

When diagnosing an existing character in a draft (used during `/script-diagnosis` Pass 3):

1. **Flaw test:** Can you state the flaw in one sentence? Is it a misconception, not a weakness?
2. **Arc test:** At what point does the flaw break or confirm? Is the Learn moment dramatized or implied?
3. **Polarization test:** Map this character against every other character. Does any pair share a worldview position without meaningful distinction?
4. **Telling details test:** Strip out all the character's dialogue. What remains? Does the action alone reveal this person?
5. **Exposition test:** Does this character say things only to inform the audience? Remove those lines. What is lost? If nothing — the character was doing the audience's job, not their own.
6. **World test:** Would this character exist and behave the same way in a different world? If yes, they haven't been built from their world — they've been imported into it.
