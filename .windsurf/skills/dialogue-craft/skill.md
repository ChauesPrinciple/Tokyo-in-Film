# DIALOGUE CRAFT

Dialogue is the last layer applied to a scene, never the first. A scene must work in silence before a single line is written. This skill governs the craft of converting silence into the minimum necessary words — and making those words carry more than their surface meaning.

**Sources:** Walley, *Turn & Burn*; Trottier, *The Screenwriter's Bible*; McKee, *Story*; Burnett/Garland/UCA integrated methodology.

---

## The Hierarchy of Dialogue Decisions

Dialogue operates at four levels. Each level depends on the one above it — do not work at a lower level until the upper levels are sound.

```
LEVEL 1: NECESSITY — Does this scene need dialogue at all?
    ↓
LEVEL 2: SUBTEXT — What is being said underneath the words?
    ↓
LEVEL 3: VOICE — Does each character sound like only themselves?
    ↓
LEVEL 4: POLISH — Is every word earning its place?
```

---

## Level 1: Necessity Test

Before writing any dialogue, answer these three questions:

1. **Does this scene already work in the silent draft?** If yes, the default position is: do not add dialogue. Dialogue is added only to fill a gap that action, environment, and props cannot fill.
2. **What specific story information or character revelation requires words?** Name it. If you cannot name the specific payload the line delivers, the line has no purpose.
3. **Could this information be conveyed through action, a prop, a visual detail, or an environmental cue instead?** If yes → use the visual version. If no → write the line.

**The principle:** Every line of dialogue is a failure of the image. The image should do everything it can before the mouth opens. When the mouth opens, it should say what the image cannot.

### Maximum Dialogue Density by Format
- **3-5 min short:** 2-3 lines per scene maximum. Most scenes should be wordless.
- **5-10 min short:** 3-5 lines per scene. Dialogue scenes are the minority.
- **10-20 min short:** 5-8 lines per scene. One or two dialogue-heavy scenes permitted if they earn it.
- **20-30 min short:** Feature rhythm, but tighter. Challenge any scene over 6 exchanges.
- **Feature:** Governed by genre and story demands. Even here, challenge every line.

## Level 2: Subtext

### The Core Principle
Characters almost never say what they mean. They talk around the truth because:
- They don't know their own feelings (common, especially early in an arc)
- They know but won't admit it (pride, fear, strategy)
- They're testing the other person (power dynamics, trust)
- The social context forbids directness (culture, hierarchy, audience)
- They're lying (deception is subtext by definition)

Only at moments of extreme crisis — the Breaking Point — does a character say exactly what they mean. This is why those moments land: they're the exception.

### The Subtext Translation Method (Walley)

For every line of dialogue, write two versions:

| Direct (what they mean) | Subtext (what they say) |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| "I'm angry you left" | "Your plant died. I kept watering it." |
| "I want you to stay" | "Traffic's terrible this time of day." |
| "I love you" | "Do you remember why we started this?" |
| "You hurt me" | "I had a dog once. Ran away. Never got another." |
| "I'm afraid of dying" | "Have you ever been to the ocean?" |
| "I don't trust you" | "Lock the door on your way out." |
| "Please forgive me" | "I made your favorite. It's in the fridge." |

**The mechanism:** The subtext line works by *displacement* — it moves the emotional weight to an object, a memory, or an unrelated topic. The audience reads the real meaning through the gap between what is said and what is felt.

### When Directness Is Correct
Direct dialogue earns its place only when:
1. The character has reached their **Breaking Point** (the flaw cracks open)
2. The directness itself is the **dramatic event** (a confession, an accusation that changes everything)
3. The genre demands it (courtroom cross-examination, military commands)
4. Indirectness would be **unbelievable** in context (a doctor delivering a diagnosis)

If none of these conditions apply, translate to subtext.

### The Chess Analogy (Walley)
Subtext-rich dialogue operates like chess. Each line is simultaneously:
- A **move** (advances the speaker's objective)
- A **reveal** (shows the audience something the characters may not see)
- A **trap** (sets up the next move, which the other character must respond to)

A line that does only one of these is thin. A line that does all three is craft.

## Level 3: Voice Architecture

### The Differentiation Principle
Every character must sound like only themselves. Voice is not accent or slang — it is the architecture of how a person constructs their thoughts in words.

### Voice Components

| Component | What It Means | Example Contrast |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Sentence length** | How much a character says per turn | Keiko: short, clipped. Aya: flowing, multi-clause. |
| **Vocabulary register** | Formal vs. informal, technical vs. colloquial | A scientist uses precise terms. A street kid uses concrete nouns. |
| **Rhythm** | Staccato vs. legato, interrupted vs. complete | One character always finishes sentences. Another never does. |
| **Deflection pattern** | How they avoid the truth | One character uses humor. Another changes the subject. Another goes silent. |
| **Confrontation style** | How they fight | One attacks with questions. Another with statements. Another by leaving. |
| **Information control** | How much they reveal and when | One character over-explains. Another withholds. |

### The Isolation Test (Walley)
Extract all of Character A's lines and read them in sequence, stripped of context. Then do the same for Character B.

Ask:
- Could any of A's lines be spoken by B without the audience noticing?
- If yes → those lines are voice-undifferentiated. Rewrite them.
- Do A's lines, read alone, sound like a consistent person with a recognizable pattern?
- If no → A's voice is not yet built.

### The Voice Mimicry Method (Walley)
When building a character's voice for the first time, cast a known voice in your head. Not for their personality — for their *speech pattern*:
- Samuel L. Jackson: declarative, rhythmic, builds to emphasis
- Sandra Bullock: conversational, self-deprecating, overlaps thoughts
- Jack Nicholson: deliberate, menacing warmth, pauses as weapons
- Jimmy Stewart: halting, honest, builds certainty through uncertainty

This is a starting point, not a destination. The character's voice will diverge as their specifics take over.

## Level 4: Polish

### Exposition Management

**The cardinal sin:** A character tells another character something they both already know, for the sole benefit of the audience.

- "As you know, we've been working on this project for three years..." — NEVER.
- "Remember when you said you'd never go back to that place?" — Only if the *remembering* is the dramatic action, not the information delivery.

**Exposition rules:**
1. Delay it. The audience will tolerate not knowing far longer than writers think. Curiosity is an asset.
2. Distribute it. One piece per scene, embedded in conflict. Never dump.
3. Make it cost something. The best exposition is revealed when a character is forced to admit it against their will (interrogation, argument, betrayal).
4. Show don't tell. If the information can be conveyed by a photograph on a mantel, a scar, a limp, a tattoo, a locked room — use the image.

**Trottier's flashback alternative:** If you need backstory, find a way to make it a mystery in the present. In *Star Trek II*, Kirk's handling of the Kobiashi Maru isn't shown in flashback — it's turned into a present-tense mystery that drives the plot. The audience *wants* to know. That's better than being told.

### Scene Culmination (Trottier)

The final line of a dialogue scene must be its strongest. This is non-negotiable for two reasons:
1. The last thing the audience hears carries into the cut — it colors the transition.
2. A trailing scene (the dialogue winds down, characters say goodbye, small talk follows the conflict) bleeds away the energy the scene built.

**Test:** Cover the last three lines of the scene. Read the scene ending on the fourth-from-last line. Is it stronger? If yes, cut the last three lines.

**The *Some Like It Hot* principle (Trottier):** The final line of the film is "Well, nobody's perfect." Every line before it builds toward it. The scene's architecture creates an ascending series of attempts/failures that make the final line inevitable and surprising at the same time. The best final lines feel like the only possible conclusion *and* a complete surprise.

### The Density Test (Burnett/Garland)

For every line, answer YES or NO:

| Question | Y/N |
|----------|-----|
| Does this line advance the plot? | |
| Does this line reveal character? | |
| Could the same meaning be conveyed visually? | |
| Is this information stated elsewhere in the script? | |
| Does this line serve the controlling idea? | |

- A line that answers NO to the first two questions should be cut.
- A line that answers YES to the third question should be replaced with the visual version.
- A line that answers YES to the fourth question is redundant — cut the weaker version.
- A line that answers NO to all five questions is dead weight.

## The Unfilmable Question (Walley)

"Unfilmables" — character thoughts, emotions, internal states written as prose — are not forbidden. They are a tool.

**When to use them:**
- When giving an actor the character's internal state produces a better performance than dictating physical mannerisms. *"Jane's worried he's about to fire her"* serves the actor better than *"Jane's eyes bulge and nostrils flare as she grips the edge of her desk."*
- When the internal description makes the script a more vivid read (spec scripts are read before they are produced — prose quality matters).
- When the alternative is so physically specific that it restricts the director's choices.

**When not to use them:**
- When the physical description IS the storytelling (a horror film where the physical response is the content)
- When overused — every action line becoming a novel paragraph
- When it replaces actual scene construction (describing emotion instead of building a scene that produces it)

**The hack (Walley):** If *"Jane's worried he's about to fire her"* feels too internal, change it to *"Jane looks worried he's about to fire her."* Same information, visual context. Nobody with production experience will reject this.

**The Bourne Identity example (Walley):** *"Those words. The way he said it. She's grabbing her purse, clearing out of the room. Slamming the door behind her. Click. It's locked."* — Tony Gilroy, Academy Award nominee. This is unfilmable prose that is also extraordinary screenwriting. Good prose is good prose.

## Dialogue Diagnosis Protocol

When diagnosing dialogue in an existing draft (invoked by `/script-diagnosis` Pass 5):

### Step 1: Subtext Audit
Read every line. Mark each as:
- **S** = subtext (character is not saying what they mean)
- **D** = direct (character says exactly what they mean)
- **E** = exposition (line exists to inform the audience)

Calculate the ratio. A healthy script should be 60-80% S, 10-20% D (at crisis/breaking points), and under 10% E. High E = rewrite priority.

### Step 2: Voice Differentiation
Run the Isolation Test for every character with more than 5 lines. Flag any character pair whose lines could swap.

### Step 3: Culmination Check
For every scene with dialogue, identify the final line. Is it the strongest? If not, identify which earlier line IS the strongest and recommend cutting everything after it.

### Step 4: Density Sweep
Run the Density Test on every line. Produce a cut list: lines that fail all criteria, ranked by scene.

### Step 5: Talking Heads Check (Trottier)
Flag any scene where two or more characters are stationary and talking without action, movement, environmental interaction, or visual business. For each flagged scene, suggest:
- A location change mid-scene
- Physical action that complements the dialogue
- A prop or environmental element that adds subtext

## Decision Tree: Writing Dialogue for a New Scene

```
SCENE WORKS IN SILENCE?
  │
  ├─ YES → Do not add dialogue unless:
  │         → A specific information payload cannot be visual
  │         → A character's voice IS the content (confession, threat, seduction)
  │         → Genre demands it (courtroom, negotiation)
  │
  └─ NO → Identify what is missing:
           │
           ├─ Missing information → Can it be a prop, a visual, a reaction?
           │    ├─ YES → Use visual. Do not add dialogue.
           │    └─ NO → Write the line. Apply subtext translation.
           │
           ├─ Missing character revelation → Is this a crisis moment?
           │    ├─ YES → Direct dialogue may be correct (Breaking Point)
           │    └─ NO → Subtext. The character reveals through evasion.
           │
           └─ Missing conflict → The problem is not dialogue — the scene is PASTO-broken.
                                 Return to scene design. Fix the structure first.
```
