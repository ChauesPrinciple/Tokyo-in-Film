# NARRATIVE STRUCTURE

## Core Architecture: Eight-Sequence Structure

Every feature-length screenplay (96 pages) maps to eight sequences of approximately 12 pages each. For a 26-minute short, the sequences compress but the architecture holds.

| Sequence | Pages (Feature) | Minutes (26-min short) | Function |
|----------|-----------------|------------------------|----------|
| 1 | 1-12 | 0:00-3:15 | Status quo establishment, inciting incident |
| 2 | 13-24 | 3:15-6:30 | Protagonist commits to goal |
| 3 | 25-36 | 6:30-9:45 | First obstacles, early failures |
| 4 | 37-48 | 9:45-13:00 | Midpoint reversal — everything changes |
| 5 | 49-60 | 13:00-16:15 | New approach, false progress |
| 6 | 61-72 | 16:15-19:30 | Crisis — everything falls apart |
| 7 | 73-84 | 19:30-22:45 | Climax — final confrontation |
| 8 | 85-96 | 22:45-26:00 | Resolution — new equilibrium or destruction |

## Three-Act Architecture

**Act One** (Sequences 1-2): Establish world, characters, stakes. End with lock-in — protagonist cannot turn back.

**Act Two** (Sequences 3-6): Progressive complication. Every obstacle must be worse than the last. The midpoint (end of Seq 4) is NOT a rest — it's a reversal that raises stakes irreversibly.

**Act Three** (Sequences 7-8): Crisis → Climax → Resolution. The climax must be the MOST difficult moment. Resolution shows the cost.

## Action Line Prose Discipline — Mandatory Pass

**Applies to ALL screenplay output. Non-negotiable. Run before delivering any draft.**

A screenplay action line describes what a camera can see or a microphone can hear. Nothing else. The following are **banned**:

| # | Violation | Example | Fix |
|---|-----------|---------|-----|
| 1 | Internal monologue ("He thinks:") | "He thinks: this time I am doing the right thing and it will kill everyone anyway." | Externalize through action, dialogue, or prop — or delete. |
| 2 | Thesis statements | "The fear of being singular is stronger than the fear of the thing itself." | Delete. If the theme works, the audience finds it. If not, stating it doesn't fix it. |
| 3 | Author commentary | "He is doing what he believes is right. This is not a comfort." | Cut the evaluation sentence. |
| 4 | Narrating unspoken thoughts | "Neither of them says: this is the entity using our caution against us." | Delete. The silence IS the content. |
| 5 | Over-precise emotional direction | "The gentle frustration of someone who believes they are watching another person make a serious mistake." | Simplify: "not anger" or "something gentler." Let the actor work. |
| 6 | Psychology in action lines | "Marshall handles uncertainty by making everything else certain first." | Show the behavior. Cut the explanation. |
| 7 | Second-person address | "The way you look at something you're trying to memorize." | Rephrase third-person or cut. |

**The single test:** Can a camera see it or a microphone hear it? YES → keep. NO → production notes or delete.

**Where thematic material goes:** Production notes, companion documents, collaborator briefs. Not the screenplay body.

## McKee Frameworks

### The Five-Part Story Design
Every story is a design in five parts: **Inciting Incident → Progressive Complications → Crisis → Climax → Resolution**.
- The **Inciting Incident** is the primary cause for everything that follows. It radically upsets the balance of forces in the protagonist's life. Locate it first — if you cannot, the story has no engine.
- **Progressive Complications** are not random obstacles. Each one must be more difficult than the last, because the protagonist adapts. The antagonistic forces must also adapt — escalating pressure, not repeating pressure.
- The **Crisis** is the moment of greatest risk and decision: the protagonist must choose between two irreconcilable goods, or the lesser of two evils. No easy path exists.
- The **Climax** is the scene of maximum energy, where the crisis decision produces an irreversible change of value.
- The **Resolution** shows the world remade by that change. It is not an epilogue — it is proof.

### Controlling Idea
Every story has one sentence that captures what it says about how and why life changes. Format: VALUE + CAUSE.
- Example: "Survival is achieved through the courage to face the unknowable."
- ON THE EDGE controlling idea: "Humanity's drive to understand the cosmos costs us everything we are."

### The Gap
A gap opens between character expectation and actual result. The character expects Action A to produce Result B. Instead, Reality produces Result C — unexpected, usually worse. The gap is where drama lives. Every meaningful scene must contain a gap.

If a scene cannot open a gap, it has no conflict. A scene without conflict is a scene without a scene.

### Cast Design — Polarization
The first principle of cast design is polarization. Between roles, devise a network of contrasting or contradictory attitudes, values, and approaches. Characters who fundamentally agree cannot generate drama. In ON THE EDGE: the rationalist scientist vs. the intuitive observer vs. the mission pragmatist — each reads the same evidence differently, producing conflict without a villain.

### Archplot
The classic story form: single protagonist, continuous timeline, causally connected events, closed ending, active protagonist, consistent reality. ON THE EDGE follows Archplot with horror genre inflection — the reality is not consistent, it is increasingly compromised.

### Subtext
Nothing a character says directly reveals what they feel. Characters talk around what they mean. The audience reads between lines. The gap between dialogue and action is subtext.

### Life Story vs. The Story Told
"The mark of a master is to select only a few moments but give us a lifetime." A character's life encompasses encyclopedic possibility. The story told is a ruthless selection from that life. Every scene included must justify the exclusion of everything it omits. Start the story at the last possible moment — just before change becomes unavoidable.

## Scene Design — McKee's Core Definition

A scene is *"a story in miniature — an action through conflict in a unity or continuity of time and space that turns the value-charged condition of a character's life."*

Four components every scene must contain:
- **Turning Point** — the moment the value charge shifts (hope → despair, safety → danger, power → powerlessness, or their reversals)
- **Setup/Payoff** — something planted earlier in the scene (or script) that detonates here
- **Emotional Dynamics** — the arc of feeling across the scene, from entry state to exit state
- **Choice** — the character must decide something, even if the decision is to do nothing

**Value Charge Test:** What value is at stake when the scene opens (hope, fear, certainty, love, power)? What is that value's state when the scene closes? If it hasn't shifted, the scene accomplished nothing — rewrite it or cut it.

## Scene Craft Rules — Trottier, *The Screenwriter's Bible*

- Every scene moves the story forward in **both plot AND character**. Plot movement without character revelation is mechanical. Character revelation without plot movement is indulgent.
- **Start as close to the end of the scene as possible.** Cut the approach, the arrival, the small talk. Enter at the first moment of conflict or its immediate precondition.
- **Scenes should culminate dramatically** — in a decision, a reversal, a revelation, or a punch line. The final line of a dialogue scene should be its strongest.
- **Avoid talking heads.** Move dialogue through action and space. The argument at breakfast continues in the car and concludes on the tennis court — each location adds characterization, pacing contrast, and visual interest.
- **Pace via peaks and valleys.** Action scene → dialogue scene. Heavy scene → light scene. The peaks must ascend toward the climax; without valleys, the peaks disappear.
- **Flashbacks**: ~95% of spec script flashbacks don't work because they interrupt the present rather than motivate it. Use a flashback only if it moves the story forward. Quick flashes are safest. Never enter a flashback until the audience cares about the present.

## Scene Placement Decision Protocol

Before placing a scene, answer:
1. **Which sequence does this belong to?** (1-8)
2. **What is its function?** (opening hook / escalation / pivot / crisis / resolution)
3. **What is the gap?** (expectation vs. result — if there's no gap, there's no scene)
4. **What is the value charge entering? What is it leaving?** (if unchanged, cut or rewrite)
5. **Does it advance BOTH plot AND character?**
6. **What is the final beat — is it the strongest moment in the scene?**

If a scene cannot answer all six questions, it does not belong in the screenplay.

## Tension Management

Tension is not constant high intensity — it is contrast. Pattern: tension builds → brief release → higher tension → release withheld → breaking point.

The release points in horror are crucial. A laugh, a moment of warmth, a small victory — these make the next horror hit harder. If every scene is at maximum fear, the audience goes numb.

**ON THE EDGE tension map:**
- Seq 1-2: Professional competence (release) → first anomaly (tension)
- Seq 3-4: Anomaly confirmed (tension) → debate/argument (release through human conflict) → irreversible contact (maximum tension, no release)
- Seq 5-6: False normalcy attempt (brief release) → psychological fracture begins (tension builds without release)
- Seq 7-8: Full disintegration (no release) → single survivor (resolution, not relief)

## Scene-Level Structure — PASTO

From Kenneth MacGowan's *Primer of Playwriting* (1951), adapted by CJ Walley for screenwriting. Every scene contains five internal beats:

| Beat | Function |
|------|----------|
| **Preparation** | Stakes established; protagonist's objective declared (may be implicit) |
| **Attack** | Protagonist actively pursues the objective |
| **Struggle** | Resistance encountered; the simple path closes |
| **Turnaround** | Something unexpected shifts the dynamic — new information, new force, reversal |
| **Outcome** | New state of play; the conditions for the next scene's Preparation |

**The causality rule:** The Outcome of one scene IS the Preparation of the next. A string of scenes without this causal chain is a list of events. A causal chain is a story.

**ON THE EDGE PASTO application** (Sequence 3, Anomaly Confirmed scene):
- Preparation: crew expects the star's behavior to match existing anomaly models
- Attack: lead scientist runs every known explanation
- Struggle: each explanation fits one aspect but breaks three others
- Turnaround: the data pattern matches no natural phenomenon — but it matches a purposeful one
- Outcome: the mission changes from observation to contact protocol

## Protagonist Architecture — Walley, *Turn & Burn*

The protagonist is a vehicle for the story's thesis, not its origin. Build the arc backward from the thesis:

1. **Identify the thesis** — the life truth the story proves (the Controlling Idea reframed as a question: *does this truth hold?*)
2. **Build the flaw as antithesis** — the protagonist's misconception about life that the story will destroy
3. **Map the arc**: **Yearn** (stasis, false comfort + desire for more) → **Turn** (antagonistic force triggered, tipping point) → **Burn** (escalating conflict, point of no return) → **Learn** (revelation, changed understanding) → **Earn** (confrontation with truth, acceptance of the life truth proven)

Heroes do not have to be likeable. They must be relatable. Relatability comes from values, not lifestyle — the audience must recognize the protagonist's *want*, even if they would never share their choices.

## Act I Architecture — Tomlinson, *Plot Basics*

The first act must deliver three elements before the Major Dramatic Question is posed:

- **Desire** — what the protagonist wants (this may be shallow initially; it will deepen)
- **Danger** — what stands in the way (the antagonistic force, obstacle, or condition)
- **Decision** — the protagonist chooses to act despite the risk (this is the threshold into Act II)

**The fractal principle:** Structure repeats at every scale — story, act, sequence, scene, beat. Each unit has its own beginning (setup), middle (complication), and end (decision/consequence). A well-structured beat implies a well-structured scene. A well-structured scene implies a well-structured sequence.

**Timing:** Begin just before change enters the protagonist's life. Start too early → the audience waits for the story to start. Start at the moment of change → the audience has no one to care about yet. Start after the change → you'll spend the whole film in flashback.

## Subplot Integration

Subplots must mirror or complicate the main plot thematically. They are NOT distractions — they provide the release valves that make the main plot bearable.

In ON THE EDGE: The interpersonal tensions between crew members (who trusts whom, who breaks first) are the subplot. They mirror the main plot (humanity vs. incomprehensible cosmos) at human scale. Each subplot beat must rhyme with a main plot beat.

## 空の美 (KURASAWA) Sequence Map — Definitive Edition

**Title:** 空の美 / Kara no Bi / "Empty Beauty"
**Controlling Idea:** The honest answer is the only defense against the question that consumes you — but honesty costs the identity you built, and someone already uploaded it.
**Inciting Incident:** The school bell rings from a tower that has been rubble for fifty-five years (Seq 2). The acoustic rules of the world change.
**Dual Protagonist:** Aya (primary arc — vanity to honesty) and Keiko (secondary arc — transactionalism recognized, not corrected).
**Major Dramatic Question:** Will Aya be consumed by the question she has been answering performatively for three years, or will she find an honest answer?
**Arc Types:** Aya = positive (answers honestly, survives, loses identity). Keiko = negative recognition (sees what she is, the knowing doesn't stop her). Riku = tragic (consumed at first encounter). ## ON THE EDGE Sequence Map — Definitive Edition

**Title:** On The Edge
**Controlling Idea:** Dignity in resistance — the entity is not defeated, it is inconvenienced; the cost of closing one door is everything, and the reward is that someone else won't walk through it.
**Inciting Incident:** The decay pattern in sector nine shifts orientation toward the Explorer III at 0340 (Day 47). Not response — regard.
**Dual Protagonist:** Reyes (primary arc — epistemological integrity) and Marshall (secondary arc — physical executor of someone else's understanding).
**Major Dramatic Question:** Can they close the door before the entity reads them completely — and is closing it worth dying for?
**Arc Types:** Reyes = positive (deletes the proof that isn't hers, does the math herself, chooses integrity over certainty). Marshall = sacrificial executor (destroys the relay with his hands because "it's a physical task and I need to do something with my hands"). Tanaka = willing→redeemed (agrees, is used, uses his access to tell Reyes how to kill it — his last lucid act against the thing he agreed to serve). Costa = tragic prophet (predicts everything, is contained for it, stops predicting, dies). Carter = logical extremist (removes his eyes because observation is the mechanism — addresses root cause).
