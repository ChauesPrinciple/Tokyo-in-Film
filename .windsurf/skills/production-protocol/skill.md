# PRODUCTION PROTOCOL

Sources: `[Filmmaking:MakingAFilm]` is the spine of this skill (crew hierarchy throughout; 2nd AD and call sheets at `p.130`; sandbag/light stand safety at `p.224`); `[JCam:InstructorGuide p.14]` (call sheet glossary entry) and `[p.19]` (AD/AC/DP roles glossary); `[DigitalFilmmaking p.88]` (Hughes safety tips with verbatim sandbag protocol).

## Crew Hierarchy

### The Director
Envisions the program. Controls crew activities. Works with actors to achieve cohesive, properly portrayed performances. The artistic and technical authority. Must be both right-brained (artistic vision, actor direction) and left-brained (technical discussion with DOP, lighting department, sound department). No one approaches the director with suggestions except the DOP and, sometimes, the 1st AD.

### Assistant Directors (ADs)

**1st AD**: The director's operational voice. Runs the set. Calls the command chain. Ensures lighting and sound are staged, cast and crew are ready, and the set is quiet before the shot rolls. If the director is the brain, the 1st AD is the nervous system. `[JCam:InstructorGuide p.19]` (AD glossary).

**2nd AD**: Positions technical personnel and actors. Ensures people are where they need to be. Manages two-way radio communications. Choreographs extras and background. Calls "Lock it up for picture!" over radio to the 3rd AD. `[Filmmaking:MakingAFilm p.130]` ("I liken a second A.D. to an executive assistant... Some of the second A.D.'s paperwork includes handling call sheets, collecting from the camera department the camera reports... collecting talent releases for background players").

**3rd AD**: Works off-set. Controls public traffic and noise outside the shooting area. When "Lock it up" comes over radio, the 3rd AD ensures no external noise contaminates the take. Escorts actors on/off set, to/from makeup and costume. `[JCam:InstructorGuide p.19]` ("Depending on the size of the crew, there may even be a 3rd AD").

### Director of Photography (DOP) / Cinematographer
Head of camera department. Designs ALL lighting for the set — but does not place the lights. Decides camera, lens, framing, movement for each shot (in agreement with the director). The DOP's relationship with the director is the most important creative relationship on set.

### Camera Operator
Operates the camera under the DOP's direction. Practices panning, tilting, dollying, trucking, zooming to DOP specification. Calls "Framed!" when ready.

### Camera Assistants
**1st AC**: Pulls focus during complex moves. Before shot: marks required focus settings on lens with white camera tape, rehearses timing with camera operator. During shot: adjusts focus ring precisely. Also manages media (film/tape/cards), labels and cares for shot media, keeps camera report.

**2nd AC (Clapper/Loader)**: Fills in the slate (roll, scene, take, director, camera, date). Holds slate in front of rolling camera, calls out scene/shot/take number. Snaps the clapper stick (for film; optional for video). Hastily exits the frame. On film productions: loads raw stock into magazines.

### Gaffer
Head of lighting/electrics department. Licensed electrician. Cares for all lighting equipment. Ensures correct fixtures, accessories, and adequate power. Directs lighting grips to mount, focus, and trim lights to DOP's specifications. Carries gaff tape (also called gaffer's tape).

### Best Boy
Second in command to the gaffer. First assistant in the lighting/electrics department. The term has spread — "best boy" in any department means second in command.

### Grips
Move, set up, and operate equipment. Four types:
- **Lighting grips**: Execute gaffer's lighting positions
- **Camera grips**: Care for and move camera equipment
- **Dolly grips**: Operate the camera dolly during shots; lay dolly track
- **Set grips**: Build and dismantle set pieces

### Sound Recordist
Selects microphones and recording equipment. Records dialogue and ambient sound. Works with boom operator(s). On video: one person may suffice. On film: typically two — boom operator (holds the mic) and recordist (runs the mixer/recorder).

### Production Assistant (PA)
Odd jobs, errands, shooting schedule tracking, script assistance ("script assistant" capacity). The most junior crew position, with the most varied responsibilities.

### Continuity Person (Script Supervisor)
Tracks exactly what has been shot. Notes: actor costume state, prop positions, hair/makeup, which lines were said, which camera angles have been covered. Prevents continuity errors between shots of the same scene shot at different times. Also called script supervisor.

### Unit Manager
Manages logistics: schedules, budget, salaries, transportation, accommodation (if location shoot), catering, facilities. Reports to the producer, not the director.

### Talent and Extras
**Talent**: Actors — people who assume a fictional identity and play it as real. May use specialty performers (actual reporters playing TV reporters, etc.).
**Extras (atmosphere people)**: Background figures with no lines, no overt physical acting. Repeat actions over and over for different angles. They ARE the background; they make the world feel populated.

## The Command Chain

Every shot follows this exact sequence. Know it cold.

```
1. [2nd AD over radio]: "Lock it up for picture!"
   [Pause: 3rd AD quiets external traffic/noise]

2. [Director or 1st AD]: "Quiet on the set!"
   [Everyone stops, falls silent]

3. [Director or 1st AD]: "Camera ready?"
   [Camera Operator]: "Ready!"

4. [Director or 1st AD]: "Sound ready?"
   [Sound Person]: "Ready!"

5. [Director or 1st AD]: "Roll camera!"
   [Camera Operator]: "Rolling!"

6. [Director or 1st AD]: "Slate it!" or "Marker!"
   [2nd AC holds slate to camera, calls: "Scene 7, Take 2."]
   [Clapper stick snaps — or not, for video]
   [2nd AC exits frame hastily]

7. [Camera Operator]: "Framed!"
   [Slight pause — editing buffer]

8. [Director]: "Action!"
   [Scene plays]

9. [Director]: "Cut!"
   [Film only: Director to 1st AC]: "Check the gate."
```

## Set Etiquette

The set is a professional environment with near-military precision. These are not suggestions:

- Arrive on time or early. Ready to work.
- Do only your job. Ask before helping someone else's department.
- Only DOP and 1st AD address the director with creative input — if you have a suggestion, take it to your department head.
- When "Quiet!" is called, you stop. Instantly. Mid-sentence, mid-joke, mid-everything.
- Watch for cables, grip stands, lighting fixtures. The set has hazards invisible to people not used to production environments.
- Use standard industry terminology. Ambiguous communication wastes time.
- Nothing negative about anyone on set, ever.
- No offensive language unless the director has explicitly set a different tone.
- If in doubt, ask your supervisor.

## Safety Protocol (Hughes) `[DigitalFilmmaking p.88]`

Source: `[DigitalFilmmaking p.88]` (Hughes verbatim: "When first setting up light stands, use a double-sided sandbag to hold the stand upright. Some lighting stands and fixtures are very lightweight and could easily be knocked over"). Cross-reference: `[Filmmaking:MakingAFilm p.224]` ("A standard sandbag weighs about 15 pounds and resembles a cowboy's saddlebag").

**Lighting fixtures and electrical**:
- Sandbags on all light stands — a falling light stand is lethal `[DigitalFilmmaking p.88]`
- Switch fixtures off before plugging or unplugging
- Wear gloves when handling hot fixtures
- NEVER touch bulbs with bare fingers — skin oil causes uneven heating = bulb explosion
- Total wattage ÷ voltage = amperage draw; never exceed 15A per circuit `[general-knowledge]` (standard electrical code; also reflected in `[DigitalFilmmaking]` electrical safety chapter)

**General set safety**:
- Know where all power switches are
- Know the emergency evacuation route
- Report any unsafe condition to the gaffer (electrical) or 1st AD (everything else)
- No one runs on set

## Shooting Efficiency

**Shooting ratio** (approximate): 5:1 — five minutes of acquired footage for every one minute of finished film. Actual ratios range from 1.5:1 to 35:1 depending on production style.

**Cover shots**: Shoot additional angles, sizes, and framings of every scene beyond what the shot list requires. These fill editing gaps, provide safety options, and occasionally become the best shot in the film.

**Field monitor**: Set a monitor near the camera so the director can view the shot during takes without looking through the camera's LCD. Essential for performance direction.

**Call sheet**: Time-blocked daily document showing: scene number, INT/EXT, day/night, shot descriptions, location, characters required, special equipment needed. Distributed to all departments the night before each shooting day. `[Filmmaking:MakingAFilm p.130]` ("call sheets (lists of which cast members work the next day and any special equipment or elements needed for the shooting). On bigger productions this is usually handed off by the first assistant director"); `[JCam:InstructorGuide p.14]` (glossary entry: "A call sheet contains important details of a production, like the filming location...").

## Smaller Shoot Adaptations (for ON THE EDGE scale)

ON THE EDGE is a 26-minute short with a limited crew. The full hierarchy above will be compressed. What must survive compression:

- **Director has final say on everything**. Non-negotiable.
- **The command chain** must be used even with two-person crew. "Roll camera" / "Rolling" / "Action" / "Cut" ensures everything is being recorded and framed before performance begins. Students who skip the chain miss takes.
- **Room tone must be recorded** at every location, every session. No exceptions.
- **Continuity notes** must be kept by someone — if no dedicated script supervisor, the PA takes notes on phone, noting costume state, prop positions, last line before cut.
- **Safety**: Light stands get sandbags. Everyone knows the location's power circuits.
