# AI FILMMAKING ENHANCEMENT

## Role

This skill activates when generating or refining any screenplay content, shot list, or production document. It applies AI-specific augmentation to traditional filmmaking knowledge — not replacing craft, but adding generative layers that expand creative options and maintain consistency across a long project.

## 1. Multiple Concept Generation

For every significant creative decision, generate three variations instead of one:

**Safe/Standard**: Technically correct, within genre conventions, what a competent filmmaker would default to.

**Elevated**: Same technical correctness, but with one unexpected choice — a non-obvious shot size, a lighting ratio that breaks the scene's pattern for specific effect, a composition that reframes the power dynamic.

**Experimental**: Pushes against convention. May violate rules (with purpose). Higher risk, higher reward. Often the most memorable shot in the film comes from the experimental option. Annotate with the rule being broken and why it serves the story.

Present all three with trade-offs annotated. The director chooses.

## 2. Character Voice Consistency Tracking

Every character has a voice profile. The profile tracks vocabulary, sentence rhythm, sentence length, silence behavior, and dominant emotional register. Once established, every line of dialogue written for that character is checked against the profile.

### Voice Profile Template
```
CHARACTER: [Name]
Dominant register: [cold/warm/clinical/fragmented/performative/etc.]
Sentence structure: [fragments/complete sentences/long/clipped]
Vocabulary tier: [technical/colloquial/mixed]
Silence behavior: [speaks into silence/lets silence happen/uses silence as weapon]
What they never say: [the thing their character psychology prevents]
What they always almost say: [the subtext they never surface]
```

### ON THE EDGE Voice Profiles

**Reyes** (Lead Scientist):
- Register: Precise, controlled, increasingly interior
- Sentence structure: Complete sentences → fragments under pressure
- Vocabulary: Scientific but not jargon-heavy (she translates)
- Silence: Lets silence happen. Speaks when she has something worth saying.
- Never says: "I don't understand" — replaces with "That's not consistent with [X]"
- Almost says: "We have to turn back"

**Marshall** (Mission Commander):
- Register: Command authority, increasingly hollow
- Sentence structure: Declarative. Short. Commands even in conversation.
- Vocabulary: Operational, military-inflected
- Silence: Fills silence — a silent Marshall is a dangerous Marshall
- Never says: "I'm afraid" or any first-person emotional disclosure
- Almost says: Everything he suppresses

**Tanaka** (Engineer):
- Register: Problem-solving language, humor as deflection
- Sentence structure: Technical assertions; questions that aren't really questions
- Vocabulary: System-level, engineering metaphors
- Silence: Uncomfortable with silence; fills it with technical observations
- Never says: Direct emotional confrontation
- Almost says: "Something is wrong with this ship"

**Costa** (Navigator):
- Register: Spatial/directional language, increasingly route-finding in emotional sense
- Sentence structure: Position statements. "We are X from Y."
- Vocabulary: Navigational, precise distances
- Silence: Watches. Observes. Speaks last.
- Never says: Speculation
- Almost says: "I know where this goes"

**Carter** (Documentarian):
- Register: Documentary framing, meta-commentary, increasingly self-aware horror
- Sentence structure: Interview-style, questions, "for the record" framing
- Vocabulary: Media-aware, narrative-aware
- Silence: Holds the camera up instead of speaking
- Never says: First-person direct experience (always mediated through the camera)
- Almost says: "I should stop filming this"

## 3. Show-Don't-Tell Enforcement

Before any dialogue or action line is finalized, scan for "telling" language and convert to visual/behavioral equivalents.

### Telling Signals (Flag These)
- "realizes" → what does she DO when she realizes it?
- "understands" → what physical response follows understanding?
- "feels" → what is the visible behavior of that feeling?
- "thinks" → what does thinking look like on this character's face?
- Dialogue that directly states emotion: "I'm scared." → what do scared people do with their hands?
- Exposition disguised as dialogue: "As you know, we are three days from the target star" → this information should arrive in the world, not in speech

### Conversion Protocol
1. Identify the emotion or information
2. Find the physical behavior that produces that emotion or carries that information
3. Write the behavior
4. Delete the emotional label

**Before**: "Reyes looks frightened as she reads the data."
**After**: "Reyes sets down the stylus. Picks it back up. Sets it down again. Her finger traces the anomalous value three times before she pulls her hand away from the screen."

## 4. AI Image Prompt Generation

For each shot in a production package, generate an AI image prompt that can be used as reference material for the DOP, gaffer, and art department.

### Prompt Structure
```
[Subject description] in [Setting/Environment], 
[Camera angle/position], 
[Lighting description — direction, quality, ratio], 
[Mood/atmosphere], 
[Film reference if applicable], 
[Technical specs: lens compression, color temperature]
```

### Examples

**SHOT: CU Reyes, Stage Two**
```
A woman in her 40s, scientific precision in her face, at a 
futuristic workstation, blue-white projection light casting 
high contrast across her features, 4:1 key-to-fill ratio, 
deep shadows on the shadow side, telephoto lens compression 
flattening the background consoles, color temperature mixed 
cool daylight key and warmer screen fill, atmosphere: isolated 
under surveillance, reference: Arrival (2016) lab sequences, 
shallow depth of field, background instruments blurred
```

**SHOT: ELS Ship approaching Entity zone, Stage Two**
```
A small spacecraft against infinite deep space, the target 
star visible in extreme background abnormally small and 
irregularly lit, ship lit by cold starlight from below, 
no fill, deep space black negative space dominating 80% 
of frame, the ship appears fragile, reference: 2001 A Space 
Odyssey Discovery One shots, telephoto compression making 
the ship and star appear closer than they should, atmosphere: 
profound isolation, color palette deep blue-black with single 
warm ship light
```

## 5. Mood Board Elements

For each act/stage of ON THE EDGE, maintain a living mood board specification:

### Stage One Mood Board
- **Color palette**: Cool white (#F0F4F8) dominant, warm amber (#D4A257) accent for human skin/practical lights, deep black (#0A0A0F) for space exterior
- **Reference films**: *Arrival* (2016) — clinical beauty; *Interstellar* (2014) — intimate spacecraft scale; *2001: A Space Odyssey* (1968) — the emptiness between
- **Key adjectives**: Controlled, purposeful, isolated, professional, nascent-dread
- **Texture**: Sharp, clean edges. High resolution. Everything legible.
- **Sound reference**: Low mechanical hum. Keyboard taps. Distant life support.

### Stage Two Mood Board
- **Color palette**: Cool white shifting toward cold blue (#8BA8C4), warm practicals failing, shadow filling formerly lit areas
- **Reference films**: *Sunshine* (2007) — mission fracture under cosmic scale; *Event Horizon* (1997) — the ship becoming hostile; *Alien* (1979) — close quarters becoming lethal
- **Key adjectives**: Unsettled, compressed, fractured, clinical-fear, unresolved
- **Texture**: Slightly degraded. Minor aberrations visible. Edges beginning to blur.
- **Sound reference**: Hum has changed register. New sub-bass frequency. Mechanical sounds slightly asynchronous.

### Stage Three Mood Board
- **Color palette**: Near-monochrome. Cold blue-black (#0D1520) dominant. Single warm source (emergency amber #FF6B1A) at maximum contrast. Near-silhouette.
- **Reference films**: *Under the Skin* (2013) — incomprehensible alien interiority; *Annihilation* (2018) — physical reality losing coherence; *The Black Hole* (1979) conceptual — the boundary where physics ends
- **Key adjectives**: Fractured, unmade, terminal, beyond-language, post-human
- **Texture**: Maximum grain. Compression artifacts intentional. Edges dissolve.
- **Sound reference**: Silence. Then sub-bass that isn't sound, it's pressure.

## 6. Visual-First Action Lines

Every action line in the screenplay must contain, in 3-5 lines maximum:
1. **Subject** — who or what
2. **Action** — what they physically do
3. **Emotional result** — what the action creates in the scene's atmosphere
4. **Story information** — what the audience learns
5. **Forward momentum** — where this takes us next

If a line can be cut without losing any of those five elements, it belongs on the cutting room floor.

**Test**: Read the action line aloud. Ask: "What does the camera see?" If the answer requires interpretation or inference about internal state, the line is not visual enough. Make it visual.
