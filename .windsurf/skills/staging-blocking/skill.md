# STAGING & BLOCKING

## The 180° Rule (Action Axis / Line of Action)

The most important continuity rule in filmmaking. Violating it invisibly destroys spatial coherence. Violating it intentionally, visibly, destroys spatial coherence as a narrative tool.

**The Rule**: Draw an imaginary line through the primary action or between the primary subjects. The camera must stay on ONE SIDE of that line for all shots in a continuous scene. If the camera crosses to the other side between cuts, subjects appear to reverse position and screen direction. The audience experiences confusion they cannot name — the scene "feels wrong."

**Why it matters**: Screen direction is the audience's spatial map. Characters who have been on the left stay on the left. Characters who move right keep moving right. Violating this silently = incompetence. Violating this visibly and intentionally = the world is breaking.

### Establishing the 180° Line

1. Identify the primary relationship in the scene (two characters talking, a character and a doorway, a character and the viewport)
2. Draw an imaginary line connecting them — this is the action axis
3. Choose which side of the line the camera will live on for this scene
4. All coverage shots stay on that same side
5. Mark the line on the floor plan

### Legal Line-Crossing Methods

The rule can be broken only if the audience sees it happen:

1. **Dolly move**: The camera physically moves across the line during a shot. The audience sees the spatial perspective shift in real time. The geography updates.
2. **Character movement**: A character physically crosses the line. The camera, following them, is now on the new side. The geography updates because the actor moved.
3. **Cutaway to neutral shot**: A neutral shot with no screen direction (an object, an ELS, a reaction from a third angle) resets the audience's spatial orientation. After the neutral, the new side is established.

**In ON THE EDGE**: Stage One maintains strict 180° discipline. The audience knows the ship's geography. In Stage Three, the line is crossed illegally — not by mistake, but as deliberate signaling that spatial reality has collapsed. The audience feels wrongness without being able to identify it. That unnameable wrongness is the Entity's effect on continuity.

## Coverage Strategy

Coverage = the set of shots acquired for a scene that will give the editor enough material to cut a complete, working sequence.

### Simple Sequence Formula (Hughes)

The minimum viable coverage for any scene:
1. **Wide establishing shot** — show where and who
2. **Story shots** — the shots that carry the scene's action and dialogue
3. **Cut-ins/Cut-aways** — close-up detail or related action
4. **Re-establishing shot** — optional return to wide, repositions audience in space

### Master Scene Shooting

**Master shot**: A single take that covers the entire scene, usually from a wide angle, continuously from first line to last. The master is the safety net. The editor can always cut to it if closer shots don't work.

**Story shots**: Medium shots and close-ups acquired separately after the master. These are the shots that will actually be cut together — the master provides the frame of reference, the story shots provide the emotional material.

**Cut-in**: A close-up of a detail WITHIN the current scene — a hand on a console, text on a screen, a face reacting. The cut-in says: this detail matters.

**Cut-away**: A shot of something OUTSIDE the current scene — action in another location, a reaction from someone not in the main shot. The cut-away says: the world is bigger than this room.

**Cover shots**: Additional shots acquired by the camera operator while the director moves to the next setup. Cover shots are insurance — angle variations, framings that might be useful. "Cover it off, [operator's name]." They often end up as collage material or transitional sequences.

## Floor Plans and Blocking Notation

### Spike Marks
Tape on the floor indicating where set pieces (furniture, props, equipment) must be positioned. Without spike marks, set pieces drift between takes and shots won't match. Use gaff tape for marks — it pulls clean.

### T-Marks
Tape on the floor indicating where an ACTOR stands for a specific shot. The T-shape: the horizontal bar indicates foot position (left foot to left, right foot to right), the vertical bar indicates standing direction. Actor hits the T-mark and the framing is guaranteed to match.

Standard notation:
- **T1**: Master position (initial blocking)
- **T2**: Story shot position (usually a step closer or to the side)
- **T3-T4**: Close-up positions
- **T5+**: ECU or special positions

### Floor Plan Elements
A simple floor plan sketch for each scene includes:
- Room outline with dimensions
- Set piece positions (desk, console, bed, door)
- Spike marks labeled
- T-marks labeled with shot number
- Camera positions labeled (C1 for master, C2 for story shots, etc.)
- 180° line drawn
- Lighting positions marked with key/fill/back labels

## Coverage Matrix by Scene Type

### Dialogue Scene (Two Characters)
- Master: Wide two-shot, full scene
- Story shots: Clean single MS each character (matching eyelines)
- Inserts: CU reactions without dialogue ("listening shots")
- Optional: Over-the-shoulder (OTS) — character at edge of frame, other character in background
- Coverage: Cutaway to environmental detail if needed to establish mood

### Action/Movement Scene
- Master: Wide enough to show geography of movement
- Story shots: MS of character in motion
- Cut-ins: ECU of goal (the button to push, the door to reach)
- Cut-aways: Obstacle, clock, other character reacting
- Coverage: Angles showing the obstacle from subject's POV (see @storyboard-artist for POV protocol)

### Solitude/Interior Scene (Single Character)
- Master: LS or MS establishing character in space
- Story shots: CU face
- Cut-ins: ECU hands, eyes, the object of attention
- Coverage: Reaction shots from character's POV

## Blocking Principles for Horror

**Doorways**: The most powerful blocking position in horror is a character framed by a doorway — between two spaces, belonging to neither. Costa's signature position. The doorway is the threshold between the known and unknown.

**Shrinking blocking area**: As tension increases, block characters into smaller and smaller portions of the frame. Stage One: characters move freely through the full frame. Stage Three: characters are static, pressed into corners, filling only a portion of the wide shot.

**Backing away from camera**: A character backing toward camera = the audience cannot see what is behind the camera. This is the most primal staging position in horror. The threat is where the audience is.

**Looking at camera (violating fourth wall)**: Used once, maximum, at the moment of maximum horror. A character looks directly into the lens. The Entity has arrived in the audience's space.
