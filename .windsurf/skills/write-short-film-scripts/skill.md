# Write Short Film Scripts Skill

## Purpose
Apply the complete integrated short film scriptwriting methodology — covering structural tiers, concept development, character engineering, visual storytelling, dialogue craft, and rewriting — for films ranging from 3 to 30 minutes.

**Sources:** Kathryn Burnett (Filmsourcing guide), Kim Garland (directing insights), UCA formatting guide.

---

## Part I: Length Tiers

### Tier 1 — The Joke (3-5 min)
- **Best for:** Comedy, horror, twist-driven narratives
- **Structure:** Setup → Mislead → Punchline
- **Characters:** 1-2 | **Locations:** Single
- **Principle:** Every second serves the final reveal
- **Beat spacing:** Every 30 seconds

| Time | Beat | Function |
|------|------|----------|
| 0:00 | Setup | Normal world, one telling detail |
| 0:30 | Inciting Incident | Disruption |
| 1:00 | Escalation 1 | Complication, attempt to resolve |
| 1:30 | Escalation 2 | Worse/more absurd |
| 2:00 | The Turn | New info reframes everything |
| 2:30 | Climax | Confrontation with truth |
| 3:00 | Punchline/Resolution | Twist, reveal, reversal |

Reference: *Six Shooter* (McDonagh)

### Tier 2 — The Scene (5-10 min)
- **Best for:** Character study, relationship drama, single emotional arc
- **Structure:** Three-act compression (Setup/Conflict/Resolution)
- **Characters:** 2-3 | **Locations:** 1-2
- **Principle:** One complete transformation
- **Beat spacing:** Every 1-2 minutes

| Time | Beat | Function |
|------|------|----------|
| 0:00 | Opening Image | Tone + world + "before" state |
| 1:00 | Inciting Incident | Challenge to status quo |
| 2:00 | Rising Action | Attempts, complications |
| 3:00 | Midpoint | Revelation, point of no return |
| 4:00 | Crisis | Everything at risk, mask drops |
| 5:00 | Climax | Transformation through action |
| 6:00 | Resolution | New status quo, emotional payoff |

Reference: *Stutterer* (Oscar winner)

### Tier 3 — The Short (10-20 min)
- **Best for:** Complex character arcs, subplots, world-building
- **Structure:** Full three-act with one subplot
- **Characters:** 3-4 | **Locations:** 2-3
- **Principle:** Standalone story that feels like part of something larger

| Act | Time | Function |
|-----|------|----------|
| Act 1 | 0:00-3:00 | Setup, inciting incident, first threshold |
| Act 2A | 3:00-7:00 | Rising action, complications, midpoint reversal |
| Act 2B | 7:00-12:00 | Crisis, dark moment, prep for climax |
| Act 3 | 12:00-15:00 | Climax, resolution, final image |

Subplot rules: max one B-story; must intersect A-story by min 10; must resolve by climax.
Reference: *Whiplash* (short)

### Tier 4 — The Mini-Feature (20-30 min)
- **Best for:** Ensemble pieces, thematic exploration, proof-of-concept for features
- **Structure:** Full three-act with B-story, multiple character arcs
- **Characters:** 4-6 | **Locations:** 3-5
- **Principle:** Feature-level complexity and cinematic experience

| Act | Time | Function |
|-----|------|----------|
| Act 1 | 0:00-7:00 | Multiple character setups, inciting incidents |
| Act 2A | 7:00-15:00 | Parallel rising actions, intersections, midpoints |
| Act 2B | 15:00-22:00 | Individual crises, collective dark moment |
| Act 3 | 22:00-30:00 | Converging climaxes, thematic resolution |

Ensemble rules: 4-6 max; each character has a distinct arc; all arcs intersect thematically; no character exists only for exposition.

## Part II: Concept Development (Burnett + Garland)

### The 10-Question Core Idea Lock (Burnett)
Before writing anything, answer in writing:
1. What is the core idea?
2. Who is it about?
3. What is the genre?
4. Who/what is the antagonist?
5. What is the central question?
6. Whose POV?
7. What happens?
8. How does it happen?
9. What is the theme?
10. How conveyed in images?

**Single Sentence Test:** "This story is about a _____ who _____ and _____ but then _____."

### The 5-Lock (Pre-Writing Decision Lock)
| Lock | Decision |
|------|----------|
| Duration | ___ minutes |
| Primary Location | _____________ |
| Secondary Location | _____________ |
| Character 1 | _____________ |
| Character 2 | _____________ |
| Character 3 | _____________ |
| Central Prop | _____________ |
| Dominant Emotion | _____________ |

### Dynamic Opening (Garland)
- Establish tone immediately
- Introduce protagonist through action, not description
- Create an information gap (curiosity)
- No exposition dump
- End with forward momentum

### Layered Storytelling (Garland)
For every scene, identify all five layers:
- **Text:** What literally happens
- **Subtext:** What characters really mean
- **Theme:** How this reflects the film's deeper meaning
- **Visual motif:** Recurring image carrying emotional weight
- **Sound design:** Audio layer reinforcing mood

## Part III: Character Engineering (Garland Method)

Build secondary characters from a recognizable archetype, then add unique details to avoid stereotype.

### Character Construction Template
| Element | Character |
|---------|-----------|
| Archetype Base | The Wounded Healer / The Rebel / The Gatekeeper / etc. |
| Unique Detail 1 | Something specific and visual |
| Unique Detail 2 | Something behavioral |
| Objective | What they want |
| Mask | How they present themselves |
| Wound | What broke them |
| Breaking Point | When the mask drops |
| Transformative Action | What they do differently by the end |

### Telling Details (show, don't state)
| Instead of stating | Show this |
|-------------------|-----------|
| "She's wealthy" | Silk blouse, high-end bike, one chipped manicure |
| "He's rebellious" | Retro bike with streamers, helmet covered in stickers |
| "They're grieving" | House full of thriving plants — except one dead in a prominent spot |
| "She's anxious" | Checks locks three times, never sits back in a chair |

## Part IV: Visual Storytelling (Burnett + Garland)

### Location as Character
Think about where each scene is set and what physical actions can carry the story without dialogue.

**Example:**
- Weak: Mother and son at kitchen table discussing grades
- Strong: Same conversation while mother breaks down a chicken with a cleaver — violence implied, tension without words

### Visual Sentence Structure (5 elements per action line)
1. Subject (who/what we see)
2. Action (what they do)
3. Emotional Result (how audience feels)
4. Story Information (what audience learns)
5. Forward Momentum (why keep watching)

**Weak:** "Maria looks sad."
**Strong:** "Maria stares at the photo on the fridge — her and a man at a carnival, laughing. She reaches to touch it, then deliberately turns away, grabs her keys, and leaves. The photo remains: two people frozen in joy, now alone in an empty kitchen."

Action description: 3-5 lines max (UCA). Show emotional state through behavior, never adverbs.

## Part V: Dialogue as Precision Tool (UCA + Garland)

### Subtext-First Translations
| Direct (cut this) | Subtext technique | Filmic (keep this) |
|------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| "I'm angry you left" | Object substitution | "Your plant died. I kept watering it." |
| "I want you to stay" | Deflection | "Traffic's terrible this time of day." |
| "I'm afraid" | Action instead | Checks locks, sits with back to wall |
| "I love you" | Question as answer | "Do you remember why we started this?" |
| "You hurt me" | Parallel scenario | "I had a dog once. Ran away. Never got another." |

### Dialogue Density Test
Cut or rewrite if the answer is "no" to any:
- Does this advance the plot?
- Does this reveal character?
- Can the same meaning be conveyed visually?
- Is the same thing said twice?
- Is the exposition natural?

## Part VI: Rewrite System (Burnett's 5 Steps)

1. **Break** — Step back from the work entirely
2. **Interrogation** — Answer in writing: core idea, genre, emotions intended, character contribution, too much backstory?, is the interesting stuff happening in the past?
3. **Scene-by-scene** — For each scene: what is its purpose? Does it contribute to the core idea? Does it end on a question that leads into the next?
4. **Efficiency pass** — Cut: driving/walking between locations, duplicate information, exposition that can be shown, any scene that ends without forward momentum
5. **Dialogue polish** — Read aloud, cut to fewer words, replace with action where possible, check naturalism

---

## Part VII: Production Reality Check

### Constraint-First Formula
`(Catalyst) + (Limitation) + (Stakes) = Concept`

Example:
- Catalyst: Teen discovers mother's secret
- Limitation: Must confront her during their only shared activity (laundry)
- Stakes: She leaves for deployment tomorrow

### Resource Checklist (answer before writing)
- **Location:** Can I secure it? Afford to light/heat/secure it? Reshoots possible? Sound issues?
- **Cast:** Can I cast the required type locally? Full schedule commitment? Skills required (driving, languages, stunts)?
- **Technical:** Practical effects achievable? VFX needed? Clean audio possible in this environment?

---

## Part VIII: The "So What?" Test
Before locking the final draft, answer all four:
1. Why does this story need to exist **now**?
2. Why does this need to be a **film** (not a story, essay, or photo)?
3. Why will the audience **remember it tomorrow**?
4. What will they **feel in their body**?
