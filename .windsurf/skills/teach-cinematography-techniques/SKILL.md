## Pedagogical Approach

### Teaching Principles

**Show, Don't Just Tell**
- Use frame grabs from course films
- Before/after composition examples
- Student work comparisons
- Video demonstrations when possible

**Smartphone-First Mindset**
- All techniques achievable with phones
- Embrace limitations creatively
- Focus on composition over gear
- Professional results with amateur tools

**Tokyo Context Integration**
- Use local architecture for examples
- Reference accessible locations
- Consider weather and seasons
- Respect cultural filming norms

**Progressive Complexity**
- Start with static composition
- Add movement gradually
- Introduce lighting concepts
- Combine techniques in final project

### Analysis Questions for Students

1. What is the dominant shot size in this scene? Why?
2. How does camera movement (or stillness) affect the mood?
3. What is the primary light source? How does it shape the image?
4. How does the composition direct your eye?
5. What visual metaphors or symbols are present?
6. How would you recreate this shot with a smartphone in Tokyo?

## Common Student Challenges

### Technical Challenges

**Shaky Footage**
- Solution: Two-handed grip, tuck elbows, breathe
- Solution: Lean against walls, use makeshift supports
- Solution: Shorter shots, cut on movement
- Solution: Smartphone stabilization features

**Poor Lighting**
- Solution: Shoot during golden hour
- Solution: Use window light indoors
- Solution: Embrace available light creatively
- Solution: Reflectors from white surfaces

**Flat Composition**
- Solution: Add foreground elements
- Solution: Use leading lines
- Solution: Create depth layers
- Solution: Vary shot sizes

**Inconsistent Coverage**
- Solution: Shot list discipline
- Solution: Master shot safety
- Solution: Shoot more than you think you need
- Solution: Match eye-lines and screen direction

### Conceptual Challenges

**Confusing Gear with Skill**
- Emphasize: Composition matters more than camera
- Show: Great phone cinematography examples
- Practice: Constraints breed creativity
- Remind: Course philosophy on resourcefulness

**Unmotivated Choices**
- Ask: "Why this shot size/angle/movement?"
- Connect: Visual choice to story/emotion
- Reference: Course film examples
- Practice: Justifying every decision

**Ignoring Location Constraints**
- Address: Tokyo filming permissions
- Plan: Backup locations and times
- Adapt: Rewrite scenes for available spaces
- Embrace: Constraints as creative fuel

## Tokyo-Specific Considerations

### Location Cinematography

**Public Spaces**
- Train stations: dynamic backgrounds, challenging light
- Parks: natural light, seasonal changes
- Streets: neon at night, crowds during day
- Temples/shrines: cultural sensitivity, beautiful architecture

**Lighting Conditions**
- Summer: harsh midday sun, long golden hours
- Winter: shorter days, softer light
- Rainy season: overcast advantages, wet reflections
- Night: neon, street lights, practical sources

**Cultural Sensitivity**
- Respect privacy in public filming
- Avoid sacred spaces without permission
- Be mindful of crowds and disruption
- Understand Japanese filming etiquette

## Voice and Tone

When teaching cinematography:
- **Empowering**: "You can achieve this with your phone"
- **Specific**: "Place your subject on the right third, facing left"
- **Visual**: "Notice how the light wraps around her face"
- **Practical**: "In Tokyo, shoot near windows during afternoon"
- **Encouraging**: "Your first attempts will improve with practice"
- **Honest**: "This won't look like a Hollywood film, but it will tell your story"
