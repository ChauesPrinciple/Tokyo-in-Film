# VISUAL GRAMMAR

## Shot Size Vocabulary

Every shot size carries a psychological contract with the audience. Choosing a shot size is not a technical decision — it is an emotional statement.

| Shot | Abbreviation | Frame Content | Psychological Read |
|------|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Extreme Long Shot | ELS | Subject in vast environment, tiny or absent | Scale, isolation, insignificance, environment as character |
| Long Shot | LS | Subject full-body, head to feet | Subject in environment, physical power or vulnerability, solitude |
| Medium Shot | MS | Waist to head | Professional relationship, conversation, competence, stability |
| Close-Up | CU | Head and shoulders | Emotional intimacy, identification, empathy, interiority |
| Extreme Close-Up | ECU | Single feature (eyes, mouth, hands, object) | Obsession, fragmentation, psychological pressure, revelation |

### The Progression Rule
Shots should progress with intention, not habit. MS → CU = increasing intimacy. CU → MS = emotional withdrawal. ECU → MS = psychological stabilization (or forced normality). Any reversal carries meaning.

### Cut-Off Rule (Hughes)
Never cut a human body at a natural joint. The joints — ankles, knees, waist, elbows, wrists, neck — are where the eye naturally goes. Cutting there amputates. Safe cut lines:
- Below ankle (full foot in frame) or mid-shin
- Mid-thigh or above knee
- Mid-torso (never at belt/waistband)
- Mid-chest or above
- Never at the neck (the "head in a box" problem)

## Compositional Rules

### Rule of Thirds
Divide the frame into a 3×3 grid. Place subjects at intersecting lines (power points), not at center. Eyes of a subject should fall on the upper horizontal third.

**Centered/balanced**: Stability, authority, symmetry, order — or oppressive confinement.
**Off-center (thirds)**: Dynamic, active, in motion, conflicted, psychologically uneasy.

In horror, controlled drift from thirds toward center = character losing agency. Center = trapped.

### Headroom
Space between top of subject's head and top of frame. Too much = subject appears to sink, lost. Too little = claustrophobic pressure (useful in horror at controlled moments). Standard: 10-15% of frame height above the eyes.

### Lead Room (Nose Room)
Space in the direction a subject faces or moves. A subject looking left needs empty space on the left. Cutting lead room = trapping the subject, denying their forward projection. Used to show characters running out of choices.

### Screen Direction Continuity
If a subject exits frame right, they must enter the next shot from frame left. Reversing screen direction without crossing the 180° line legally = spatial confusion. In horror: deliberate spatial confusion signals reality breakdown.

## Psychological Reads by Shot — ON THE EDGE Application

**ELS**: The ship against the cosmos. The star being unmade. Use to establish scale disparity between human and Entity. The ship should look like a mote.

**LS**: A character alone in a corridor. Used for isolation beats — the character is physically in the ship but psychologically adrift. Tanaka's final walk.

**MS**: The baseline of the film. Stage One lives here. Scientists at work, competent, functional. MS = things are still okay.

**CU**: The pressure shot. Stage Two begins when CU becomes the default for conversation. The face fills the frame. There is no escape from interiority.

**ECU**: Stage Three. Hands trembling on a console. An eye reflected in a viewport. The margin of a document filled with equations that go wrong. ECU = the mind cannot hold.

## Intentional Rule Violations

Rules are violated for narrative purpose, never by accident.

**Cutting at the neck**: Legitimate in horror when the body is becoming alien to the mind. The "head in a box" ECU signals dissociation.

**No lead room**: Character has no future to move toward. Used in crisis scenes.

**Centered frame in a thirds film**: When a character has been completely trapped by circumstance, center them. The audience feels the wrongness.

**Breaking cut-off at a joint**: In extreme horror, cutting a character at the knee or waist suggests fragmentation of identity. Use once, maximum — loses power with repetition.

## Shot Size × Stage Matrix — ON THE EDGE

| Stage | Default Shot | Permitted | Prohibited |
|-------|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Stage One | MS | LS, MS, occasional CU | ECU, violations |
| Stage Two | CU | MS, CU, ECU (sparingly) | ELS (safety is gone) |
| Stage Three | ECU | CU, ECU, LS (silhouette only) | MS (stability gone) |

## DECISION PROTOCOL

### When to Invoke This Skill

This skill is invoked **first** in Mode A (production filmmaking). No lighting, lens, or editorial decision is made before shot size and composition are established. Shot size determines what the lighting must illuminate. Composition determines where the camera must be.

### How to Choose Shot Size

Source: Storyboarding course VTT 18 (visual language module — ECU "can create an uncomfortable or an intense effect... and heighten the intensity of any scene"); VTT 9 (shot types module — close-up "helps with audience identification"); Kuleshov effect (Soviet montage, 1910s)

```
ASK: What does the audience need to feel about this subject at this moment?

→ The subject is irrelevant against the world around them
     → ELS: Scale, isolation, cosmic indifference. Environment is the subject.

→ Geography is information the audience needs to understand the scene
     → LS: Subject in environment. Establishes spatial relationships.

→ Two or more people; relationship between them is the subject
     → MS: Conversation, confrontation, competence. Stage One default.

→ One person's internal state is what the scene is about
     → CU: Emotion, interiority, identification. Stage Two default.
     → Rule (VTT 18): Place CU where you need the audience to feel what the character feels.

→ A specific detail carries the entire weight of the narrative moment
     → ECU: Fragmentation, obsession, psychological rupture. Stage Three default.

PROGRESSION RULE: ELS → LS → MS → CU → ECU maps increasing intimacy.
Reversing it (ECU → MS) signals psychological stabilization — or forced normality.
Violating it without intent produces incoherence.
```

### How to Choose Composition

Source: Storyboarding course VTT 19 (composition principles module — centered frame conveys "stability... normality"; off-center/thirds conveys "uneasiness, a dynamic quality... conflict"; composition used to "underscore that story point")

```
ASK: Who holds power in this scene?

→ Character in control, stable, authoritative
     → CENTER FRAME: Stability, symmetry, authority. Symmetry = the character owns the moment.

→ Character under pressure, conflicted, weakening
     → RULE OF THIRDS: Off-center = visual tension. Place in weak third if losing, strong third if fighting.

→ Two characters in conflict
     → Each occupies one third. The space between them IS the conflict.

→ Character is trapped — by circumstance, a relationship, a choice
     → Corner placement. The environment fills the dominant portion of the frame.
     → The image argues what the story is saying without a word of dialogue.

VIOLATED ONLY WHEN: A character who has lived in the thirds suddenly centers.
In a thirds-dominant film, a centered character reads as completely trapped.
```
