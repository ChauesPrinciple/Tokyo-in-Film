Composition Notes

Reference notes — composition principles for cinematography

1. What Composition Actually Is

Before we get into the rules, let me tell you what composition is not. It's not a checklist. It's not a formula you plug numbers into. Composition is the arrangement of visual elements within your frame, and when it's done well, the audience doesn't notice it at all. They just feel it. The shot feels right, or it feels wrong, and most of the time they can't tell you why.

That's where you come in. As the filmmaker, you're making deliberate choices about where to place your subject, how many elements to include, what angle to shoot from, how tight or wide to frame. You've got thousands of options. Most of them are mediocre. A few of them are exactly right. The goal of studying composition is to train your eye so that finding those few right choices becomes instinct rather than accident.

There are three ingredients that make a composition work: rules, flow, and story.

The rules are the structural guidelines (the rule of thirds, the golden ratio, leading lines, color theory). They tell you where elements want to sit in the frame so the image feels balanced.

Flow is about where the eye travels. You can place every element perfectly on a grid, but if the viewer's eye has nowhere to go, the composition is dead. Flow means there's a path through the image. The audience looks at one thing, and that thing leads them to the next thing, and so on. You're choreographing their attention.

Story is the reason you made those choices. If a character feels isolated, you show that visually: small figure, vast empty space. If two characters are equals, you frame them symmetrically. Composition isn't decoration. It's narrative. Every choice you make in the frame is telling the audience something, whether you intend to or not.

2. The Rule of Thirds

I know, I know. The creative types never want to hear about rules. But the rule of thirds isn't there to restrict you, it's there to keep you from putting everything dead center and calling it a day.

The concept is simple: divide your frame into nine equal boxes by drawing two horizontal lines and two vertical lines. Most cameras, including your phone, can overlay this grid on the viewfinder. If you haven't turned it on yet, do that now. It stays on permanently.

The way you use this grid is by aligning the important elements in your shot to these lines or, better yet, to the four points where the lines intersect. Those intersections are where the eye naturally wants to look. Place your subject there, and the composition immediately feels more intentional than a centered shot.

Landscapes are the easiest way to practice this. Place the horizon on the top horizontal line if the ground is more interesting; place it on the bottom line if the sky is more interesting. Either way, you're making a choice about what the shot is about, and that choice shows.

With people, the key is the eyes. If your subject is looking directly into the camera, place their eyes on the top grid line. If they're looking off to one side, there exists a viewing direction, the direction the person is looking. You need to give that direction space. Place the subject on the opposite side of the frame so there's room in front of their gaze. This is sometimes called looking room or lead room, and ignoring it is one of the fastest ways to make a shot feel claustrophobic for no reason.

When you've got two people in the frame, you can use the grid to establish hierarchy. Put one person on a vertical line and the other between the lines. The one on the line will feel more prominent. The audience reads it as importance without knowing why. That's the power of this stuff; it works even when nobody is consciously thinking about it.

Now, there's nothing wrong with centering your subject. It works for direct address (interviews, presentations, someone talking to camera) and it works when you specifically want the audience locked onto one thing. But centering gives you no flow, no space for other elements, no room for the eye to wander. It's a closed composition. The rule of thirds opens it up.

3. The Golden Ratio

The golden ratio is the rule of thirds' older, more sophisticated sibling. Same basic idea (a grid that tells you where to place elements) but with a more complex geometry behind it.

The math is simple enough: divide a frame so that one section is approximately 1.6 times larger than the other. Keep subdividing by that same ratio, and you get progressively smaller rectangles. Draw a curve through them and you get the Fibonacci spiral, named after the mathematician who described the pattern. You've seen it before, even if you didn't know the name. It shows up in the shell of a snail, the arrangement of petals on a flower, the proportions of the human face. It's everywhere in nature, and that's precisely why compositions built on it feel so natural to the eye.

In practice, you use the golden ratio the same way you use the rule of thirds: as a guide for where to place your subject and supporting elements. The difference is that the spiral gives you a built-in flow. The eye enters at the wide end of the spiral and follows the curve inward to the focal point. If you place your subject's eyes at the tight center of that spiral, you've just created a composition that pulls the viewer exactly where you want them to look.

The golden ratio is especially useful in outdoor or uncontrolled environments, where you can't rearrange the background. You can't move a building, but you can position your camera so that the building, a tree, and your subject all fall along the spiral. Suddenly a chaotic street corner has a sense of order. In a studio or controlled set, you have even more power; you can place every element precisely on the spiral and create something that feels effortlessly balanced.

Do I expect you to overlay a Fibonacci grid on every shot? No. But once you internalize the shape, you start seeing it. And once you start seeing it, your compositions get better without you having to think about it.

4. Depth

Film is a two-dimensional medium pretending to be three-dimensional. Your job as a cinematographer is to sell that lie. The primary tool for doing so is depth, creating layers in the image so the viewer perceives distance between the foreground, middle ground, and background.

The simplest way to add depth is with foreground elements. Place something close to the camera (a shoulder, a branch, the edge of a doorframe) and let it fall out of focus. Your subject sits further back, in focus. That out-of-focus foreground element does two things: it fills empty space in the frame, and it immediately communicates that there's distance between the camera and the subject. One layer, and the image already feels three-dimensional.

The classic over-the-shoulder shot works on exactly this principle. The blurred shoulder in the foreground isn't the subject, it's a depth cue. It tells the audience where the camera is relative to the two characters. You can achieve the same effect with any foreground object: a fence, a plant, a piece of furniture. The beauty of foreground elements is that they don't have to be recognizable. Because they're out of focus, they function as shape and color rather than identifiable objects.

Backgrounds are trickier. You need to decide: is the background important to the story? If it's distracting or irrelevant, use a shallow depth of field to push it out of focus. If it's meaningful (if the location matters, if there's action happening back there) keep it sharp and use other techniques like leading lines, framing, or color contrast to separate your subject from it.

The question you should always ask yourself: how many layers does this shot have? If the answer is one, you're probably leaving depth on the table.

5. Leading Lines

Composition ultimately comes down to one thing: controlling where the audience looks. Leading lines are the most powerful tool you have for doing that.

A leading line is any visual element (a road, a fence, a shadow, a row of buildings, a crack in the sidewalk) that creates a path for the eye to follow. That path should end at your subject. You're not pointing at your subject with a neon arrow; you're using the geometry of the environment to guide attention there naturally. The audience follows the line without realizing they're being directed.

Leading lines are especially critical in wide shots and establishing shots, where your subject is small in the frame and might otherwise get lost. A long road converging toward a figure in the distance turns a flat, empty landscape into a composition with focus and intention.

Here's where it gets interesting. Leading lines are themselves elements in the frame, which means they interact with your composition grid. If you can align a leading line along one of the rule-of-thirds guidelines, you get a balanced shot with built-in flow. If you can find an element that traces the Fibonacci spiral (a curved path, a winding staircase, the bend of a river) you've got a leading line and a golden ratio composition working together. That combination is extremely compelling, because the eye travels through the entire frame before arriving at the subject. It makes the shot feel dynamic and explorable. It also means you can hold that shot longer in the edit without it going stale, because there's more for the viewer to discover.

When you scout locations in Tokyo, train yourself to see leading lines everywhere. They're there: railway tracks, temple paths, neon-lit corridors, bridges, power lines. The environment is full of them. You just have to notice.

6. Triangles

This one gets overlooked. Triangles are one of the most versatile composition tools in cinematography, and most students never think about them.

The principle is straightforward: when you draw imaginary lines between three points in your frame, you form a triangle. That triangle adds structure and visual stability. It also creates a natural path for the eye, because the viewer moves between the three points, which keeps them engaged with the full frame rather than fixating on one spot.

Triangles are everywhere if you look for them. The roofline of a building. A person leaning against a wall with their arms creating angles. A road narrowing into the distance. Three people standing together at staggered heights. An actor's elbows, shoulders, and head. Every group shot you've ever seen that just "felt right" probably had an implied triangle in it.

The orientation of the triangle matters. A wide base at the bottom with the peak at the top feels stable, grounded, authoritative. Flip it (wide at the top, point at the bottom) and it feels precarious, unstable, tense. That tension is a storytelling tool. A villain framed in an inverted triangle feels dangerous. A hero framed in a stable triangle feels solid. Same subject, different geometry, different story.

7. Symmetry

Symmetry is authority. When both halves of the frame mirror each other, the composition radiates control, power, and order. Think of every shot you've seen of a character standing dead center at the end of a long hallway, or framed perfectly in a doorway. That feeling of inevitability, of command? That's symmetry doing its work.

Wes Anderson built an entire visual language out of it. Stanley Kubrick used it to make you feel like something terrible was about to happen. Symmetry is one of the strongest compositional choices you can make, precisely because it's so deliberate. Nothing about a symmetrical frame looks accidental.

But the real power of symmetry is in breaking it. If you establish a perfectly balanced frame and then one element shifts (a character steps to one side, a door opens on the left but not the right) the audience feels the disruption. They may not consciously register that the composition changed, but they feel the imbalance. That contrast between order and disorder is pure visual storytelling.

Near-symmetry can be even more effective than the real thing. A frame that's almost balanced but not quite creates a subtle discomfort. The viewer senses that something is off without being able to identify it. That unease is a tool. Use it when the scene demands it.

8. Movement and Composition: Blocking

Everything we've discussed so far assumes a still frame. But film moves. And the moment your subjects start moving within the frame, your composition changes in real time. There are two ways to create movement in a composition: move the camera, or keep the camera still and let the elements in the frame move. That could be an actor walking, rain falling, smoke drifting out of a vent. Any motion within a static frame changes the composition.

The most remarkable director when it comes to movement as composition is probably Akira Kurosawa. The way he turns motion (wind, rain, bodies, horses) into compositional elements is worth studying closely. Movement in his films is never incidental. It's always doing compositional work.

Movement creates new leading lines in real time. If your subject turns their head to look in a new direction, their gaze becomes a leading line pointing the audience's eye toward whatever they're looking at. Rain can function the same way; it lets you keep a prominent, active sky while the audience's focus still falls on the subject at the bottom of the frame. The motion guides the eye downward.

Movement also manipulates the point of interest. If you have two people in a static frame and one of them suddenly moves, the audience's attention snaps to the motion. You didn't cut. You didn't reframe. The movement did the work. Similarly, someone walking into an otherwise stable shot immediately becomes the point of interest, simply by being the thing that changed. If you want to draw attention in a crowd, you don't need a close-up, just have your subject move while everyone else is still.

You can also use movement to change the framing within a single shot. Start with your subject in a wide shot and have them walk toward the camera. By the time they stop, you're in a medium shot. There's a distinct beginning and ending to the composition, and the movement connects them without a cut. That flow, that continuity, is something a cut can't replicate.

One more thing: you can add movement in post-production with overlay effects like rain, mist, lens flares, or drifting smoke. A static shot becomes more dynamic when something is drifting through it. The motion makes the viewer's eye wander, which means the environment becomes more important. It's a small addition that changes how long the audience is willing to sit with the image.

Don't waste this. Let your actors move. Let them enter and exit the frame. Let the composition breathe. A static frame with no movement is a photograph. You're making a film.

9. Movement and Composition: Camera

The second type of compositional movement is the camera itself. When you physically move the camera during a shot, you're not just changing what the audience sees, you're changing the composition of what they see. A shift from a low angle to a high angle doesn't just reveal more of the environment; it changes the power dynamic, the visual story, the entire feeling of the shot.

Consider a tracking shot where your subject is walking. You frame them on one side of the rule of thirds with empty space in front of them. This is walking space, and it communicates forward momentum. The audience reads the empty space as the direction the character intends to go. If the character suddenly stops and you reframe so the empty space is now behind them, you've just told the audience that something changed. The character is going back. You communicated that through composition alone, without a word of dialogue.

Camera movement can also introduce new elements into a composition. Imagine a shot where your subject is framed tightly. As you pull the camera backward, new objects enter the frame: a tree, a railing, another person. Each new element changes the composition. If you're thoughtful about what enters and when, you can guide the viewer's eye along a path through the expanding frame, building visual interest as the shot progresses.

And don't forget: camera movement doesn't have to be physical. A focus pull is a form of compositional movement. Shifting focus from one subject to another redirects the viewer's attention entirely. The frame hasn't changed, but the composition has, because the element that was dominant is now soft and the element that was background is now sharp. It's one of the most elegant tools in cinematography.

You can also use camera movement to strengthen existing compositional elements. A leading line that looks flat from one angle becomes dramatically three-dimensional when you shift the camera closer and lower. A bench that's just a horizontal line in a wide shot becomes a strong diagonal pointing at your subject when you change your perspective on it. The line was always there. The camera movement made it matter.

The bottom line: every time you move the camera, you're not just capturing the scene from a new position. You're creating a new composition. Think about what that composition communicates before you move.
