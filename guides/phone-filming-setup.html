Phone Filming Setup

Print this page or keep it on a second device. Walk through each section before your first shoot day.

1. Turn On the Grid

The grid overlays two horizontal and two vertical lines on your viewfinder, dividing the screen into nine boxes. This is your rule-of-thirds guide. It stays on permanently.

iPhone

  1. Open Settings
  2. Scroll down and tap Camera
  3. Under Composition, toggle Grid ON
  4. Also toggle Level ON (keeps your horizons straight)

Samsung

  1. Open the Camera app
  2. Tap the gear icon (top left)
  3. Scroll to General
  4. Toggle Grid lines ON

Google Pixel

  1. Open the Camera app
  2. Tap Settings (bottom left)
  3. Under Composition, select Grid type
  4. Choose 3×3

OnePlus

  1. Open the Camera app
  2. Go to More > Settings
  3. Tap Grid and guides
  4. Select Grid

2. Set Resolution & Frame Rate

Open your camera app, switch to video mode, and look for resolution and frame rate settings (usually a number like “HD” or “30” in the corner).

What to Set

  1. Resolution: 1080p minimum. Use 4K if your storage allows it.
  2. Frame rate:
    • 24fps — cinematic look, natural motion blur. Use this for standard scenes.
    • 30fps — smooth general-purpose footage. Use this if 24fps is unavailable.
    • 60fps — slow-motion only. Plays back at half speed in a 30fps or 24fps timeline.
Why this matters: You can always downgrade quality in editing, but you cannot upgrade it. Film class projects get screened on large monitors. Low resolution will look terrible when projected.
Storage warning: A 10-minute 4K clip can exceed 3 GB. Check your available storage before shoot day and transfer or delete files if needed.

3. Lock Focus & Exposure

Your phone’s auto-focus and auto-exposure will shift during a shot, creating distracting brightness changes and focus “breathing.” Lock them before every take.

All Phones (iOS & Android)

  1. Switch to video mode
  2. Tap and hold on your subject until you see AE/AF Lock or a lock icon
  3. Focus and exposure are now fixed for the duration of the shot
  4. To adjust brightness after locking: swipe up or down on the sun/brightness slider
Do this every take. If you tap anywhere else on the screen, the lock resets and your phone goes back to auto.

4. Airplane Mode & Do Not Disturb

A phone call or notification will interrupt your recording. Turn both on before you shoot.

iPhone

  1. Swipe down from the top-right corner (or up from bottom on older models)
  2. Tap the airplane icon
  3. Tap the crescent moon icon (Do Not Disturb / Focus)

Android

  1. Swipe down from the top of the screen to open Quick Settings
  2. Tap Airplane mode
  3. Tap Do Not Disturb

5. Hold Landscape

Turn the phone sideways. Every shot for this class must be in landscape (horizontal) orientation. Vertical video creates black bars on standard screens and signals that you do not understand film conventions.

If you are unsure, check that the longer edge of your phone is horizontal before you press record.

6. Do Not Use Digital Zoom

Never pinch to zoom while filming. Your phone’s digital zoom does not change the focal length like a real lens. It crops into the center of your image and enlarges the pixels, destroying resolution.

The rule: If the subject is too small, walk closer.

If your phone has multiple camera lenses (0.5×, 1×, 2×), switching between those is fine — each is a real optical lens. It is the pinch-to-zoom between those fixed points that damages quality.

7. Stabilize the Shot

You do not need a gimbal or tripod. You need technique.

Body Technique

  1. Hold the phone with both hands
  2. Tuck your elbows against your body
  3. Take a breath, let half out, hold while shooting
  4. Lean against a wall, pole, or railing if available
  5. For moving shots: bend your knees slightly and walk heel-to-toe (the “ninja walk”)

Improvised Supports

  1. Rest the phone on tables, window sills, shelves, or railings
  2. Stack books to adjust height for a static shot
  3. Use a full water bottle as a weighted base
  4. Prop the phone against a wall for low-angle shots

8. Audio Check

Bad audio will ruin a project faster than bad video. Your phone’s built-in microphone picks up everything equally — your subject and every background noise at the same volume.

Before You Shoot

  1. Find a quiet space. Turn off AC, fans, and anything that hums.
  2. Record a 10-second test clip and listen with headphones.
  3. Get the phone as close to the audio source as possible.
  4. If outdoors, cup your hand gently around the bottom mic to block wind (do not cover it fully).
  5. Record 30 seconds of “silence” at your location (room tone) for use in editing.

If you have earbuds with a microphone, clip or tape them closer to your subject and use them as an improvised lav mic. Sync the audio in editing.

Pre-Shoot Checklist

Print this page separately. Run through it before every shoot.

Technical

  • Grid enabled
  • Resolution set to 1080p or 4K
  • Frame rate set (24 / 30 / 60fps)
  • Airplane mode ON
  • Do Not Disturb ON
  • Battery charged
  • Storage space available
  • Focus and exposure locked on subject

Composition

  • Phone held horizontally (landscape)
  • Subject positioned using rule of thirds
  • Eyes on top grid line for interview shots
  • Looking room given in direction subject faces
  • No distracting background elements

Lighting

  • Subject lit from front or side (not backlit)
  • Phone flash OFF
  • Even, soft light on subject’s face
  • Test shot reviewed for exposure

Audio

  • Location is quiet (no AC, traffic, crowds)
  • Phone close to audio source
  • Test recording checked with headphones
  • Room tone recorded (30 seconds of silence)

Stabilization

  • Two-handed grip, elbows tucked
  • Phone supported or body braced
  • Movement planned (ninja walk if mobile)
