Local Business Commercial Guide

1. Identify the Business You Will Promote

Definition

Choose a real local business and gather the essential information directly from an owner, manager, or employee.

What to do:

  • Pick a business willing to let you film
  • Interview someone who works there
  • Ask three questions:
    • "What single service or product do you want more people to know about?"
    • "Who is your ideal customer for that service?"
    • "What do you want viewers to do after watching the video?"
  • Write down their exact answers. These control your entire commercial.
Examples:
The bike shop wants more tune-ups, daily riders, and wants people to "bring the bike in today."
The café wants to promote their breakfast items, morning commuters, and wants viewers to "stop by on your way in."
The barbershop wants weekday walk-ins, working adults, and wants people to "come in today for a cut."
Choices:
If the business offers too many services, ask which one they want more customers for.
If they cannot define their ideal customer, ask who shows up most often.
If they struggle with a CTA, offer options until they pick one that sounds natural.

2. Write the Hook

Definition

A short, spoken line that instantly makes the viewer think, "This is about me."

What to do:

  • Write one simple sentence naming the viewer's real situation, problem, or need
  • Keep it under three seconds when spoken
  • Match it to the target customer defined in Step 1
Examples:
"If this keeps slowing you down, here's your fix."
"If you want this part of your day to go smoother, watch this."
"If you're tired of dealing with this, we can help."
Choices:
If customers feel frustrated → write a problem hook.
If customers want improvement → write a desire hook.
If the process looks interesting → write a curiosity hook.
If the result is visually strong → write an outcome hook.
If the business wants authority → write a bold claim hook.

3. Write the Experience Section

Definition

Explain what the customer goes through from start to finish.

What to do:

  • Write three to four short sentences describing the customer's actual journey inside this business
  • Write it in the same order a real customer experiences it
  • Make every sentence filmable
  • Use the employee's or owner's descriptions from your interview
Examples:
"You walk in and we look at what you need. We show you the best option right away. The process is quick. You leave with the problem handled."
"You come in, we take a look, we fix what's wrong, and you walk out ready for the day."
Choices:
Build the sequence as "Start → Middle → End."
or
Build it as "Arrive → We help → You leave better."

4. Write the Offer + Call to Action

Definition

State what the viewer gets and tell them exactly what to do.

What to do:

  • Write one sentence describing the offer the business wants to promote
  • Write one sentence giving the exact action the business wants viewers to take
Examples:
"Bring it in and we'll take care of it today. Come on in."
"Need it handled this week? Call us and we'll get you on the schedule."
"Stop by today and try it for yourself."
Choices:
If the business depends on walk-ins → "Come in today."
If they book appointments → "Call us and reserve your spot."
If customers bring items → "Bring it in and we'll fix it."

5. Turn the Script into a Shot Plan

Definition

Decide exactly what the viewer sees for each line of the script.

What to do:

  • Read the script line by line
  • Assign a visual that clearly shows that moment
  • Use the real business environment: entrance, counter, workspace, tools, results, customer reactions
Examples:
Line: "You walk in." → Shot: the front door opening.
Line: "We show you what needs to happen." → Shot: employee demonstrating something.
Line: "You leave ready for the day." → Shot: satisfied customer walking out.
Choices:
Use wide shots for establishing space.
Use medium shots to show interactions.
Use close shots for hands, tools, and important details.
Use reaction shots to confirm the result.

6. Shooting Order

Definition

Film in a sequence that keeps the production organized and prevents missing anything.

What to do:

  1. Film the hook first
  2. Film the CTA second
  3. Record the entire script as narration third
  4. Then film all B-roll in the same order the script is written
Examples:
Record: "If this keeps slowing you down, here's your fix."
Record: "Come in today and we'll take care of you."
Record narration: "You walk in. We look at what you need. You leave with it handled."
Film visuals: entrance → employee assessing → problem solved → customer leaving.

7. Edit the Commercial

Definition

Assemble the story so the viewer understands it instantly.

What to do:

  1. Put the narration on the timeline
  2. Place the hook shot over the first line
  3. Place the CTA shot over the last line
  4. Match every narration line to a corresponding visual
  5. Cut each shot down to the simplest version that communicates the idea

8. Create the Vertical Version

Definition

Reframe the finished video for phones.

What to do:

  • Duplicate the finished horizontal edit
  • Convert the sequence to vertical
  • Reframe each shot so important elements stay centered
Choices:
Shift upward to show faces.
Shift downward to show tools.
Use closer shots if wide ones crop poorly.
Adjust text placement so it stays within safe zones.

9. Final Check

Definition

Confirm the commercial works from the audience's perspective.

What to do:

  • Watch the first three seconds. The hook must instantly describe the viewer's situation.
  • Watch the middle. The experience must be clear and easy to follow visually.
  • Watch the ending. The action must be direct and unmistakable.
Choices:
If any section is weak, revise just that part.
Do not export until all three pass clearly.
