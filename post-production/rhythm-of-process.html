The Rhythm of Process

Purpose

This assignment invites you to analyze how editors use montage and rhythm to depict the mastery of a skill. Using Jiro Dreams of Sushi (2011) as our reference example, you will examine how time compression and repetitive cutting patterns allow the audience to "learn" the process alongside the master.

Throughline Example: Jiro Dreams of Sushi (2011), David Gelb

The Pulse

Process is boring. Watching a man massage an octopus for 45 minutes is not cinematic. But watching three seconds of massaging, cut to three seconds of slicing, cut to three seconds of pressing? That is a rhythm. That is a pulse. In Jiro Dreams of Sushi, the editing mimics the heartbeat of Jiro's routine.

The film uses a specific editing pattern to demonstrate Jiro's mastery: Repetition. We see the same shot of the fish market, the same shot of the rice, the same shot of the plate, over and over. But each time, the pace quickens or the focus tightens. The editor, Brandon Driscoll-Luttringer, aligns the cuts with the classical music score, turning the act of cooking into a dance.

The "Mastery" is not just in Jiro's hands; it is in the timeline. By removing the mistakes, the pauses, and the waiting, the editor constructs a version of reality where perfection is continuous. The montage manipulates time to convince us that Jiro is the process.

How it Works

  • The Ritual (The Beat): Establishing a baseline rhythm of the daily routine (Train, Market, Kitchen).
  • The Compression (The Montage): Compressing hours of labor (massaging the octopus) into a flurry of cuts to show effort without boredom.
  • The Flow (The Mastery): The final sequence of serving sushi, where the cuts are seamless, matching the fluid motion of the master.

Films to Choose From

Analyze the rhythm of process in a scene from one of these films:

  • Fast and Furious: Tokyo Drift, Justin Lin (The drift training montage)
  • Initial D, Andrew Lau (The cup of water drift technique)
  • Pale Flower, Masahiro Shinoda (The hanafuda gambling ritual)
  • Ramen Heads, Koki Shigeno (The soup preparation)

Prompts

Prompt 1: The Steps (The Ritual)

Goal: Deconstruct the mechanics of the skill.

Ask Yourself:

  • What specific steps does the editor choose to show? (e.g., Shifting gears, slicing fish)
  • What steps are skipped? Why?
  • How does the sound design emphasize the mechanical nature of the act?

Example: In Jiro, we see the knife slice, but we rarely see the cleaning of the knife. The editor focuses on the action, not the maintenance.

Prompt 2: The Compression (The Montage)

Goal: Analyze how time is manipulated.

Ask Yourself:

  • Does the scene feel faster or slower than real time?
  • How does the music drive the pace of the cuts?
  • Does the rhythm accelerate as the character gets better at the skill?

Example: In Tokyo Drift, the first drift attempts are choppy and chaotic. As Sean learns, the shots become longer and more fluid. The editing mirrors his competence.

Prompt 3: The Flow (The Mastery)

Goal: Explain how the edit sells the expertise.

Ask Yourself:

  • How does the final sequence prove the character is a master?
  • Is there a "flow state" where the editing becomes invisible?
  • Does the editor use slow motion to emphasize a specific detail of the technique?

Example: The final service in Jiro is presented almost without dialogue. The rhythm of the sushi being placed on the plate tells the whole story.

Textbook Contribution

If your analysis reveals the hidden rhythm of the scene, it belongs in the book.

  • The Technique: What skill was analyzed?
  • The Edit: How was it constructed?
  • The Takeaway: What does the rhythm tell us about the character?

Deliverables

  • Length: 500+ words. Focus on the timeline.
  • Formatting: MLA style. Note average shot length if possible.
  • Key Terms: Use the language of the course (montage, rhythm, compression, ellipsis).
  • Goal: Show us the clockwork.

Evaluation Criteria

  • Precision: Did you analyze the cuts, not just the action?
  • Connection: Did you link the editing speed to the narrative goal?
  • Evidence: Specific examples of shot progression.
  • Polish: Is it professional?
